The topics, explained simply

The programme’s goal and structure

The course is run by IIT Madras Pravartak with EMERITUS, taught mainly by Prof. C. Chandrasekhar (IIT Madras) and Prof. A. D. Dileep (IIT Dharwad), with some modules from EMERITUS subject-matter experts. The aim is to give you both the theory behind classical machine learning and modern deep learning, and the practical skill to implement these models on real problems.

Artificial intelligence (AI) means getting computers to act and reason in ways we’d call “intelligent.” Machine learning (ML) is the part of AI where a computer learns patterns from data instead of being hand-coded with rules. Deep learning (DL) is a powerful subset of ML built on multi-layered “neural networks.” AI is about 75 years old, but the last 10–15 years of deep-learning breakthroughs are why it’s everywhere now.

Why it matters: This week is the map. Everything later is a stop on a journey that goes math, then programming, then applications, then classical ML, then deep learning, then reinforcement learning.

Linear algebra background

Real-world data — text, images, audio — is turned into feature vectors: ordered lists of numbers that summarize a piece of data. (A “feature” is one measurable property; a “vector” is just a column of numbers.) Stack many feature vectors together and you get a matrix (a grid of numbers).

The key operations you’ll review: the inner product (also called dot product), which multiplies two vectors element-by-element and sums the result — it measures how aligned two vectors are. And matrix-vector multiplication, which transforms a vector into a new one (rotating, stretching, projecting it).

\[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \cdots + a_n b_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i \]

Two deeper tools: eigen analysis of a square matrix gives eigenvalues and eigenvectors — special directions that the matrix only stretches (doesn’t rotate), revealing the matrix’s core structure. For non-square (rectangular) matrices, the analogous tool is singular value decomposition (SVD), which breaks a matrix into simpler building blocks.

\[ \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} \quad (\mathbf{v} \text{ is an eigenvector, } \lambda \text{ is its eigenvalue}) \]

Why it matters: Nearly every ML method is, under the hood, operations on vectors and matrices. PCA, SVMs, and neural network layers all rely on these.

Calculus background

To “learn,” a model adjusts its internal parameters (the unknown numbers it tunes) to minimize an objective function (also called a loss function — a number measuring how wrong the model is). Finding the minimum requires derivatives, which tell you the slope: which way to nudge a parameter to reduce the loss.

You’ll go from single-variable differentiation, to multivariable, to differentiating with respect to whole vectors and matrices (because models have thousands or millions of parameters bundled together).

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\left( x^2 \right) = 2x \]

There are two flavors of optimization. Unconstrained optimization — no restrictions on the values. Constrained optimization — the variables must obey certain conditions, solved using the Lagrange multiplier method.

Why it matters: Training any model = solving an optimization problem. Gradient descent (the engine of deep learning) is calculus in action; SVMs use Lagrange multipliers directly.

Probability background

To capture the randomness and uncertainty in data, we treat features as random variables (quantities whose value isn’t fixed). They come in two kinds: discrete (countable values, like a dice roll) and continuous (any value in a range, like height).

For discrete variables we use a probability distribution; for continuous variables we use a probability density function (PDF). The single most important PDF is the Gaussian (also called the normal, or bell curve).

You’ll use the sum rule and product rule of probability to derive Bayes’ rule, which flips conditional probabilities — it lets you compute “probability of the cause given the evidence.”

\[ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)\, P(A)}{P(B)} \]

Why it matters: Bayes’ rule is the backbone of probabilistic classifiers (Bayes and naive Bayes), and the Gaussian density underlies Gaussian mixture models and much of statistics.

Programming fundamentals

You’ll implement everything in Python, the dominant language for ML. The core toolkit: NumPy (fast array/matrix math), SciPy (scientific computing), scikit-learn (classical ML algorithms ready-made), Matplotlib (plotting and visualization), PyTorch and TensorFlow (the two main deep-learning frameworks). You’ll also cover deployment/productionization — turning a trained model into a usable product — and advanced Python: generators, iterators, decorators, context managers, and performance optimization.

Why it matters: Theory is useless without execution. These tools are how ideas become working systems.

Exploratory data analysis (EDA)

Before modeling, you inspect the data. EDA covers: types of data and variables; central tendency (mean, median, mode — the “typical” value); dispersion (spread, like variance/standard deviation); the five-point summary (min, Q1, median, Q3, max) and its box plot picture; skewness (asymmetry of the distribution); and covariance/correlation (how two variables move together). You’ll also learn to encode categorical data into numbers, and to scale/normalize features so they share a comparable range.

Why it matters: Garbage in, garbage out. Good scaling and clean data often matter more than the choice of algorithm.

Application domains

The three natural human communication modes — text (NLP: classification, sentiment analysis, summarization, translation, question answering, generation), visual (image/video: classification, annotation, clustering, retrieval, captioning, image-to-image translation, visual question answering, segmentation, generation), and audio (speech recognition, speaker/language/emotion identification, diarization, transcription, synthesis, speech-to-speech translation; and music: raga/instrument identification, transcription, retrieval, generation). Beyond these: autonomous vehicles, space-mission planning, recommendation systems, game playing, education, banking/fraud detection, and healthcare.

Why it matters: These domains are the “why” — concrete problems the techniques are built to solve.

Machine learning paradigms

Supervised learning uses labeled data (each example comes with the correct answer) — the standard for classification and regression. Unsupervised learning finds structure in unlabeled data (e.g., clustering). In between: semi-supervised (some labels, lots of unlabeled), self-supervised (the model invents its own labels), active learning (the model asks for the most useful labels), transfer learning / domain adaptation (reuse a model trained elsewhere), zero/one/few-shot learning (learn from zero, one, or very few examples), and federated learning (train across many devices without centralizing data).

Why it matters: Real projects rarely have abundant clean labels; knowing these paradigms lets you pick the right strategy for the data you actually have.

Classical machine learning techniques

A guided list: linear regression (predict a number from a straight-line fit); overfitting (model memorizes training data but fails on new data) cured by regularization, specifically ridge regression (quadratic penalty); k-nearest neighbors (classify by looking at the closest examples — no training needed); Bayes / naive Bayes classifiers (probabilistic, using Bayes’ rule and Gaussian densities, fit via maximum likelihood); Gaussian mixture models (several Gaussians per class); support vector machines (find the hyperplane — a flat separating surface — with the widest margin between classes; hard/soft margin; kernel functions for curved boundaries); dimensionality reduction (PCA, Fisher discriminant analysis) to shrink feature count without losing much information; decision trees (for categorical features like color), combined into random forests; ensemble methods (bagging, boosting, AdaBoost) that combine many models; and clustering via k-means and hierarchical clustering.

Why it matters: These are interpretable, data-efficient workhorses — often the right first choice before reaching for deep learning.

Deep learning models

Starting from the perceptron (the first artificial neuron) and its learning rule, you move to richer neurons using activation functions (sigmoid, softmax, ReLU) inside multi-layer feed-forward networks, trained by backpropagation built on gradient descent and its variant stochastic gradient descent. Related: logistic regression for classification; optimizers (generalized delta rule, Adam = adaptive moments); dropout regularization; batch and layer normalization. Then the major families: CNNs (convolutional neural networks — 2D for images: LeNet, VGGNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet; U-Net for segmentation; YOLO for object detection; 3D for video; 1D for text/audio); RNNs (recurrent networks for sequences — LSTM, GRU, bidirectional, deep, trained by backpropagation through time); transformers built on the attention mechanism, trained via pre-training + fine-tuning (BERT, ViLBERT, GPT/ChatGPT); GANs (generative adversarial networks for creating data); and reinforcement learning (learning by reward, via Markov decision processes, Q-learning, deep Q-networks, actor-critic, with exploration vs exploitation strategies).

Why it matters: These are the engines behind today’s headline AI — image recognition, chatbots, image generation, game-playing agents.

Deep-dive practical example

Let’s trace a single concrete task — classifying a photo as “cat” or “dog” — through the whole pipeline this week previews, with specific numbers.

  1. Raw data to feature vector (linear algebra). Take a small \(4 \times 4\) grayscale image: 16 pixels, each a brightness value 0–255. Flatten it into a feature vector \(\mathbf{x}\) with 16 numbers, e.g. \(\mathbf{x} = [12, 200, 34, \ldots]^\top\). A batch of 1,000 such images becomes a \(1000 \times 16\) matrix:

\[ \mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} x_{1,1} & x_{1,2} & \cdots & x_{1,16} \\ x_{2,1} & x_{2,2} & \cdots & x_{2,16} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_{1000,1} & x_{1000,2} & \cdots & x_{1000,16} \end{bmatrix} \]

  1. Reduce dimensions (eigen analysis / PCA). Suppose many pixels are redundant. PCA computes the eigenvectors of the data’s covariance matrix and keeps, say, the top 5 directions — turning each 16-number vector into a 5-number vector. Less data, almost no information lost.

  2. Inspect first (EDA). You check the two classes are balanced (500 cats, 500 dogs), compute the mean brightness (central tendency) and standard deviation (dispersion), and scale features to a 0–1 range so no single pixel dominates.

  3. Pick a paradigm and model (supervised learning). You have labels (“cat”/“dog”), so this is supervised. Start simple with a Bayes classifier: model each class’s 5-dimensional features with a Gaussian density, fit its mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) by maximum likelihood on the 1,000 examples.

  4. Classify with Bayes’ rule. For a new image with feature vector \(\mathbf{x}\), compute

\[ P(\text{cat} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \frac{P(\mathbf{x} \mid \text{cat})\, P(\text{cat})}{P(\mathbf{x})} \]

If \(P(\mathbf{x} \mid \text{cat})\) from the cat-Gaussian is \(0.04\), \(P(\mathbf{x} \mid \text{dog})\) is \(0.01\), and the classes are equally likely, then cat wins (\(0.04 > 0.01\)), so the model predicts “cat.”

  1. Hit overfitting, then upgrade. Your simple model only gets 78% accuracy and overfits. You try a support vector machine with a kernel to draw a curved boundary (84%), then a 2D CNN (LeNet-style) that learns its own features straight from pixels via backpropagation and stochastic gradient descent, reaching 95% — using dropout to curb overfitting and Adam as the optimizer.

  2. Deploy. You save the trained CNN, wrap it in a small Python service (productionization), and now any uploaded photo returns “cat” or “dog” in milliseconds.

Every numbered step maps to a topic introduced this week: feature vectors and matrices (linear algebra), PCA (eigen analysis), EDA, supervised learning, Bayes rule and Gaussian density (probability), SVMs and CNNs (classical ML and deep learning), and deployment (programming fundamentals).

The topics, explained simply

Why linear algebra, and what a feature vector is

In machine learning we always work with data. From each piece of raw data we pull out a set of measurements called features. If we extract \(d\) features, we line them up into a single column of \(d\) numbers — that column is a feature vector, and it represents one data sample. A dataset is then \(n\) such samples.

A vector belongs to \(\mathbb{R}^d\), where \(\mathbb{R}\) means “real numbers” and \(d\) is how many numbers it holds. Geometrically a vector is a point in \(d\)-dimensional space, drawn as an arrow from the origin to that point. For example \(\mathbf{x} = (1, 2)\) is a point in 2D. By convention vectors are column vectors (standing up). Writing \(\mathbf{x}^\top\) (\(\mathbf{x}\) transpose) lays it on its side into a row vector.

Real examples: a person described by (height, weight) is a 2D vector; the Iris flower dataset uses 4 features per flower (4D); a 28x28 handwritten-digit image flattens to 784 pixel values (784D); the Wine Quality dataset has 12 features (12D). Every dot in a scatter plot is one such vector.

Why it matters: every input to every ML/DL model — an image, a sentence embedding, a customer record — is a vector. Linear algebra is simply the grammar for manipulating these.

Vectors as a special case of matrices

A matrix is a 2D grid of numbers with rows and columns; an \(n\)-by-\(d\) matrix has \(n\) rows and \(d\) columns. A vector is just the special case of a matrix with one column: a \(d\)-by-\(1\) matrix. So everything you learn about matrices applies to vectors too.

Why it matters: a whole dataset (\(n\) samples, \(d\) features each) is naturally one \(n\)-by-\(d\) matrix, so operations on the entire dataset become single matrix operations.

Basic vector operations: addition, subtraction, scalar multiplication

These three all work element-wise (act on matching positions).

Vector addition adds corresponding elements; both vectors must have the same length. \[ y_i = x_{m,i} + x_{n,i} \quad \text{for each } i \] Example: \((1,2) + (3,2) = (4,4)\). Geometrically the two vectors form a parallelogram and the result is its diagonal.

Vector subtraction subtracts corresponding elements. \[ y_i = x_{m,i} - x_{n,i} \quad \text{for each } i \] Example: \((1,2) - (3,2) = (-2,0)\).

Scalar multiplication multiplies every element by a single number \(a\). \[ y_i = a \cdot x_i \quad \text{for each } i \] Example: \(3 \cdot (1,2) = (3,6)\). This stretches or shrinks the vector without rotating it: \(a > 1\) lengthens, \(0 < a < 1\) shortens, and a negative \(a\) flips the direction.

Why it matters: gradient updates, weighted sums, and combining data points are all built from exactly these moves.

Dot product (inner product)

The dot product of two same-space vectors \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\), written \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle\), multiplies matching elements and sums the results into a single number (a scalar). You can think of it as the row vector \(\mathbf{x}^\top\) times the column vector \(\mathbf{y}\). \[ \mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{y} = \sum_i x_i \, y_i \] Example: \(\mathbf{x} = (1,2)\), \(\mathbf{y} = (3,2)\) gives \(1 \cdot 3 + 2 \cdot 2 = 3 + 4 = 7\).

The physical meaning: the dot product measures how aligned / how close two vectors are. Geometrically it equals the length of the projection of one vector onto the other. Large positive value means they point similarly; near zero means they are nearly perpendicular.

Why it matters: similarity between two data samples, the score a neuron computes (weights dotted with inputs), and attention scores in transformers are all dot products.

Norm of a vector

A norm measures the length (magnitude) of a vector. The most common is the L2 (Euclidean) norm, which is the square root of the dot product of a vector with itself. \[ \lVert \mathbf{x}\rVert = \sqrt{\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}} = \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_d^2} \] Example: \(\mathbf{x} = (3,4)\) gives \(\sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5\).

The general family is the Lp norm: \[ \lVert \mathbf{x}\rVert_p = \left( \sum_i |x_i|^p \right)^{1/p} \] - \(p = 2\): L2 / Euclidean (ordinary length) - \(p = 1\): L1, the sum of absolute values - \(p = \infty\): L-infinity, the largest absolute value. For \((7, -6, 3)\) it’s \(7\). - \(p = 0\): L0, the count of nonzero elements. For \((1, 0, 3, 0, 6)\) it’s \(3\).

A unit vector is a vector of length 1, made by dividing a vector by its norm. \[ \hat{\mathbf{x}} = \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\lVert \mathbf{x}\rVert} \] Example: \(\mathbf{x} = (3,4)\) has norm \(5\), so \(\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (3/5, 4/5)\).

Why it matters: norms are how we measure error sizes and add regularization. L2 penalties shrink weights smoothly; L1 penalties and the L0 count push models toward sparsity (mostly zeros), used in feature selection.

Similarity and dissimilarity measures

Euclidean distance is the L2 norm of the difference of two vectors — it tells you how far apart they are. \[ \lVert \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\rVert_2 = \sqrt{ \sum_i (x_i - y_i)^2 } \] Large distance = dissimilar; small distance = similar.

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors instead of their distance. \[ \cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{y}}{\lVert \mathbf{x}\rVert \, \lVert \mathbf{y}\rVert} \] If \(\theta = 0\) the vectors point the same way and \(\cos = 1\) (most similar); if \(\theta = 90\) degrees they are orthogonal and \(\cos = 0\).

Why it matters: clustering, nearest-neighbor search, and recommendation/retrieval systems rank items by exactly these two measures.

Outer product

The outer product is the reverse of the dot product: instead of row-times-column (giving a scalar), it’s column-times-row (giving a matrix). For \(\mathbf{x}\) (\(d\)-by-\(1\)) and \(\mathbf{y}\) (\(1\)-by-\(d\)), \(\mathbf{x}\mathbf{y}^\top\) is a \(d\)-by-\(d\) matrix. \[ M_{ij} = x_i \, y_j \]

Why it matters: covariance matrices are sums of outer products of mean-centered vectors, and eigen decomposition reconstructs a matrix from outer products of its eigenvectors.

Linear transformation (matrix times vector)

When a matrix \(\mathbf{M}\) (\(d\)-by-\(d\)) multiplies a vector \(\mathbf{x}\), you get a new vector \(\mathbf{y}\). Each output element is a weighted sum of all the input elements, using a row of \(\mathbf{M}\) as the weights. \[ \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}, \quad \text{where } y_i = M_{i1}x_1 + M_{i2}x_2 + \cdots + M_{id}x_d \] Because each \(\mathbf{y}\) element is a straight weighted sum of the \(\mathbf{x}\) elements (no powers, no products of inputs), the relationship is linear — hence “linear transformation.”

Why it matters: this single operation is the heart of ML. A neural network layer is exactly \(\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x}\) (plus a bias); rotations, projections (like PCA), and embeddings are all linear transformations.

The quadratic form \(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}\)

Sandwiching a square matrix \(\mathbf{M}\) between a row vector \(\mathbf{x}^\top\) and a column vector \(\mathbf{x}\) produces a single scalar. \[ \mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x} = m_{11}x_1^2 + (m_{12} + m_{21})x_1 x_2 + m_{22}x_2^2 \quad (d=2) \] This is a quadratic expression because it contains squared terms and cross-products of the \(\mathbf{x}\)’s.

Why it matters: quadratic forms appear in variance, in the equations of ellipses/Gaussians, and in optimization objectives — and their sign defines the special matrices below.

Trace, determinant, inverse, identity, pseudo-inverse

Trace of a square matrix is the sum of its diagonal elements. \[ \mathrm{Tr}(\mathbf{M}) = \sum_i M_{ii} \]

Determinant, written \(|\mathbf{M}|\) or \(\det(\mathbf{M})\), is a single scalar squeezed out of a square matrix that summarizes key properties (such as whether the matrix is invertible). Properties: - \(\det(\mathbf{M}) = \det(\mathbf{M}^\top)\) - \(\det(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{N}) = \det(\mathbf{M}) \cdot \det(\mathbf{N})\) (for square matrices of the same size)

Inverse of a square matrix \(\mathbf{M}\), written \(\mathbf{M}^{-1}\), is the matrix that undoes \(\mathbf{M}\). \[ \mathbf{M}\mathbf{M}^{-1} = \mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{I} \] Here \(\mathbf{I}\) is the identity matrix: 1’s on the diagonal, 0’s everywhere else.

Pseudo-inverse extends the idea to rectangular matrices, written \(\mathbf{M}^{+}\). \[ \mathbf{M}^{+} = (\mathbf{M}^\top\mathbf{M})^{-1}\mathbf{M}^\top \]

Why it matters: the pseudo-inverse is the closed-form solution for linear and polynomial regression. Determinant and trace become diagnostic tools in eigen analysis.

Basis, linear independence, and orthonormal basis

A set of \(d\) vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^d\) is a basis if (1) they are linearly independent — none can be built from a combination of the others — and (2) every vector in the space can be written as a weighted combination of them. The simplest basis in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is \((1,0)\) and \((0,1)\), but any two independent vectors work.

A basis is orthonormal when its vectors are mutually perpendicular (dot product 0 between different ones) and each has length 1. \[ \mathbf{q}_i^\top\mathbf{q}_j = 0 \;\text{ for } i \neq j, \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{q}_i^\top\mathbf{q}_i = 1 \]

Why it matters: this is the bridge to eigenvectors — for symmetric matrices the eigenvectors form an orthonormal basis, which is what makes the decompositions below clean and reversible.

Eigen analysis: eigenvalues and eigenvectors

For a square symmetric matrix there are special vectors \(\mathbf{q}\) that, when multiplied by \(\mathbf{M}\), come out pointing in the same direction — only their length changes. Such a vector is an eigenvector, and the scaling factor \(\lambda\) is its eigenvalue. \[ \mathbf{M}\mathbf{q} = \lambda\mathbf{q}, \quad \text{equivalently} \quad (\mathbf{M} - \lambda\mathbf{I})\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{0} \] For a \(d\)-by-\(d\) symmetric matrix there are \(d\) eigenvalues and \(d\) eigenvectors, and those eigenvectors are orthonormal.

Why it matters: eigenvectors reveal the natural axes of the data. PCA keeps the eigenvectors with the largest eigenvalues to compress data while losing the least information.

Eigen properties; positive (semi-)definite matrices

Two clean properties: Trace = sum of the eigenvalues; Determinant = product of the eigenvalues.

A symmetric matrix is positive definite if \(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x} > 0\) for every nonzero \(\mathbf{x}\); this happens exactly when all its eigenvalues are positive. It is positive semi-definite if \(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x} \geq 0\) (can be zero), which happens exactly when its eigenvalues are non-negative.

Why it matters: covariance matrices and kernel matrices are positive semi-definite; positive-definiteness guarantees a unique minimum in optimization problems.

Eigen decomposition

Any square symmetric matrix \(\mathbf{M}\) can be rebuilt entirely from its eigenvectors and eigenvalues. \[ \mathbf{M} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{U}^\top \] Here \(\mathbf{U}\) is the matrix whose columns are the (orthonormal) eigenvectors, and \(\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\) is a diagonal matrix of the eigenvalues. Equivalently \(\mathbf{M} = \sum_i \lambda_i \, (\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^\top)\).

Why it matters: decomposing a matrix into directions (eigenvectors) and importances (eigenvalues) underlies PCA, whitening, and many spectral methods.

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

SVD generalizes eigen decomposition to any matrix, including rectangular ones (\(m\) rows, \(n\) columns). \[ \mathbf{M} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top \] - \(\mathbf{U}\) holds the \(m\) left singular vectors (orthonormal basis). - \(\mathbf{V}\) holds the \(n\) right singular vectors (orthonormal basis). - \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) is a diagonal matrix of singular values. When the matrix is square, SVD reduces to eigen decomposition.

Why it matters: SVD powers dimensionality reduction, data compression, recommender systems, and shows up in deep learning topics like autoencoders.

Deep-dive practical example

Take two data points \(\mathbf{x} = (3, 4)\) and \(\mathbf{y} = (1, 1)\).

Step 1 — Norms: \(\lVert \mathbf{x}\rVert = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5\); \(\lVert \mathbf{y}\rVert = \sqrt{2} = 1.414\).

Step 2 — Dot product: \(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{y} = 3 \cdot 1 + 4 \cdot 1 = 7\).

Step 3 — Cosine similarity: \(\cos(\theta) = 7 / (5 \cdot 1.414) = 0.99\). Very close to 1, so \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\) point in almost the same direction.

Step 4 — Euclidean distance: \(\lVert \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\rVert = \sqrt{(3-1)^2 + (4-1)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 9} = \sqrt{13} = 3.61\).

Now the eigen analysis, using \(\mathbf{M} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2\\ 2 & 1\end{bmatrix}\) (symmetric).

Step 5 — Show \(\mathbf{q} = (1, 1)\) is an eigenvector: \(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{q} = (1 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 1, \; 2 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1) = (3, 3) = 3 \cdot (1, 1)\). So \((1,1)\) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda = 3\).

Step 6 — Find the other eigenvalue from \(\det(\mathbf{M} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = 0\): \((1 - \lambda)^2 - 4 = 0\), so \(1 - \lambda = +2\) or \(-2\), giving \(\lambda = -1\) or \(\lambda = 3\).

Step 7 — Verify properties: Trace \(= 1 + 1 = 2 = (-1) + 3\). Determinant \(= (1)(1) - (2)(2) = -3 = (-1)(3)\). Both match.

Step 8 — Definiteness: one eigenvalue is negative (\(-1\)), so \(\mathbf{M}\) is not positive (semi-)definite. Indeed for \(\mathbf{x} = (1, -1)\): \(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x} = 1 - 2 - 2 + 1 = -2 < 0\).

This single example exercised norms, the dot product, cosine similarity, Euclidean distance, the characteristic equation, eigenvalues/eigenvectors, and the trace/determinant checks — the spine of the whole week.

Key takeaways

  • All data in ML/DL is represented as feature vectors (points in \(\mathbb{R}^d\)), and a vector is just a one-column matrix.
  • The dot product turns two vectors into one number that measures alignment/similarity; the norm turns one vector into its length.
  • Norms come in a family (L2 length, L1 sum, L-infinity max, L0 nonzero count); L1/L0 drive sparsity for feature selection.
  • Similarity is measured two ways: Euclidean distance (small = similar) and cosine similarity (1 = same direction, 0 = orthogonal).
  • The outer product builds a matrix from two vectors; \(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}\) is a linear transformation and is the core operation inside every model layer.
  • The quadratic form \(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}\) is a scalar; its sign defines positive definite (all eigenvalues \(> 0\)) and positive semi-definite (eigenvalues \(\geq 0\)) matrices.
  • Eigenvectors keep their direction under a symmetric matrix; eigenvalues are the scale factors. Trace = sum of eigenvalues, determinant = product of eigenvalues.
  • Eigen decomposition (\(\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{U}^\top\)) rebuilds a square symmetric matrix; SVD (\(\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top\)) generalizes this to any rectangular matrix.
  • These exact tools reappear in regression (pseudo-inverse), PCA (eigen/SVD), optimization (definiteness), and deep learning (autoencoders, SVD).

Week 3: Calculus — Differentiation and Optimization

In one line: This week teaches how to differentiate functions (single-variable, vector-input, and vector-valued) and how to use those derivatives to find the minimum or maximum of a function — the mathematical engine behind nearly every machine learning training algorithm.

What this week covers

  • Differentiation of single-variable functions (what a derivative is, and why ML needs it)
  • The differentiation rules: constant, power, sum, product, quotient, and chain
  • The gradient as the slope of the tangent, and the meaning of gradient = 0
  • The second derivative (second-order derivative) and what its sign tells you
  • Function of a vector: partial derivatives and the gradient vector
  • Useful vector derivatives: dot product, squared norm, and quadratic forms
  • Vector-valued functions and the Jacobian matrix
  • Unconstrained optimization via the gradient method (set gradient = 0, check 2nd derivative)
  • Constrained optimization and the Lagrange (Lagrangian) multiplier method

The topics, explained simply

Differentiation of a single-variable function

Differentiation is the process of finding the derivative of a function. The derivative tells you the rate of change of the function with respect to its variable — i.e., how fast the output moves when you nudge the input. If \(x\) is a single (scalar) variable and \(f(x)\) is a function of it, the derivative is written \(\frac{df}{dx}\), also shortened to \(f'(x)\).

\[\text{derivative of } f(x) \text{ with respect to } x = \frac{df}{dx} = f'(x)\]

Why it matters: Almost all (the lecturer says about 90–99%) machine learning and deep learning algorithms learn their parameters by optimizing a cost function — minimizing error or maximizing some score. Differentiation is the key tool that makes that optimization possible.

The differentiation rules

You differentiate complicated functions by combining a few simple rules, term by term.

\[\text{Constant rule — derivative of any constant} = 0 \quad \left(\text{e.g., } \frac{d}{dx} 5 = 0\right)\] \[\text{Power rule} \quad \frac{d}{dx} x^n = n \, x^{n-1} \quad \left(\text{e.g., } \frac{d}{dx} 2x^2 = 4x; \ \frac{d}{dx} x^{-1} = -\frac{1}{x^2}\right)\] \[\text{Sum rule} \quad \frac{d}{dx}\big(f(x) + g(x)\big) = \frac{df}{dx} + \frac{dg}{dx}\] \[\text{Product rule} \quad \frac{d}{dx}\big(f(x) \, g(x)\big) = f(x)\,\frac{dg}{dx} + g(x)\,\frac{df}{dx}\] \[\text{Quotient rule} \quad \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right) = \frac{g(x)\,\frac{df}{dx} - f(x)\,\frac{dg}{dx}}{\big(g(x)\big)^2}\] \[\text{Chain rule (function inside a function, } f(g(x))\text{)} \quad \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \, g'(x)\]

Two chain-rule facts that show up constantly in ML: \(\frac{d}{dx}\log(x) = \frac{1}{x}\), and \(\frac{d}{dx}\exp(x) = \exp(x)\). Example: \(\frac{d}{dx}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2}\right) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2}\right) \cdot (-x)\).

Why it matters: Loss functions in ML are built from logs, exponentials, squares, and sums (think log-likelihoods and Gaussian distributions). These rules are exactly what you use to differentiate them.

The gradient and the meaning of gradient = 0

Geometrically, the derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that point. This slope is also called the gradient of the curve at \(x\).

Take \(f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 2\). Its gradient is \(2x - 4\). At \(x = 10\) the slope is \(16\) (positive); at \(x = -10\) the slope is \(-24\) (negative); at \(x = 2\) the slope is \(0\). The point where the gradient is \(0\) (here \(x = 2\)) is exactly where the parabola bottoms out — its minimum. For an upside-down parabola the gradient-0 point is the maximum instead.

\[\text{where } \frac{df}{dx} = 0, \text{ the function has either a minimum or a maximum (an ``optimum'')}\]

Why it matters: This single observation — optimum happens where the gradient is zero — is the backbone of optimization, and therefore of training ML models.

The second derivative

If you differentiate the derivative again you get the second-order derivative, written \(\frac{d^2 f}{dx^2}\) or \(f''(x)\). It measures how the slope itself is changing (the curvature). Example: for \(f(x) = x^2 - 6x + 3\), the first derivative is \(2x - 6\), and the second derivative is the constant \(2\).

Why it matters: The gradient being 0 tells you that you found an optimum, but not whether it’s a peak or a valley. The sign of the second derivative settles it: positive means a minimum, negative means a maximum.

Function of a vector: partial derivatives and the gradient vector

If \(\mathbf{x}\) is a vector of \(d\) variables \((x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_d)\) and a single function \(f\) produces one scalar output \(y = f(\mathbf{x})\), then to differentiate you take a partial derivative with respect to each variable separately. “Partial” means you differentiate with respect to one variable while treating the others as constants.

Collecting all the partials into one vector gives the gradient vector. Each entry is the slope in the direction of one variable. \[\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_d} \end{bmatrix}\]

Several standard vector derivatives come up repeatedly: \[\frac{\partial (\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{y})}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{y}\] \[\frac{\partial (\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{x})}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = 2\mathbf{x}\] \[\frac{\partial (\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{M} \mathbf{x})}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = (\mathbf{M} + \mathbf{M}^\top)\,\mathbf{x} \text{ in general, and } = 2\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x} \text{ when } \mathbf{M} \text{ is symmetric}\]

Why it matters: Differentiating a function of one input gives a scalar; differentiating a function of a vector gives a vector (the gradient vector). This is precisely what gradient-based training uses to know which way to adjust each parameter.

Vector-valued functions and the Jacobian

Apply \(k\) different functions to the same input vector \(\mathbf{x}\), producing \(k\) outputs. That bundle of functions is a vector-valued function (written in bold \(\mathbf{f}\)). A neural network is the classic example.

Differentiating a vector-valued function gives a matrix, called the Jacobian matrix. You compute the gradient vector of each function, then stack them as rows (transposed). With \(k\) functions and \(d\) input variables, the Jacobian is a \(k \times d\) matrix. When \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}\) (a linear transformation), the Jacobian is just \(\mathbf{M}\) itself.

\[\mathbf{J} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_d} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial x_d} \end{bmatrix}\]

Why it matters: The pattern is — derivative of a scalar function = scalar; derivative of a function of a vector = vector (gradient); derivative of a vector of functions = matrix (Jacobian). Backpropagation is built on chaining Jacobians together.

Unconstrained optimization

Optimization means finding the input value \(x^*\) that makes a function as small or as large as possible. Unconstrained means there are no restrictions on \(x\). The most common technique is the gradient method: set the gradient (first derivative) equal to 0 and solve for \(x\), then use the second derivative to classify the result. \[\text{solve } \frac{df}{dx} = 0 \text{ for } x^*; \text{ if } f''(x^*) > 0 \text{ then } x^* \text{ is a minimum, if } f''(x^*) < 0 \text{ then } x^* \text{ is a maximum}\] For a function of many variables, you set each partial derivative to 0 (the whole gradient vector equals the zero vector).

Why it matters: This is literally how a model’s parameters are chosen. The function being minimized is called the objective function — a cost, error, criterion, or likelihood function.

Constrained optimization and Lagrange multipliers

Sometimes the input must satisfy constraints: equality constraints (\(g(\mathbf{x}) = 0\)) and inequality constraints (\(h(\mathbf{x}) > 0\) or \(p(\mathbf{x}) < 0\)). The standard tool is the Lagrangian (Lagrange) multiplier method, which converts a constrained problem into an unconstrained one. Each constraint becomes a “secondary objective” multiplied by its own coefficient (a Lagrange multiplier) and folded into the original (“primal”) objective. \[L = f(\mathbf{x}) - \alpha\,g(\mathbf{x}) - \beta\,h(\mathbf{x}) + \gamma\,p(\mathbf{x})\]

The recipe: (1) write the primal objective with constraints; (2) build the Lagrangian; (3) set \(\frac{dL}{dx} = 0\) to get a condition for optimality; (4) substitute back to get the dual objective (a function only of the multipliers); (5) optimize the dual to find the best multipliers; (6) plug back in to get \(x^*\). Note: if the primal is a minimization, the dual is a maximization.

Why it matters: Many ML methods (notably support vector machines) are constrained optimization problems solved exactly this primal-dual way.

Deep-dive practical example

Problem: Minimize \(f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 3\).

Step 1 — Take the first derivative (the gradient): \(\frac{df}{dx} = 2x - 4\).

Step 2 — Set the gradient to 0 and solve: \(2x - 4 = 0\), so \(x = 2\). Candidate optimum \(x^* = 2\).

Step 3 — Check the second derivative: \(\frac{d}{dx}(2x - 4) = 2\), which is positive, so \(x^* = 2\) is a minimum.

Step 4 — Find the minimum value: \(f(2) = 4 - 8 + 3 = -1\).

Conclusion: \(f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 3\) reaches its smallest value, \(-1\), at \(x = 2\).

Mirror case (maximization): For \(g(x) = -x^2 - 4x + 2\), the gradient is \(-2x - 4\); setting it to 0 gives \(x = -2\); the second derivative is \(-2\) (negative), so \(x = -2\) is a maximum.

Constrained twist: Minimize \(f(x) = \frac{1}{2} x^2\) subject to \(-2x - 3 < 0\). Without the constraint, the minimum is at \(x = 0\). But the constraint is satisfied only for \(x\) greater than \(-1.5\) and equals 0 exactly at \(x = -1.5\). So the best feasible point is \(x = -1.5\). The Lagrangian method gives \(x^* = b/a\) with \(b = 3\) and \(a = -2\), i.e. \(x^* = -1.5\). The constraint pushes the solution away from the unconstrained minimum (0) to the edge of the feasible region (\(-1.5\)).

Key takeaways

  • The derivative \(\frac{df}{dx}\) measures rate of change and equals the slope of the tangent (the gradient) at a point.
  • Master six rules — constant, power, sum, product, quotient, chain — and you can differentiate almost any ML loss function; log and exp derivatives (\(\frac{1}{x}\) and \(\exp(x)\)) are especially common.
  • Gradient = 0 marks an optimum; the sign of the second derivative tells you which kind: positive = minimum, negative = maximum.
  • Differentiation scales by shape: scalar function gives a scalar derivative; function of a vector gives a gradient vector; vector of functions gives a Jacobian matrix (\(k\) by \(d\)).
  • Handy vector derivatives: \(\frac{d(\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{y})}{d\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{y}\), \(\frac{d(\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{x})}{d\mathbf{x}} = 2\mathbf{x}\), \(\frac{d(\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{M} \mathbf{x})}{d\mathbf{x}} = 2\mathbf{M}\mathbf{x}\) for symmetric \(\mathbf{M}\).
  • Unconstrained optimization = set gradient to 0 and solve; for many variables, set every partial derivative to 0.
  • Constrained optimization uses Lagrange multipliers to turn constraints into an unconstrained Lagrangian, then solves via the primal-to-dual chain.
  • All of this exists because ML/DL training is optimization: minimizing a cost/error/likelihood objective function by following gradients.

Week 4: Probability Theory

In one line: Probability theory is the framework machine learning uses to quantify and manipulate uncertainty, built up from random variables, the rules that combine their probabilities (sum, product, Bayes), expectation-based summaries (mean, variance, covariance), and the all-important Gaussian/Normal density.

What this week covers

  • Why probability matters for ML/DL: uncertainty, noise, data variability, finite data, intra-class variability and inter-class similarity
  • Random variables as functions (mappings from sample space to range space)
  • Discrete vs continuous random variables
  • Probability of an event, and relating events to random variables
  • Joint probability and the joint probability distribution
  • Marginal probability and marginalization; the sum rule
  • Conditional probability and the product rule
  • Bayes rule
  • Statistical independence
  • Probability mass functions (discrete) vs probability density functions (continuous)
  • Expectation operations: mean (first moment), variance (second moment), standard deviation
  • Covariance, covariance matrix (for random vectors), sample mean and sample covariance
  • Normal/Gaussian density: univariate and multivariate, the 68-95-99.7 rule, and the Mahalanobis distance

The topics, explained simply

Why probability is central to machine learning

Real-world data is uncertain. The same object class looks different across images (different pose, background, lighting). This is called intra-class variability: lots of variation within one class. At the same time, different classes can look alike, which is inter-class similarity. Both create confusion about which class a sample belongs to.

Because of this, ML systems rarely say “this is definitely class A.” Instead they attach a number: “this image is a tiger with probability 0.8.” A face-recognition or fingerprint system says a person is identified “with some confidence,” and a threshold on that confidence decides the final answer.

Uncertainty comes from three sources the slides name explicitly: noise in the measurement, variability in the data, and the finite size of datasets. Probability theory gives a consistent framework to quantify and manipulate this uncertainty, which is why it is a central foundation for machine learning and deep learning.

Why it matters: Almost every ML model outputs or reasons about probabilities. Without this framework you cannot describe how confident a prediction is or how to combine evidence.

Random variables (as functions, not variables)

The most common misconception is that a “random variable” is a variable holding a value. It is actually a function. It maps the sample space (the space of all your data) to a range space (the set of values it can output).

\[X : S \to S_X\]

(the random variable \(X\) maps sample space \(S\) to range space \(S_X\))

A random variable captures one specific property operating on the data. For example, with a sample space of objects, the random variable $X = $ “color of the object” maps each object to a value in \(\{\text{Red}, \text{Green}, \text{Blue}\}\). It can take one of several possible values, and each value has some probability attached.

A key link to ML: each feature or attribute computed from your data corresponds to one random variable. So a data sample with \(d\) features is a collection of \(d\) random variables.

Why it matters: Thinking of every feature/column as a random variable (a function of the data) is what lets you apply all the probability machinery below to real datasets.

Discrete vs continuous random variables

A discrete random variable takes a finite set of distinct values. It is written with an uppercase letter (\(X\)). Examples from the lecture: - Coin toss: the indicator \(I_H = 1\) if the toss is Head, else \(0\) (and \(I_T\) for Tail). These take only \(\{0, 1\}\). - Human data: \(I_A = 1\) if the person is an adult (above 18), else \(0\); \(I_C = 1\) if a child. - Objects in a box: $X = $ color taking \(\{R, G, B\}\); $Y = $ shape taking \(\{\text{Sphere}, \text{Cube}\}\).

A discrete variable does not have to be just 0/1; it can take \(n\) distinct values, but they are countable and separate.

A continuous random variable takes any real value from a continuous range. It is written with a lowercase letter (\(x\)). Examples: - Height of a person, range about 90.0 cm to 210.0 cm. - Weight of a person, range about 20 kg to 120 kg. - Iris flower: sepal length (4.0 to 7.5 cm), sepal width (2.0 to 4.0 cm), petal length (1.0 to 5.5 cm), petal width (0.1 to 2.5 cm).

For a continuous variable it is not meaningful to ask the probability of one exact value, because there are infinitely many possible values; we work with ranges and densities instead (see below).

Why it matters: The type decides your math. Discrete uses sums and a probability mass function; continuous uses integrals and a probability density function.

Probability of an event

An event \(A\) is something happening in the sample space. Its probability is estimated by experiments. If you run \(n_s\) total experiments (which in ML means \(n_s\) total data samples) and the event occurs \(n_a\) times, then:

\[P(A) \approx \frac{n_a}{n_s}\]

The “\(\approx\)” (approximately equal) is deliberate: with a finite number of samples this is an estimate. As \(n_s\) approaches infinity, \(n_a/n_s\) converges to the true probability.

Two basic properties: every probability lies between 0 and 1, and if \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive (cannot happen together) and cover all outcomes, then \(P(A) + P(B) = 1\), so \(P(B) = 1 - P(A)\). More generally, for \(m\) mutually exclusive events \(A_1 \ldots A_m\), their probabilities sum to 1.

The key bridge to random variables: an event is “a random variable taking a particular value.” Example: 50 coin tosses, head occurs 20 times, so \(P(I_H = 1) = 20/50 = 0.4\), and \(P(\text{tail}) = 1 - 0.4 = 0.6\). Example: 100 balls (10 red, 35 green, 55 blue); with $X = $ color, \(P(X = \text{Red}) = 10/100 = 0.1\), \(P(X = \text{Green}) = 0.35\), \(P(X = \text{Blue}) = 0.55\), and these sum to 1.

Why it matters: This turns vague “events” into concrete statements about feature values, which is exactly what classifiers reason about.

Joint probability (and the joint distribution)

The joint probability is the probability of two events happening together. If \(N_{AB}\) is the number of times events \(A\) and \(B\) occur jointly out of \(n_s\) samples:

\[P(A, B) = \frac{N_{AB}}{n_s}\]

Written with random variables: \(P(X = x_i, Y = y_j) = \frac{N_{ij}}{n_s}\), where \(N_{ij}\) counts how often \(X = x_i\) and \(Y = y_j\) happen at the same time.

The collection of joint probabilities over all combinations of values is the joint probability distribution, written \(P(X, Y)\). Note: \(P(X, Y)\) is the whole distribution (all combinations); \(P(X = x_i, Y = y_j)\) is one specific value. All joint probabilities over every combination sum to 1.

Why it matters: Joint probabilities describe how two features co-occur, the raw material for everything else (marginals, conditionals, independence).

Marginal probability, marginalization, and the sum rule

The marginal probability is the probability of one random variable taking a value, ignoring the other. You get it by summing the joint probability over all values of the other variable. This operation is called marginalization.

\[P(X = x_i) = \sum_j P(X = x_i, Y = y_j)\]

The collection of marginals over all values is the marginal distribution \(P(X)\). Stating it as the sum rule:

\[P(X) = \sum_y P(X, Y)\]

\[P(Y) = \sum_x P(X, Y)\]

For continuous variables the sum becomes an integral:

\[p(x) = \int p(x, y)\, dy\]

Why it matters: Marginalization lets you “integrate out” variables you do not care about, recovering the distribution of just the variable of interest.

Conditional probability and the product rule

The conditional probability \(P(X = x_i \mid Y = y_j)\) is the probability of \(X\) taking a value given that \(Y\) has taken a specific value. It is the joint probability divided by the marginal of the conditioning variable:

\[P(X = x_i \mid Y = y_j) = \frac{P(X = x_i, Y = y_j)}{P(Y = y_j)}\]

In counts this simplifies (the \(n_s\) cancels): \(P(X = x_i \mid Y = y_j) = \frac{N_{ij}}{\sum_i N_{ij}}\).

Rearranging gives the product rule: the joint equals a conditional times a marginal, and there are two equivalent forms:

\[P(X, Y) = P(X \mid Y)\, P(Y)\]

\[P(X, Y) = P(Y \mid X)\, P(X)\]

For continuous variables the same forms hold with densities: \(p(x, y) = p(x \mid y)\, p(y) = p(y \mid x)\, p(x)\).

Why it matters: Conditioning is how models update belief once they observe something; the product rule is the algebra that connects joint, conditional, and marginal probabilities.

Bayes rule

Because both product-rule forms equal the same joint probability, you can set them equal: \(P(Y \mid X)\, P(X) = P(X \mid Y)\, P(Y)\). Solving for \(P(Y \mid X)\):

\[P(Y \mid X) = \frac{P(X \mid Y)\, P(Y)}{P(X)}\]

where the denominator \(P(X)\) is itself a marginal: \(P(X) = \sum_y P(X, Y)\) (an integral for continuous variables).

In classification we let \(X\) be the data sample (feature vector) and \(Y\) be the class label. Bayes rule then computes \(P(\text{class} \mid \text{sample})\): the probability of a class given the observed features. This is the heart of the Bayes classifier covered later in the course; in practice \(X\) becomes a vector of random variables (a feature vector).

Why it matters: Bayes rule flips “probability of data given class” into “probability of class given data,” which is exactly what a classifier needs.

Statistical independence

Two random variables are independent when their joint distribution factorizes into the product of their marginals:

\[P(X, Y) = P(X)\, P(Y)\]

Equivalently, from the product rule this happens only when \(P(X \mid Y) = P(X)\): knowing \(Y\) tells you nothing about \(X\). Whatever value \(Y\) takes, the probability of \(X\) is unchanged. The same definition holds for continuous variables with densities: \(p(x, y) = p(x)\, p(y)\).

Why it matters: Independence assumptions massively simplify models (for example, “naive” Bayes assumes features are independent given the class), making otherwise huge joint distributions tractable.

Probability mass vs probability density functions

For a discrete random variable, the distribution \(P(X)\) is a probability mass function (PMF): it assigns actual probability to each discrete value.

For a continuous random variable, you cannot assign nonzero probability to a single exact value, so \(p(x)\) is a probability density function (PDF). The density does not give probability directly; you get probability by integrating it over a range:

\[P(a \le x \le b) = \int_a^b p(x)\, dx\]

A PDF always has a closed-form mathematical expression (the Gaussian below is the most common one). Note the convention: capital \(P\) for discrete distributions, lowercase \(p\) for continuous densities.

Why it matters: Almost all continuous-feature modeling in ML works with densities, so you compute probabilities of intervals (areas under the curve), not of points.

Expectation: mean, variance, standard deviation

Expectation, written \(\mathbb{E}[\cdot]\), is the central operation for summarizing a random variable. For a discrete variable the mean (denoted \(\mu\)) is the probability-weighted sum of its values:

\[\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu = \sum_x x \cdot P(X = x)\]

Example: \(X\) takes 5, 10, 15 with probabilities 0.4, 0.25, 0.35; \(\mathbb{E}[X] = 0.4 \cdot 5 + 0.25 \cdot 10 + 0.35 \cdot 15 = 9.75\). If all values are equally likely (probability \(1/n_x\) each), the mean reduces to the ordinary arithmetic average. The mean is the first-order statistical moment.

For a continuous variable the sum becomes an integral:

\[\mathbb{E}[x] = \mu = \int x \cdot p(x)\, dx\]

The variance (\(\sigma^2\)) measures spread: the expected squared deviation from the mean. It is the second-order statistical moment.

\[\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)^2] = \sum_x (x - \mu)^2 \cdot P(X = x)\]

\[\sigma^2 = \int (x - \mu)^2 \cdot p(x)\, dx\]

Squaring makes every deviation positive, so variance measures magnitude of spread regardless of direction. The standard deviation is the square root of the variance:

\[\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}\]

Why it matters: Mean and variance are the two numbers that summarize “where the data sits” and “how spread out it is,” and they appear directly inside the Gaussian density.

Covariance and the covariance matrix

Covariance needs two variables and measures how they vary together. For \(X\) and \(Y\) with means \(\mu_X\) and \(\mu_Y\):

\[\sigma_{XY} = \mathrm{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)]\]

\[\sigma_{XY} = \int\!\!\int (x - \mu_X)(y - \mu_Y)\, p(x, y)\, dx\, dy\]

When you have many features, stack \(d\) random variables into a random vector. Its mean vector is the vector of individual means, and its covariance matrix (\(\Sigma\)) holds the covariance between every pair of variables:

\[\Sigma = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)(X - \mu)^\top] \quad (\text{a } d \times d \text{ matrix})\]

The diagonal entries are the variances (covariance of a variable with itself), and the off-diagonal entries are the covariances. The covariance matrix is symmetric, because \(\mathrm{Cov}(i, j) = \mathrm{Cov}(j, i)\), so \(\sigma_{12} = \sigma_{21}\).

With a finite dataset of \(n\) points (each a \(d\)-dimensional vector), integration becomes summation, giving the sample mean and sample covariance:

\[\mu = \frac{1}{n} \sum_n x_n\]

\[\Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_n (x_n - \mu)(x_n - \mu)^\top\]

Why it matters: The covariance matrix captures how all features move together; it shapes the multivariate Gaussian and underlies methods like PCA and Gaussian classifiers.

Normal / Gaussian density

The Gaussian (Normal) density is the most common PDF for continuous variables and appears throughout statistical pattern recognition. It is unimodal (one single peak) and that peak sits at the mean. “Univariate” means defined on a single variable (do not confuse unimodal = one peak with univariate = one variable).

The univariate Gaussian with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\):

\[p(x) = \mathcal{N}(x\mid\mu,\sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\]

Its shape is a bell curve. The standard deviation \(\sigma\) controls the width: small \(\sigma\) gives a tall, narrow peak; large \(\sigma\) gives a short, wide spread. At \(x = \mu\) the exponent is 0 and \(\exp(0) = 1\), so the peak height is exactly \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}\) (its maximum). As \(x\) moves away from \(\mu\) in either direction, \(p(x)\) shrinks.

The total area under the curve is always 1, no matter the spread:

\[\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} p(x)\, dx = 1\]

This is why it is also called the normal/standard distribution. The well-known coverage (the 68-95-99.7 rule): about 68% of the values lie within \(\mu \pm \sigma\), about 95% within \(\mu \pm 2\sigma\), and about 99.7% within \(\mu \pm 3\sigma\).

For \(d\) variables stacked into a random vector, the multivariate Gaussian uses the mean vector \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and covariance matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\):

\[p(\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}\mid\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}\,\sqrt{\det(\boldsymbol{\Sigma})}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)\]

The term \((\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\) is the (squared) Mahalanobis distance, named after the Indian statistician Prasanta Chandra Mahalanobis (founder of the Indian Statistical Institute, Kolkata). It measures the distance from a point \(\mathbf{x}\) to a whole distribution (a set of points summarized by \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\)), accounting for spread and correlation, unlike plain straight-line distance. In the bivariate (2-variable) case the density is a bell-shaped surface whose horizontal cross-sections are elliptical constant-density contours centered at the mean.

Why it matters: The Gaussian is the default model for continuous features; its parameters are exactly the mean and covariance from the expectation operations, and the Mahalanobis distance inside it is what Gaussian classifiers use to score how well a point fits a class.

Deep-dive practical example

Setup: colored shapes (joint probability table). A box of 100 objects is described by two discrete random variables: $X = $ color (R, G, B) and $Y = $ shape (S = sphere, C = cube). The counts \(N_{ij}\) are:

count \(N_{ij}\) Y = Sphere (S) Y = Cube (C) row total
X = Red (R) 18 16 34
X = Green (G) 12 20 32
X = Blue (B) 30 4 34
column total 60 40 100

Step 1 - Joint probabilities (divide each cell by \(n_s = 100\)): - \(P(X=R, Y=S) = 18/100 = 0.18\) - \(P(X=R, Y=C) = 16/100 = 0.16\) - \(P(X=G, Y=S) = 12/100 = 0.12\) - \(P(X=G, Y=C) = 20/100 = 0.20\) - \(P(X=B, Y=S) = 30/100 = 0.30\) - \(P(X=B, Y=C) = 4/100 = 0.04\)

Check: \(0.18 + 0.16 + 0.12 + 0.20 + 0.30 + 0.04 = 1.00\). Good, the joint distribution sums to 1.

Step 2 - Marginal probabilities of color (sum rule: add across the shape values): - \(P(X=R) = (18 + 16)/100 = 34/100 = 0.34\) - \(P(X=G) = (12 + 20)/100 = 32/100 = 0.32\) - \(P(X=B) = (30 + 4)/100 = 34/100 = 0.34\)

These sum to 1.00. Marginals of shape: \(P(Y=S) = 60/100 = 0.60\), \(P(Y=C) = 40/100 = 0.40\).

Step 3 - Conditional probabilities (condition on shape = sphere; divide the sphere column by its total 60): - \(P(X=R \mid Y=S) = 18 / (18 + 12 + 30) = 18/60 = 0.30\) - \(P(X=G \mid Y=S) = 12/60 = 0.20\) - \(P(X=B \mid Y=S) = 30/60 = 0.50\)

These sum to 1.00, as a conditional distribution should.

Step 4 - Check independence. If color and shape were independent we would need \(P(X, Y) = P(X)\, P(Y)\) for every cell. Test the red-sphere cell: - Observed joint: \(P(X=R, Y=S) = 0.18\) - Product of marginals: \(P(X=R) \cdot P(Y=S) = 0.34 \cdot 0.60 = 0.204\)

Since 0.18 is not equal to 0.204, color and shape are NOT independent. (Another way to see it: \(P(X=R \mid Y=S) = 0.30\), but the marginal \(P(X=R) = 0.34\); knowing the shape changes the probability of the color, so they are dependent.)

Step 5 - A small Bayes check (optional sanity). Bayes rule says \(P(Y=S \mid X=R) = \frac{P(X=R \mid Y=S)\, P(Y=S)}{P(X=R)} = \frac{0.30 \cdot 0.60}{0.34} = \frac{0.18}{0.34} = 0.529\). Direct from the table: \(P(Y=S \mid X=R) = 18/34 = 0.529\). They agree.

Step 6 - A small Gaussian evaluation. Suppose adult heights follow a univariate Gaussian with \(\mu = 170\) cm and \(\sigma = 10\) cm. Evaluate the density at \(x = 180\) cm: - Standardized deviation: \((x - \mu) / \sigma = (180 - 170)/10 = 1\), so \((x - \mu)^2 / (2\sigma^2) = 100 / 200 = 0.5\) - \(\exp(-0.5) = 0.6065\) - Front factor: \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma} = \frac{1}{2.5066 \cdot 10} = \frac{1}{25.066} = 0.03989\) - \(p(180) = 0.03989 \cdot 0.6065 = 0.0242\) (per cm)

This is a density, not a probability. To get the probability of a range we would integrate. Using the 68-95-99.7 rule, the interval \(\mu \pm \sigma = [160, 180]\) holds about 68% of people, \([150, 190]\) (\(\mu \pm 2\sigma\)) about 95%, and \([140, 200]\) (\(\mu \pm 3\sigma\)) about 99.7%. The peak density is at \(x = \mu = 170\), where \(p(170) = 0.03989\) (the maximum), and our value at 180 (\(0.0242\)) is lower because 180 is one standard deviation away from the mean.

Key takeaways

  • A random variable is a function that maps the sample space to a range of values; each feature/column in your data is one random variable.
  • Discrete variables (uppercase \(X\)) use sums and a probability mass function; continuous variables (lowercase \(x\)) use integrals and a probability density function, where probability comes from area under the curve.
  • An event “\(A\)” is really “a random variable taking a particular value,” so \(P(A) \approx n_a/n_s\); all probabilities lie in \([0, 1]\) and mutually exclusive outcomes sum to 1.
  • Three rules tie everything together: sum rule (marginalize out a variable), product rule (joint = conditional times marginal), and Bayes rule \(P(Y \mid X) = \frac{P(X \mid Y)\, P(Y)}{P(X)}\), which powers classification.
  • Independence means \(P(X, Y) = P(X)\, P(Y)\); equivalently \(P(X \mid Y) = P(X)\). Check it by comparing the observed joint to the product of marginals.
  • Expectation gives the mean (first moment, \(\mu = \mathbb{E}[X]\)) and variance (second moment, \(\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2]\)); standard deviation is \(\sqrt{\text{variance}}\) and measures spread.
  • Covariance \(\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)]\) measures how two variables move together; for a random vector this becomes a symmetric \(d \times d\) covariance matrix with variances on the diagonal.
  • The Gaussian density \(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) is the workhorse continuous distribution: a bell curve whose area is 1, with the 68-95-99.7 coverage rule; its multivariate form \(\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})\) contains the Mahalanobis distance, which measures distance from a point to a whole distribution.

The topics, explained simply

Why Python & Data Structures

Python is beginner-friendly and lets you work with numbers, strings, and logical expressions while automating tasks with if-else conditions and loops. The slides frame it as “Turn numbers into insights” — analyse data and automate solutions.

Before storing data, you have to decide how to store it. Think of organising a messy bookshelf: everything needs a proper place for easy access. Python gives you four main containers, each suited to a different job. The slide summary is worth memorising:

  • Lists — flexible and editable, like a grocery list.
  • Tuples — fixed and unchangeable, like an ID card.
  • Sets — unique and unordered, like a toy box.
  • Dictionaries — quick lookups, like a word dictionary.

Why it matters: Picking the right data structure makes your code faster, clearer, and easier to maintain — the single most important early habit for data work.

Lists

A list stores an ordered sequence of items. Lists are mutable (you can change them in place), allow duplicates, and can mix data types. You create one with square brackets, empty or pre-filled.

my_list = []                  # empty list
my_list = [1, 'apple', 3.5]   # mixed data types

Because lists are ordered, you access items by index. Python uses zero-based indexing, and negative indices count from the end.

fruits = ['apple', 'banana', 'cherry']
print(fruits[0])    # apple  (first item)
print(fruits[-1])   # cherry (last item)

Lists can be modified directly, and Python ships many built-in methods for common jobs.

numbers = [1, 2, 3, 4]
numbers[1] = 9          # update second item -> [1, 9, 3, 4]
numbers.append(5)       # add at the end    -> [1, 9, 3, 4, 5]
numbers.remove(3)       # remove by value   -> [1, 9, 4, 5]

colors = ['red', 'blue', 'green', 'blue']
colors.sort()                 # alphabetical
print(len(colors))            # 4
print(colors.count('blue'))   # 2

Slicing extracts a sublist without changing the original (end index is excluded), and you can iterate with a for loop or build new lists compactly with a list comprehension.

letters = ['a', 'b', 'c', 'd', 'e']
print(letters[1:4])                     # ['b', 'c', 'd']

squares = [n * n for n in [1, 2, 3]]    # [1, 4, 9]

Why it matters: Lists are the everyday workhorse for collections that grow, shrink, and change — the structure you reach for most often.

Tuples

A tuple is like an ID card: once created, its contents cannot be changed (immutable). Tuples are ordered, can hold mixed data types, and are created with parentheses. The one gotcha: a single-element tuple needs a trailing comma, or Python treats it as a plain value.

empty_tuple = ()
mixed_tuple = (1, 'apple', 3.5)
single_item_tuple = (5,)      # the comma makes it a tuple

You access tuple elements by index just like lists, and you can slice, concatenate, and repeat them — these operations build new tuples rather than modifying the original.

point = (10, 20, 30)
print(point[0])      # 10
print(point[-1])     # 30
print(point[0:2])    # (10, 20)

Immutability brings benefits: data integrity, slightly better performance than lists in some cases, and the ability to be used as dictionary keys (if all elements are immutable). Tuples are also commonly used to return multiple values from a function.

Why it matters: When data must stay constant — coordinates, fixed records, configuration — tuples protect it from accidental change. Use lists when you need to modify; use tuples when you don’t.

Sets

A set is a bag of unique items where order does not matter and duplicates are automatically removed. Sets are mutable, but they cannot store mutable elements (like lists). Create them with curly braces or set() — but note that {} alone makes an empty dictionary, not a set.

unique_nums = {1, 2, 2, 3}    # becomes {1, 2, 3}
empty_set = set()             # {} would be a dict!

unique_nums.add(4)            # insert an element
unique_nums.discard(10)       # safe remove (no error if missing)
unique_nums.remove(2)         # raises an error if missing

Sets support mathematical operations, which makes them powerful for comparing collections:

a = {1, 2, 3}
b = {2, 3, 4}
print(a | b)   # union               -> {1, 2, 3, 4}
print(a & b)   # intersection        -> {2, 3}
print(a - b)   # difference          -> {1}
print(a ^ b)   # symmetric difference-> {1, 4}

Limitations: sets are unordered, so you cannot index or slice them, and discard won’t warn you if an item is missing — which can hide logic bugs.

Why it matters: Sets are the cleanest way to remove duplicates and to test membership or compare datasets quickly.

Dictionaries

A dictionary stores data as key-value pairs, like a real dictionary maps words to definitions. Each key is unique and immutable (string, number, or tuple); values can be any type. Dictionaries give fast, efficient lookups and are mutable.

person = {'name': 'Alex', 'age': 30}
person = dict(name='Alex', age=30)   # alternative

person['city'] = 'NY'   # add a new key
person['age'] = 31      # update a value
del person['city']      # remove a key

Useful built-in methods make access safe and structured:

print(person.get('email', 'not found'))  # default if key missing
print(person.keys())                      # all keys
print(person.values())                    # all values
print(person.items())                     # all key-value pairs

Dictionaries can be nested (a dictionary inside a dictionary) to model hierarchical data such as a product catalogue or an org chart. Limitations: keys must be unique and immutable, and dictionaries can use more memory than lists or tuples.

Why it matters: Dictionaries are the backbone of structured data, counting, grouping, and fast lookups — exactly the patterns you hit constantly in data science.

Functions

A function is a reusable block of code that performs one specific task — like a kitchen appliance built for one job. Define it with def, a descriptive name, parentheses for parameters, an indented body, and usually a return.

def greet(name):
    return f"Hi {name}, how are you?"

print(greet('Alex'))   # Hi Alex, how are you?

Parameters are placeholders in the definition; arguments are the real values you pass when calling. The return statement sends a result back and immediately stops the function; if you omit it, the function returns None.

Scope decides where a variable is visible. Variables created inside a function are local (gone once the function ends); variables defined outside are global. The global keyword lets a function modify a global variable.

x = 2000          # global

def stats(a, b):
    total = a + b   # local: not visible outside
    return total

Lambda functions are small, anonymous, one-line functions for quick tasks:

square = lambda n: n * n
print(square(5))   # 25

Why it matters: Functions follow the DRY principle (“Don’t Repeat Yourself”), make code readable and easy to debug, enable modularity, and let teams collaborate through clear interfaces — the gateway from line-by-line scripts to real software.

Loops

Loops repeat a block of code, automating repetitive work. A for loop iterates over a sequence (list, tuple, string); use range() when you just need to repeat a set number of times.

animals = ['cat', 'dog', 'rabbit']
for animal in animals:
    print(animal)

for i in range(3):     # 0, 1, 2
    print(i)

A while loop keeps running as long as a condition stays true — ideal when you don’t know the number of iterations in advance. Always make sure the condition eventually becomes false to avoid an infinite loop.

count = 0
while count < 3:
    print(count)
    count += 1

Control flow inside loops uses break (exit immediately) and continue (skip to the next iteration). Nested loops put one loop inside another for multi-dimensional data — each outer pass runs the whole inner loop.

for row in [[1, 2], [3, 4]]:
    for value in row:
        print(value)

Why it matters: Loops are how you process large datasets and automate anything repetitive without copy-pasting code.

Writing Functions (Advanced Arguments & Modules)

There are several ways to pass data into a function:

  • Positional arguments — matched by order; if you reverse them, your logic breaks.
  • Keyword arguments — name each value (age=30, name='Nikola'); order no longer matters and the call reads like a sentence. Positional arguments must come before keyword arguments.
  • Default parameters — give a parameter a fallback value (defaults must come after non-default parameters).
  • *args — collects any number of extra positional arguments into a tuple.
  • **kwargs — collects any number of named arguments into a dictionary.
def power(base, exponent=2):       # default argument
    return base ** exponent
print(power(4, 3))   # 64
print(power(5))      # 25 (uses default)

def add_all(*nums):                # *args -> tuple
    return sum(nums)
print(add_all(2, 7, 10, 20))       # 39

def show_details(**info):          # **kwargs -> dict
    print(info)
show_details(name='Nikola', age=25, city='NY')

Functions can return multiple values, which Python packs into a tuple you can unpack:

def stats(x, y):
    return x + y, x - y

res1, res2 = stats(10, 2)   # res1 = 12, res2 = 8

For reuse, save functions in a .py helper module and import them. Document each function with a docstring (triple quotes) so help() explains it later.

# math_utils.py
def area_circle(radius):
    """Calculates area of a circle from radius (numeric only)."""
    return 3.14 * radius ** 2
from math_utils import area_circle
print(area_circle(2))   # 12.56

Wrap any script/test code in the if __name__ == "__main__": guard so it runs only when the file is executed directly — not when it’s imported as a module.

def greet():
    print("hello")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    greet()   # runs only on direct execution

Best practices: add docstrings, use clear names (calculate_mean, not cm), keep each function short and single-purpose (ideally under 20 lines), and write test cases covering edge cases.

Why it matters: Flexible argument styles and reusable modules are what let you build maintainable data-science and ML codebases instead of throwaway scripts.

Deep-dive practical example

The week’s demo builds ShopWise, a lightweight command-line backend for an e-commerce platform — combining dictionaries, lists, functions, and loops. It supports: viewing the catalogue, placing orders, cancelling orders, viewing all/customer orders, and a sales summary. Here is a faithful, self-contained version of the core logic.

# 1. Sample data: a dictionary of dictionaries for the product catalogue
catalogue = {
    "P1001": {"name": "Wireless Mouse", "price": 500, "stock": 20},
    "P1002": {"name": "Keyboard",       "price": 800, "stock": 15},
    "P1003": {"name": "USB Cable",       "price": 100, "stock": 50},
}

orders = []            # list of order dictionaries
order_counter = [1]    # one-item list so functions can mutate it without 'global'

# 2. View the catalogue
def view_catalogue():
    print("Code | Name | Price | Stock")
    for code, item in catalogue.items():
        print(f"{code} | {item['name']} | Rs.{item['price']} | stock {item['stock']}")

# 3. Place an order
def place_order(name, code, qty):
    catalogue[code]["stock"] -= qty                  # reduce stock
    order_id = "O" + str(order_counter[0]).zfill(4)  # e.g. O0001
    order_counter[0] += 1
    orders.append({"order_id": order_id, "customer": name,
                   "code": code, "qty": qty})
    print(f"Order placed: {order_id}")

# 4. Cancel an order (restores stock, then filters the order out)
def cancel_order(order_id):
    for o in orders:
        if o["order_id"] == order_id:
            catalogue[o["code"]]["stock"] += o["qty"]   # restock
    # list comprehension keeps every order EXCEPT the cancelled one
    remaining = [o for o in orders if o["order_id"] != order_id]
    orders.clear()
    orders.extend(remaining)
    print(f"Order {order_id} cancelled")

# 5. View all orders (look up product name from the catalogue)
def view_all_orders():
    print("All orders:")
    for o in orders:
        product = catalogue[o["code"]]["name"]
        print(f"{o['order_id']} | {o['customer']} | {product} | qty {o['qty']}")

# 6. View one customer's orders (uses an if-condition filter)
def view_customer_orders(customer):
    for o in orders:
        if o["customer"] == customer:
            product = catalogue[o["code"]]["name"]
            print(f"{o['order_id']} | {product} | qty {o['qty']}")

# 7. Sales summary: total units sold per product (dictionary counter)
def sales_summary():
    summary = {code: 0 for code in catalogue}     # dict comprehension
    for o in orders:
        summary[o["code"]] += o["qty"]
    for code, count in summary.items():
        print(f"{catalogue[code]['name']}: {count} units")

Step-by-step walkthrough:

  1. The catalogue is a dictionary of dictionaries — each product code ("P1001") is a key whose value is another dictionary holding name, price, and stock. This gives instant lookups: catalogue["P1001"]["name"] returns "Wireless Mouse".
  2. orders is a list that collects one dictionary per transaction. order_counter is deliberately a one-item list because numbers in Python are immutable — wrapping it in a list lets the function update the counter across calls without using a global variable.
  3. place_order reduces the product’s stock, builds a zero-padded unique ID (O0001, O0002, …) with zfill, and appends a new order dictionary.
  4. cancel_order restores the stock for the matching order, then uses a list comprehension to rebuild orders keeping every order except the cancelled one — a clean example of managing state with comprehensions.
  5. view_all_orders loops through orders and does a “VLOOKUP”-style cross-reference: it uses each order’s product code to fetch the readable product name from the catalogue.
  6. view_customer_orders adds a simple if filter to show only one customer’s orders.
  7. sales_summary initialises a counter dictionary from the catalogue codes, then sums quantities per product — the aggregation pattern that underpins real analytics workflows.

A small menu dictionary then maps the user’s numeric choice to the right function (menu.get(choice)), demonstrating dictionary-based dispatch. (The instructor notes a deliberate gap to improve later, once conditionals are covered: the system currently lets stock go negative and doesn’t show available products at order time — good practice exercises.)

  • Python’s four core data structures each have a purpose: lists (ordered, changeable), tuples (ordered, fixed), sets (unique, unordered), dictionaries (key-value, fast lookup).
  • Use zero-based and negative indexing for ordered structures; remember sets and dictionaries can’t be indexed.
  • Mutability matters: lists/sets/dicts are mutable; tuples and dictionary keys are immutable. Single-element tuples need a trailing comma; {} makes a dict, not a set.
  • Functions keep code DRY, readable, modular, and team-friendly. Master positional, keyword, default, *args, and **kwargs arguments, and remember return both sends back a value and exits the function.
  • Understand local vs global scope — variables inside a function don’t exist outside it.
  • Loops (for, while) plus break/continue and range() automate repetitive work; always guard against infinite loops.
  • Package reusable logic in .py helper modules, document with docstrings, and protect script code with if __name__ == "__main__":.

Generators

A generator is a function that produces values one at a time, on demand, instead of computing and storing them all up front. You write it like a normal function but use the yield keyword instead of return. Each call to next() runs the function until the next yield, hands back that value, and then pauses — remembering exactly where it left off — until the next request. When there are no more values, it raises StopIteration.

This laziness is the whole point. A regular function that returns a list of a million squares allocates memory for all million values at once; a generator computes each square only when asked, so its memory footprint stays tiny no matter how large the sequence.

def square_generator(n):
    for x in range(n):
        yield x * x

gen = square_generator(1_000_000)   # almost no memory used yet
print(next(gen))   # 0
print(next(gen))   # 1

You can also write a generator expression, which looks like a list comprehension but with parentheses: (x*x for x in range(10)) yields values lazily, whereas [x*x for x in range(10)] builds the full list in memory. Generators are also composable — one generator can feed another (chaining), letting you build modular data pipelines that process data step by step.

Why it matters: Generators are the standard way to handle large files, streaming/real-time feeds, and even infinite sequences (like a Fibonacci stream) without running out of memory — exactly the situations you hit constantly in data and ML work.

Iterators

An iterator is the object that actually walks through a collection one element at a time. It follows the iterator protocol: it implements __iter__() (which returns the iterator itself) and __next__() (which returns the next value, or raises StopIteration when exhausted). When you write a for loop, Python is calling these methods behind the scenes.

It helps to separate two ideas. An iterable is anything that can be looped over — lists, tuples, sets, dictionaries — and it implements __iter__(). An iterator is the thing that does the actual stepping and implements both __iter__() and __next__(). Every iterator is an iterable, but not the reverse: a list is iterable but is not itself an iterator until you convert it with iter(). Crucially, iterators are single-use — once exhausted, they cannot be rewound.

numbers = [10, 20, 30]
it = iter(numbers)      # turn the iterable into an iterator
print(next(it))         # 10
print(next(it))         # 20
# one more next() past the end raises StopIteration

You can build a custom iterator as a class with __init__, __iter__, and __next__, which is useful for custom traversal logic. For ready-made iterator tools, the itertools module offers count() (an infinite counter), cycle() (loops through elements forever), and repeat() (repeats a value), all of which produce values lazily.

import itertools
counter = itertools.count(start=1, step=2)   # 1, 3, 5, 7, ...
print(next(counter))   # 1
print(next(counter))   # 3

Why it matters: Iterators give you controlled, lazy, one-at-a-time access to data — the foundation for reading huge files line by line, processing real-time streams, and traversing custom data structures without loading everything into memory.

Decorators

A decorator is a function that wraps another function to add behaviour without changing the original function’s code. It takes the original function as an argument, defines an inner wrapper that does something extra (before, after, or around the call), and returns that wrapper. The @decorator_name syntax placed above a function is just shorthand for func = decorator_name(func).

def decorator_function(original_function):
    def wrapper_function():
        print(f'Executing {original_function.__name__}...')
        return original_function()
    return wrapper_function

@decorator_function
def greet():
    print("Hello, World!")

greet()   # prints the "Executing greet..." line, then "Hello, World!"

Decorators can take arguments (by adding another layer of nesting), and they can also be written as classes using __call__ when they need to hold state or handle more complex logic. One gotcha: wrapping a function replaces its metadata (its __name__ and docstring) with the wrapper’s. The fix is functools.wraps, applied to the inner wrapper, which copies the original function’s identity through. When you stack multiple decorators, they apply bottom-to-top — the decorator nearest the function runs first (innermost).

The common real-world uses are timing (measure execution speed), authentication (block unauthorised users), caching/memoisation (store results to avoid recomputation), and logging/input-validation.

import time
def timing_decorator(func):
    def wrapper(*args, **kwargs):
        start = time.time()
        result = func(*args, **kwargs)
        print(f'{func.__name__} executed in {time.time() - start:.4f} seconds')
        return result
    return wrapper

Why it matters: Decorators let you bolt on cross-cutting concerns — timing, security, caching, logging — cleanly and reusably, keeping the core logic of each function focused and readable.

Context managers

A context manager handles the setup and cleanup of a resource automatically, using the with statement. The classic example is open(): inside with open(...) as f: the file is opened on entry and guaranteed to be closed when the block ends — even if an exception is raised — so you never leak file handles.

You can write your own context manager as a class with two special methods: __enter__ (acquires the resource and returns it) and __exit__ (releases it). __exit__ receives any exception details, and returning True from it suppresses the exception.

class FileManager:
    def __init__(self, filename, mode):
        self.filename = filename
        self.mode = mode
    def __enter__(self):
        self.file = open(self.filename, self.mode)
        return self.file
    def __exit__(self, exc_type, exc_value, traceback):
        self.file.close()

with FileManager("test.txt", "w") as f:
    f.write("Hello, World!")

There is also a more concise function-based style using the @contextmanager decorator from contextlib: code before the yield is setup, the yielded value is what with ... as binds to, and a finally block handles cleanup. The standard library gives you ready helpers too — contextlib.closing() to ensure something gets .close()d (e.g. a database connection), redirect_stdout() to send print output to a file, and tempfile.TemporaryFile() for auto-deleting temp files. You can also nest several managers in one with statement (e.g. read from one file, write to another), and the same pattern handles thread locks (with lock:) to prevent race conditions.

Why it matters: Context managers reduce boilerplate, guarantee timely cleanup, and prevent resource leaks (open files, dangling DB connections, unreleased locks) — making code both safer and more readable.

Performance optimisation techniques

Several simple habits make Python noticeably faster and leaner. Use generators instead of lists when you only need to stream through values once — you avoid allocating the whole sequence. Lean on built-in functions like functools.reduce instead of hand-written accumulation loops, since the built-ins are implemented efficiently.

from functools import reduce
nums = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
result = reduce(lambda x, y: x * y, nums)   # 120

Prefer list comprehensions over manual append loops for both speed and clarity, and use sets instead of lists for membership tests — checking value in some_set is far faster than scanning a list because sets are built for \(O(1)\) lookups. For genuinely CPU-heavy, independent work, use multiprocessing to spread the load across multiple cores.

from multiprocessing import Pool

def square(n):
    return n * n

if __name__ == "__main__":
    with Pool(4) as p:
        print(p.map(square, [1, 2, 3, 4]))

Why it matters: These techniques are the difference between code that works on a toy dataset and code that stays fast and memory-stable as data grows from thousands to millions of rows.

Scaling Python for data

Most real-world data — sales records, customer tables, product catalogues — is structured: rows and columns, like a spreadsheet or SQL table. You can represent it in core Python as a list of dictionaries and compute things like total revenue per product with a defaultdict and a loop, and that works fine for small cases.

from collections import defaultdict
revenue = defaultdict(float)
for order in orders:
    revenue[order["Product"]] += order["Price"] * order["Units"]

But this approach falls apart at scale, for four reasons: core Python has no true tabular structure (column selection and sorting feel clunky), filtering/aggregating/transforming requires verbose loops, lists and dicts are not memory-optimised for large data, and statistical or matrix operations aren’t intuitive. The fix is a mindset shift from iteration to vectorisation — stop thinking row-by-row and start thinking column-wise. In core Python, adding two lists concatenates them rather than summing element-wise; in NumPy/Pandas, an operation applies to an entire column at once, executed in optimised C under the hood.

import pandas as pd
df = pd.DataFrame(orders)
df["revenue"] = df["Price"] * df["Units"]          # vectorised, no loop
result = df.groupby("Product")["revenue"].sum()    # one-line aggregation

The Pandas version replaces 8–10 lines of loops and defaultdict logic with 3–4 readable lines that are also faster on large datasets.

Why it matters: This columnar, vectorised way of thinking — not just the syntax — is what separates beginner scripts from professional data pipelines that scale cleanly from thousands to millions of rows, and it’s the bridge into the NumPy/Pandas deep dives ahead.

Deep-dive practical example

Let’s combine several of the week’s ideas into one cohesive data-processing pipeline: read a (potentially huge) numbers file lazily with a generator, transform it through a chained generator, time the whole run with a decorator, and write results safely using a context manager.

import time
from functools import wraps
from contextlib import contextmanager

# 1. A timing DECORATOR (with functools.wraps to keep metadata)
def timed(func):
    @wraps(func)
    def wrapper(*args, **kwargs):
        start = time.time()
        result = func(*args, **kwargs)
        print(f"{func.__name__} ran in {time.time() - start:.4f}s")
        return result
    return wrapper

# 2. A function-based CONTEXT MANAGER for safe file writing
@contextmanager
def open_output(path):
    f = open(path, "w")
    try:
        yield f
    finally:
        f.close()          # guaranteed cleanup, even on error

# 3. A GENERATOR that streams lines from a large file (lazy, low memory)
def read_numbers(path):
    with open(path) as f:
        for line in f:
            line = line.strip()
            if line:
                yield int(line)

# 4. A second GENERATOR chained onto the first — squares each value
def squares(numbers):
    for n in numbers:
        yield n * n

# 5. Tie it together; the decorator times the whole pipeline
@timed
def process(in_path, out_path):
    pipeline = squares(read_numbers(in_path))   # nothing computed yet
    with open_output(out_path) as out:          # context manager handles the file
        for value in pipeline:                   # values produced one at a time
            out.write(f"{value}\n")

process("numbers.txt", "squared.txt")

Step by step: when process is called, read_numbers(in_path) and squares(...) create generator objects but do not run yet — they’re lazy. The with open_output(...) line enters the context manager, opening the output file and guaranteeing it will be closed afterwards. The for value in pipeline loop is what actually drives everything: each iteration pulls one number from the file, immediately squares it, and writes it out — so at no point is the entire file (or the entire list of squares) held in memory. Because the chain is generator-based, this same code handles a 10-line file or a 10-million-line file with the same tiny memory footprint. The @timed decorator wraps the whole call to report how long it took, and @wraps ensures process.__name__ still reads as "process" for clean logging. This is the canonical pattern for memory-efficient data pipelines in Python.

From analysis to production-ready pipelines

Most people can explore data and train a model in a notebook. Real impact comes when that experiment becomes a system that runs the same way every time — in training, in testing, and in production. The course frames this as a maturity ladder: writing scripts → building notebooks → creating workflows → designing pipelines. Maturity in data science is about better systems, not just better models.

A data pipeline is a structured, deterministic sequence of transformations that turns raw data into usable insights. “Structured” means ordered steps; “deterministic” means the same input always gives the same output; “modular/testable” means components are independent; “scalable” means it copes as data grows. The standard progression is Ingest → Clean → Transform → Feature Engineering, and order matters — you never engineer features on dirty data.

Good pipelines share five qualities: reproducible (no uncontrolled randomness or hidden state), modular (one function does one job), transparent (no magic numbers), configurable (thresholds and paths live in config files, not hard-coded), and easy to debug (they fail loudly, not silently). Two design styles exist: functional pipelines (pure functions, simple, composable) and class-based pipelines (encapsulate learned state like a scaler’s mean/variance, aligning with scikit-learn’s .fit()/.transform() model).

# Functional style: each step is a pure function
def clean_data(df):
    return df.dropna(subset=["price"]).drop_duplicates()

def engineer_features(df):
    df = df.copy()
    df["margin"] = df["revenue"] / df["cost"]
    return df

df = engineer_features(clean_data(raw_df))

Why it matters: Many data projects fail not because the model is weak, but because the workflow is fragile — notebook chaos, cells run out of order, hidden dependencies, and leakage that inflates validation scores but collapses in production. Structure is what turns an experiment into engineering.

Advanced pandas: vectorisation over loops

Python loops process rows one at a time, paying interpreter overhead on every row. Vectorisation pushes the work down to C-optimised array operations, often 10x–100x faster on large data. The rule of thumb: if you reach for .apply() or a for loop over rows, stop and look for a vectorised alternative.

# Slow: row-wise loop
df["total"] = [r.price * r.qty for r in df.itertuples()]

# Fast: vectorised
df["total"] = df["price"] * df["qty"]

Why it matters: Performance differences are not marginal. Vectorised code is faster, uses less memory, and is usually shorter and clearer — which matters when pipelines run on multi-gigabyte datasets.

Efficient filtering, grouping and aggregation

Filtering is foundational but must be readable and fast. Modern pandas gives you multi-condition boolean masks (&, |), .query() for readable expressions, .isin() for categorical membership, and .between() for numeric ranges. Grouping unlocks analytical power: basic groupby gives sum/mean, but multi-column and hierarchical grouping reveals nested patterns (e.g. revenue by region, then by product within each region), and custom aggregations compute several business metrics in a single pass.

# Readable filtering
adults = df.query("age > 30 and income < 50000")
df[df["city"].isin(["London", "Paris"])]
df[df["score"].between(0.4, 0.8)]

# Multi-dimension grouping with multiple aggregations in one pass
summary = (df.groupby(["region", "product"])
             .agg(revenue=("revenue", "sum"),
                  avg_margin=("margin", "mean"),
                  customers=("customer_id", "nunique")))

Why it matters: Well-written filtering and grouping logic is the core building block of a reliable pipeline. GroupBy is “analytical architecture” — design the aggregations around the business question first, then implement them cleanly.

Window functions, pivots, missing data and dtype optimisation

Window functions compute over a moving subset of rows without collapsing the data: rolling metrics (e.g. a 7-day moving average to smooth noise), expanding metrics (cumulative/year-to-date), and ranking functions (percentiles, top performers). Pivot tables turn raw rows into dashboard-ready cross-tabs (Region × Product) with one command.

Missing data is a business risk, not just a technical one: first identify the pattern (MCAR / MAR / MNAR), then choose a method (drop, mean/median imputation, forward-fill, model-based), then assess whether it biases the distribution. Dtype optimisation is one of the most overlooked wins — category for low-cardinality strings, int8/int16 for small ranges, float32 where precision allows — yielding up to ~70% memory reduction and faster ops.

df["sales_7d"] = df["sales"].rolling(7).mean()      # rolling
df["cum_rev"] = df["revenue"].expanding().sum()     # expanding
df["rank"] = df["revenue"].rank(pct=True)           # ranking

# Memory optimisation
df["region"] = df["region"].astype("category")
df["age"] = df["age"].astype("int8")

Why it matters: These tools convert raw chronological data into actionable signals and keep large datasets in memory. Always run dataset health checks (distributions, outliers via IQR/Z-score, skewness) before feature engineering — poor data quality silently degrades models.

Feature engineering: turning raw data into predictive power

Models can only learn from what you give them. Feature engineering — transforming columns, combining variables, injecting business logic, extracting signal — often matters more than the choice of model, because features set the upper limit of performance. The four major types are: numeric transformations (scaling, log/power, binning), categorical encoding, date-feature extraction, and interaction features.

Binning turns a continuous variable into meaningful segments (age → 18–25, 26–35, …), capturing non-linear, business-aligned relationships. Encoding converts categories to numbers: one-hot for nominal variables (city, product type), ordinal encoding when categories have a natural order (Low/Medium/High), and label encoding only with care (it invents artificial order). Date extraction pulls year, month, day-of-week, quarter and holiday flags out of a single timestamp to expose seasonality. Interaction/ratio features combine variables: \(\text{Price} \times \text{Quantity} = \text{Revenue}\), \(\text{Revenue} / \text{Cost} = \text{Margin}\).

# Binning
df["age_band"] = pd.cut(df["age"], bins=[18, 25, 35, 50, 120],
                        labels=["18-25", "26-35", "36-50", "51+"])

# Date features
ts = pd.to_datetime(df["order_date"])
df["month"], df["dow"], df["quarter"] = ts.dt.month, ts.dt.dayofweek, ts.dt.quarter

# Interaction + log transform for a skewed column
df["revenue"] = df["price"] * df["qty"]
df["log_income"] = np.log1p(df["income"])

Why it matters: Features are the lenses through which the model sees the world — better lenses, clearer predictions. Scaling (standardisation to mean \(0\) / std \(1\), or min-max to \([0,1]\)) matters for distance- and gradient-based models (SVM, KNN, neural nets) but not for tree-based models (Random Forest, XGBoost). Transform only when the distribution requires it, not automatically — and document why every feature exists.

Data leakage and the fit/transform principle

Leakage is the most dangerous modelling mistake. Three forms: target leakage (a feature encodes the answer, e.g. using “loan approved” to predict “loan default”), temporal leakage (using future data to predict the past), and data contamination (test-set statistics influencing training transformations). The golden rule: if the information would not be available at prediction time, it cannot be used in training.

The fix is the fit/transform principle. fit() learns parameters (means, std, encoding categories, imputation values) from the training data only; transform() then applies those learned parameters to both train and test. Never fit preprocessing on the full dataset before splitting — that leaks test information. In cross-validation, each fold must fit preprocessing independently on its own training subset.

from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
scaler = StandardScaler()
scaler.fit(X_train)                  # learn mean/std from TRAIN only
X_train_s = scaler.transform(X_train)
X_test_s  = scaler.transform(X_test)  # apply the SAME learned stats

Why it matters: Leakage produces beautiful validation scores that fall apart in production. Honest, fold-isolated preprocessing is the difference between a research result and a deployable system.

scikit-learn Pipelines & ColumnTransformer

Manual preprocessing relies on discipline — and inevitably someone scales train and test differently, computes stats on the full dataset, or fails to reproduce a notebook transformation in production. A Pipeline chains preprocessing and modelling into one object: .fit() runs every step in order on training data; .predict() automatically re-applies the exact same transformations before inference. This prevents leakage (transformers fit on train only), improves reproducibility, and makes experiments cleaner (swap StandardScaler for MinMaxScaler by editing one line).

Real datasets are heterogeneous, so a ColumnTransformer applies different mini-pipelines to different column groups in parallel — e.g. numeric columns get impute + scale, categorical columns get impute + one-hot — then concatenates the results into one feature matrix.

from sklearn.compose import ColumnTransformer
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler, OneHotEncoder
from sklearn.impute import SimpleImputer

num = Pipeline([("impute", SimpleImputer(strategy="median")),
                ("scale", StandardScaler())])
cat = Pipeline([("impute", SimpleImputer(strategy="most_frequent")),
                ("ohe", OneHotEncoder(handle_unknown="ignore"))])

pre = ColumnTransformer([("num", num, num_cols),
                         ("cat", cat, cat_cols)])

Why it matters: Pipelines enforce discipline automatically instead of relying on the analyst to remember it. They mirror production systems where transformation and modelling are tightly integrated, and the whole object can be serialised with joblib so deployment behaves identically to the notebook.

Scaling data preparation

As data grows, single-machine pandas hits walls: multi-GB CSVs exceed RAM, large joins crash, and single-core processing bottlenecks. The mindset shifts from “can my laptop handle this?” to “how should this run at scale?” Dask offers parallel, out-of-core computation with a near-pandas API; Polars is a Rust-based engine with lazy evaluation and Apache-Arrow memory efficiency. When infrastructure is limited, chunk-based processing (read_csv(..., chunksize=...)) computes statistics incrementally without distributed systems. Automated EDA tools speed up profiling but don’t replace judgement, and validation frameworks (Great Expectations, Pandera) turn assumptions into enforceable schema and quality rules that catch silent data drift.

# Process a file larger than memory, one chunk at a time
total = 0
for chunk in pd.read_csv("huge.csv", chunksize=100_000):
    total += chunk["amount"].sum()

Why it matters: Scaling is part of professional engineering. The end-to-end lifecycle — Load → Clean → Engineer → Validate → Model — and enterprise concerns like data contracts, layered transformation stages and governance are what make pipelines reliable, collaborative and compliant.

Deep-dive practical example

A single leakage-safe scikit-learn workflow that imputes, encodes, scales, trains, tunes with cross-validation, and serialises for deployment. This is the canonical pattern that ties the whole week together.

import joblib
import pandas as pd
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split, GridSearchCV
from sklearn.compose import ColumnTransformer
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from sklearn.impute import SimpleImputer
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler, OneHotEncoder
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier

# 1. Split FIRST — before any fitting — so test data never leaks into preprocessing.
df = pd.read_csv("customers.csv")
X, y = df.drop(columns="churned"), df["churned"]
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(
    X, y, test_size=0.2, stratify=y, random_state=42)

num_cols = ["age", "tenure", "monthly_spend"]
cat_cols = ["region", "plan"]

# 2. Column-specific preprocessing. Each transformer learns its parameters
#    (medians, modes, means/stds, encoding categories) on training folds only.
num_pipe = Pipeline([
    ("impute", SimpleImputer(strategy="median")),
    ("scale", StandardScaler()),
])
cat_pipe = Pipeline([
    ("impute", SimpleImputer(strategy="most_frequent")),
    ("ohe", OneHotEncoder(handle_unknown="ignore")),
])
preprocess = ColumnTransformer([
    ("num", num_pipe, num_cols),
    ("cat", cat_pipe, cat_cols),
])

# 3. One pipeline = preprocessing + model. .fit() applies every step in order;
#    .predict() re-applies the identical transformations automatically.
pipe = Pipeline([
    ("pre", preprocess),
    ("model", RandomForestClassifier(random_state=42)),
])

# 4. Tune preprocessing AND model parameters together in one cross-validated search.
#    Inside each fold, preprocessing is fit on that fold's training portion only,
#    so cross-validation stays leakage-free.
param_grid = {
    "pre__num__impute__strategy": ["median", "mean"],
    "model__n_estimators": [200, 400],
    "model__max_depth": [None, 10, 20],
}
search = GridSearchCV(pipe, param_grid, cv=5, scoring="roc_auc", n_jobs=-1)
search.fit(X_train, y_train)

print("Best CV ROC-AUC:", round(search.best_score_, 4))
print("Best params:", search.best_params_)

# 5. Honest evaluation on the held-out test set.
best = search.best_estimator_
print("Test ROC-AUC:", round(best.score(X_test, y_test), 4))

# 6. Serialise the ENTIRE pipeline — preprocessing steps, learned parameters,
#    and model weights — into one deployable artifact.
joblib.dump(best, "churn_pipeline.joblib")

# In production: new raw data flows through the exact same transformations.
loaded = joblib.load("churn_pipeline.joblib")
predictions = loaded.predict(X_test)

Step by step: (1) the split happens before any .fit(), so the test set can never influence imputation values, scaling statistics, or encoding categories. (2) the ColumnTransformer routes numeric and categorical columns to their own mini-pipelines and runs them in parallel. (3) wrapping everything in one Pipeline means there is exactly one .fit() and one .predict() — no step can be forgotten or applied inconsistently. (4) GridSearchCV tunes preprocessing choices and model hyperparameters in a single cross-validated search, and because the pipeline is inside the CV loop, each fold fits preprocessing only on its own training data — leakage-safe by construction. (5) the test set is touched once, for an honest estimate. (6) joblib.dump packages the whole transformation-plus-model system so the deployed service behaves identically to the notebook.

Key takeaways

  • A pipeline is an engineered process, not just code: structured, deterministic, modular, configurable, and built in the order Ingest → Clean → Transform → Feature Engineer.
  • Prefer vectorised pandas over loops and .apply() (10x–100x faster); use .query(), .isin(), .between(), multi-level groupby, window functions and dtype optimisation for real-world scale.
  • Feature engineering often beats model choice — bin, encode (one-hot vs ordinal by whether order is meaningful), extract date parts, build interaction/ratio features, and scale only for distance/gradient-based models.
  • Leakage is the cardinal sin. If information wouldn’t exist at prediction time, it can’t train the model. Always fit on train only, transform on both, and split before preprocessing.
  • scikit-learn Pipelines + ColumnTransformer enforce this discipline automatically, enable unified hyperparameter tuning, keep cross-validation honest, and serialise into one deployable artifact via joblib.
  • For data beyond memory, reach for Dask, Polars, chunked reads, and validation frameworks (Great Expectations / Pandera); follow the full lifecycle Load → Clean → Engineer → Validate → Model.

In one line: EDA is the disciplined first pass over a dataset where you summarise, visualise, clean, and probe your data so you understand its structure, spot problems, and prepare it for reliable modelling.

What this week covers

  • Data and variable types: numerical (continuous, discrete) vs categorical (nominal, ordinal), and why type dictates the analysis, visualisation, and encoding you use
  • Central tendency: mean (average), median (middle value), mode (most frequent value)
  • Dispersion: variance, standard deviation, range, and the interquartile range (IQR)
  • Five-number summary and box plots: minimum, \(Q_1\), median, \(Q_3\), maximum
  • Skewness: how right-skew, left-skew, and symmetric distributions relate mean, median, and mode
  • Data cleaning and missing values: imputation (mean/median/mode), removal, forward/backward fill, interpolation, KNN and regression imputation, duplicate removal
  • Outliers: detection via Z-score and the IQR (\(1.5 \times \text{IQR}\)) rule, and why they matter
  • Covariance and correlation: measuring the direction and strength of relationships between variables
  • Encoding categorical data: label encoding and one-hot encoding
  • Scaling and normalization: min-max scaling (normalisation) and standardisation (z-score)
  • Advanced EDA and visualisation: pair plots, correlation heatmaps, target-wise analysis, feature engineering, data leakage, and automated EDA tools

The topics, explained simply

What EDA is and the EDA workflow

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) means examining and summarising a dataset to identify patterns, detect anomalies, test hypotheses, and confirm assumptions before you build any model. It combines descriptive statistics with visual representations like histograms, scatter plots, and correlation matrices to understand the structure and distribution of the data.

The workflow has four stages. First, data collection gathers raw data from sources such as databases, APIs, and CSV files. Second, data cleaning handles missing values and removes duplicates. Third, data profiling generates statistics and checks distributions to understand patterns. Fourth, data visualisation explores those patterns with charts and graphs.

Why it matters: EDA is the bedrock of a data science project. Done well, it improves model accuracy by ensuring correct model selection, and it prevents errors by surfacing inconsistencies, missing values, and biases that would otherwise lead to wrong conclusions.

Data and variable types

Variables fall into two families. Numerical (quantitative) data is either continuous (values on a scale, like age or fare) or discrete (countable values, like number of siblings aboard). Categorical (qualitative) data is either nominal (no order, like gender) or ordinal (ranked, like passenger class: first, second, third).

The type drives everything downstream. Numerical data supports mean, standard deviation, correlation, and regression. Categorical data needs encoding (one-hot or label) before a model can use it. Time-series data is analysed for trend and seasonality, and text data needs tokenisation and vectorisation. Type also dictates the right chart: histograms/box plots/scatter plots for numerical, bar/pie charts for categorical, line/decomposition plots for time-series, word clouds for text.

Why it matters: choosing the wrong statistic or chart for a data type gives misleading results. Knowing the type up front sets the correct handling, method selection, feature engineering, and even memory/compute efficiency.

import pandas as pd
import seaborn as sns

df = sns.load_dataset('titanic')
print(df.dtypes)  # show the type of each column

cat_vars = df.select_dtypes(include=['object', 'category']).columns
num_vars = df.select_dtypes(include=['int64', 'float64']).columns

Central tendency: mean, median, mode

These three measures describe the “centre” of the data. The mean is the arithmetic average. The median is the middle value when the data is sorted. The mode is the most frequently occurring value.

\[\text{mean} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i\]

The mean uses every value, so it is pulled toward extreme values. The median only cares about position, so it is robust to extremes. The mode is the only one that works for categorical data.

Why it matters: the average alone can mislead. If a few very large values exist, the mean overstates the “typical” value while the median stays representative. Reporting more than one measure gives an honest picture.

print("Mean:", df['age'].mean())
print("Median:", df['age'].median())
print("Mode:", df['age'].mode()[0])

Dispersion: variance, standard deviation, range

Central tendency tells you the centre; dispersion tells you how spread out the data is around that centre. Variance quantifies the average squared distance from the mean. Standard deviation is the square root of variance, putting the spread back into the original units. The range is simply the largest value minus the smallest.

\[\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu)^2\]

\[\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}\]

\[\text{range} = \max - \min\]

A small standard deviation means values cluster tightly around the mean; a large one means they are widely scattered.

Why it matters: two datasets can share the same mean yet behave completely differently. Dispersion is what distinguishes “everyone scored near 70” from “scores ranged from 20 to 100.” It tells the full story the average hides.

print("Variance:", df['age'].var())
print("Std Dev:", df['age'].std())
print("Range:", df['age'].max() - df['age'].min())

Five-number summary and box plots

The five-number (five-point) summary is a quick snapshot of a distribution: minimum, first quartile (\(Q_1\), the 25th percentile), median (\(Q_2\), the 50th percentile), third quartile (\(Q_3\), the 75th percentile), and maximum. \(Q_1\) is the value below which 25% of the data falls; \(Q_3\) is the value below which 75% falls.

A box plot (whisker plot) draws this summary visually. The box spans \(Q_1\) to \(Q_3\), a line marks the median, and the whiskers extend to the smallest and largest values that are not outliers. Points beyond the whiskers are flagged as outliers.

\[\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1\]

Why it matters: a box plot shows spread, skew, and outliers in a single glance and makes comparing groups (e.g. fare for survivors vs non-survivors) easy. In pandas, df['age'].describe() prints the summary directly.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
print(df['age'].describe())
sns.boxplot(x=df['age'])
plt.show()

Skewness

Skewness describes whether a distribution leans to one side. If data is right-skewed (positively skewed), a long tail stretches to the right and \(\text{mean} > \text{median} > \text{mode}\). If data is left-skewed (negatively skewed), the tail stretches left and \(\text{mean} < \text{median} < \text{mode}\). If data is symmetric (zero skewness), the mean, median, and mode are approximately equal.

A histogram with a kernel density estimate (KDE) curve is the standard way to see skew. The relationship between the three central measures is itself a quick diagnostic: if the mean is well above the median, suspect a right skew.

Why it matters: skew tells you which central measure to trust (median for skewed data) and signals when a transformation (like a log) might make the data more model-friendly.

from scipy.stats import skew
print("Skew:", skew(df['age'].dropna()))
sns.histplot(df['age'], bins=30, kde=True)
plt.show()

Data cleaning and missing values

Data cleaning fixes errors, inconsistencies, and inaccuracies so the data is reliable. It covers handling outliers, standardising formats (e.g. one unit for weight, one date format), removing duplicates, and addressing missing values.

Missing values arise from data-entry errors, incomplete records, merging issues, system failures, or natural non-applicability (a “sunroof” field is blank for cars without one). You handle them in several ways: - Imputation: fill gaps with the mean, median, or mode of the column (best for numerical data missing at random). - Removal: delete affected rows when only a small proportion is missing and the loss does not affect analysis. - Forward/backward fill: carry the last (or next) known value forward/back, useful for ordered or time-based data. - Linear interpolation: estimate a missing value assuming a straight line between known neighbours, useful for time series. - KNN imputation: estimate from the most similar records (neighbours). - Regression imputation: predict the missing value from other features when a strong relationship exists.

Some algorithms, like decision trees and random forests, can handle missing values natively by splitting on available data.

Duplicate removal matters because duplicates skew statistics and cause overfitting (the model memorises repeated rows). Use drop_duplicates(), deciding whether to keep the first occurrence, the last, or drop all.

Why it matters: garbage in, garbage out. Untreated gaps and duplicates distort every statistic and weaken every model trained on the data.

df = df.dropna()              # remove rows with missing values
df['age'] = df['age'].fillna(df['age'].median())  # or impute
df = df.drop_duplicates()     # remove duplicate rows

Outliers: Z-score and IQR

Outliers are data points that deviate significantly from the rest. They distort averages, skew distributions, and degrade distribution-sensitive models like linear regression and KNN.

The Z-score method measures how many standard deviations a point sits from the mean. A common rule flags any point with a Z-score greater than 3 or less than -3 as an outlier. It works best on roughly normally distributed data.

The IQR method uses the box plot logic: any point beyond 1.5 times the IQR below \(Q_1\) or above \(Q_3\) is an outlier.

\[z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\]

\[\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1\]

\[\text{outlier if } x < Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR} \quad \text{or} \quad x > Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\]

Why it matters: a single extreme value can drag the mean and ruin a model. Detecting outliers early lets you decide whether they are genuine signal or errors to remove.

from scipy.stats import zscore
df['z'] = zscore(df['age'].dropna())
outliers = df[df['z'].abs() > 3]

Covariance and correlation

Both measure the relationship between two variables. Covariance captures the direction: positive means the two variables rise together, negative means one rises as the other falls. But its magnitude is not standardised, so you cannot compare covariances across different pairs.

Correlation fixes that by standardising to a fixed range. It measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship and ranges from \(-1\) to \(+1\): \(+1\) is a perfect positive relationship, \(0\) is no linear relationship, and \(-1\) is a perfect negative relationship.

\[-1 \le \text{correlation} \le +1\]

Why it matters: correlation guides feature selection (features strongly correlated with the target are good predictors) and flags redundancy (two features highly correlated with each other create multicollinearity, so you may drop one). A heatmap of the correlation matrix makes these patterns obvious at a glance.

print(df[['age', 'fare']].cov())   # covariance matrix
print(df[['age', 'fare']].corr())  # correlation matrix
sns.heatmap(df.corr(numeric_only=True), annot=True, cmap='coolwarm')

Encoding categorical data

Models work with numbers, so categorical text must be converted. Label encoding assigns each category a unique integer (e.g. red=0, green=1, blue=2). One-hot encoding creates a separate binary (0/1) column for each category, marking which one a row belongs to.

Why it matters: label encoding is compact but implies a false order, so it suits ordinal data. One-hot encoding avoids implying order, so it suits nominal data, at the cost of extra columns.

from sklearn.preprocessing import LabelEncoder
df['sex_label'] = LabelEncoder().fit_transform(df['sex'])
df_onehot = pd.get_dummies(df, columns=['embarked'])

Scaling and normalization

Feature scaling puts numerical features on a comparable scale so no single large-valued feature dominates the model. Min-max scaling (normalisation) squeezes values into a fixed range, typically 0 to 1. Standardisation (z-score normalisation) centres the data to a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1.

\[x_{\text{scaled}} = \frac{x - \min}{\max - \min}\]

\[x_{\text{standardised}} = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\]

Why it matters: distance- and gradient-based models (KNN, linear/logistic regression, neural networks) are sensitive to feature magnitude. Scaling prevents a feature measured in thousands (like fare) from overwhelming one measured in tens (like age).

from sklearn.preprocessing import MinMaxScaler, StandardScaler
df['fare_scaled'] = MinMaxScaler().fit_transform(df[['fare']])
df['age_std'] = StandardScaler().fit_transform(df[['age']])

Advanced EDA and visualisation

Basic EDA cleans and prepares; advanced EDA uncovers hidden patterns and interactions to guide modelling. Key tools: - Pair plots show every pairwise relationship among several numerical variables at once, revealing clusters, linear/non-linear trends, and class-separable regions. - Correlation heatmaps colour the correlation matrix so strong positive/negative relationships (dark red/blue) and redundant features stand out instantly. - Target-wise EDA compares feature distributions across target classes, e.g. box plots of fare for survivors vs non-survivors, or average survival rate by passenger class and by sex. - Feature engineering creates better predictors: binning a continuous variable into categories, building interaction features (e.g. living area per room), log-transforming skewed targets, extracting date components, and grouping rare categories under an “other” label to prevent overfitting. - Data leakage is a dangerous pitfall: a feature that would only be known after the target event leaks future information into training, inflating performance unrealistically. Suspiciously high correlation with the target is a warning sign. - Automated EDA tools like ydata-profiling and Sweetviz generate full HTML reports (summaries, missing-value analysis, correlation heatmaps, warnings) in one step, with Sweetviz specialising in comparing train vs test sets.

Why it matters: advanced EDA is where the data starts to tell its story, pointing directly at which features to keep, transform, or drop, and protecting you from leakage that would otherwise make a model look great in training but fail in the real world.

Deep-dive practical example

Suppose we collect the ages of 9 passengers (a small numerical sample):

ages = [22, 25, 25, 28, 30, 32, 35, 40, 80]

Step 1 - Mean. Sum \(= 22+25+25+28+30+32+35+40+80 = 317\). Mean \(= 317 / 9 = 35.22\).

Step 2 - Median. The data is already sorted; with 9 values the middle (5th) value is 30. So median \(= 30\).

Step 3 - Mode. The value 25 appears twice; every other value appears once. So mode \(= 25\).

Notice mean \((35.22) >\) median \((30) >\) mode \((25)\). That ordering signals a right (positive) skew, almost certainly caused by the value 80.

Step 4 - Range. Range \(= \max - \min = 80 - 22 = 58\).

Step 5 - Variance and standard deviation (population form, dividing by \(n = 9\)). Deviations from the mean (35.22) squared: - \((22-35.22)^2 = 174.8\) - \((25-35.22)^2 = 104.5\) (twice, since 25 appears twice) \(= 209.0\) - \((28-35.22)^2 = 52.2\) - \((30-35.22)^2 = 27.3\) - \((32-35.22)^2 = 10.4\) - \((35-35.22)^2 = 0.05\) - \((40-35.22)^2 = 22.8\) - \((80-35.22)^2 = 2005.3\)

Sum of squared deviations approximately \(= 2501.9\). Variance \(= 2501.9 / 9 = 278.0\). Standard deviation \(= \sqrt{278.0} = 16.67\). The large standard deviation relative to the cluster of values confirms one value is stretching the spread.

Step 6 - Five-number summary and IQR outlier check. - Minimum \(= 22\) - \(Q_1\) (25th percentile) \(= 25\) - Median (\(Q_2\)) \(= 30\) - \(Q_3\) (75th percentile) \(= 35\) - Maximum \(= 80\)

\(\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 35 - 25 = 10\). Upper fence \(= Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR} = 35 + 15 = 50\). Lower fence \(= Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR} = 25 - 15 = 10\). The value 80 exceeds the upper fence of 50, so it is an outlier. A box plot would draw 80 as an isolated point beyond the whisker.

Step 7 - Correlation. Now pair each age with a fare paid:

ages = [22, 25, 25, 28, 30, 32, 35, 40, 80] fares = [7, 8, 8, 10, 12, 13, 15, 20, 60]

As age rises, fare rises too, so we expect a strong positive correlation. Computing Pearson’s correlation on these two columns gives a value very close to \(+0.98\), indicating an almost perfectly positive linear relationship: older passengers in this sample paid higher fares. In a real workflow you would compute this with df[['age','fare']].corr() and confirm it visually with a scatter plot or heatmap. (Note: the shared outlier at age 80 / fare 60 inflates this correlation, exactly the kind of effect EDA is meant to expose.)

This single example exercised the whole EDA toolkit: central tendency revealed a skew, dispersion quantified the spread, the five-number summary plus the IQR rule caught the outlier, and correlation measured how two features move together.

Key takeaways

  • EDA is the foundation of every data science project: collect, clean, profile, and visualise before you model.
  • Always identify variable types first (numerical vs categorical, continuous vs discrete, nominal vs ordinal); the type decides your statistics, charts, and encoding.
  • Report central tendency (mean, median, mode) alongside dispersion (variance, std, range, IQR); the average alone can mislead.
  • The five-number summary and box plot reveal spread, skew, and outliers at a glance.
  • Skew shows in the ordering of mean, median, and mode: \(\text{mean} > \text{median} > \text{mode}\) is right-skewed; the reverse is left-skewed.
  • Clean missing values deliberately (imputation, removal, fill, interpolation, KNN/regression) and remove duplicates to prevent distortion and overfitting.
  • Detect outliers with the Z-score (\(|z| > 3\)) or IQR (beyond \(1.5 \times \text{IQR}\)) rule and decide whether they are signal or error.
  • Use correlation (\(-1\) to \(+1\)) to find strong predictors and redundant features; covariance gives direction but not standardised strength.
  • Encode categoricals (label for ordinal, one-hot for nominal) and scale numericals (min-max or standardisation) so models treat features fairly.
  • Advanced EDA (pair plots, heatmaps, target-wise analysis, feature engineering) uncovers hidden patterns, and watch out for data leakage that inflates performance unrealistically.

  • Learning and generalization: how machines acquire knowledge from examples (training) and why the real goal is good performance on new, unseen data.
  • The four families of learning techniques mentioned: statistical models, artificial neural networks, kernel methods, and deep learning models.

  • The text-to-numbers pipeline: text must ultimately be represented as numbers (features) before any model can work on it.

The topics, explained simply

What AI is, and where NLP fits

Artificial Intelligence has many definitions. One classic test (the Turing test, “acting humanly”) says a computer is intelligent if a human interrogator, after asking written questions, cannot tell whether the answers came from a person or a machine. To pass, the machine would need several abilities: natural language processing, knowledge representation, automated reasoning, machine learning, computer vision, and robotics. A second view (“thinking rationally”, the laws-of-thought approach) compares the steps of reasoning a program takes to the steps a human takes on the same problem.

Natural Language Processing is the very first capability on that list: communicating in a natural language like English, Hindi, or Tamil. This week zooms into that one capability and the kinds of text tasks machine learning and deep learning can solve.

Why it matters: NLP is what lets a machine read, interpret, and write language, which is the most natural way humans share knowledge, so it sits at the heart of any system meant to behave intelligently.

Pattern recognition and prediction

A pattern is simply any regularity or structure in data. Pattern recognition is the automatic discovery of those patterns. The course illustrates this with number-sequence puzzles. Given “1, 2, 3, 4, 5, ?” you predict 6 because you recognize positive integers. Given “1, 3, 5, 7, 9, ?” you predict 11 (odd numbers), and for “2, 3, 5, 7, 11, ?” you predict 13 (prime numbers). Some sequences need real knowledge: squares (\(1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ? \to 36\)), powers of 2 (\(1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, ? \to 64\)), or the Fibonacci sequence (\(1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, ? \to 13\), since each number is the sum of the previous two).

The key lesson: you can only predict the next item when there is a structure to capture. A truly random sequence has no pattern, so no prediction is possible. Recognizing the structure first, then using it to predict, is exactly what all pattern-recognition systems do, whether the data is text, images, or audio.

Why it matters: Every NLP task in this week is a pattern-recognition task in disguise. Sentiment, POS tags, and entities are all patterns the model learns to detect in text and then predict on new sentences.

Learning and generalization

Learning means acquiring new knowledge or modifying existing knowledge. Here, “knowledge” is familiarity with the information present in the data (knowing what a prime number is, what a Fibonacci sequence is, and so on). Machine learning is mostly data-driven: the model learns from examples, a process called training.

The catch is that a model usually performs very well on the exact examples it trained on, but the real test is how it does on new data it has never seen. That ability to do well on unseen data is called generalization, and good generalization is the actual target of every learning technique. The course names four families that pursue this goal: statistical models, artificial neural networks (1960s), kernel methods (1990s), and deep learning models (from about 2004 onward, an order of magnitude more complex).

Why it matters: A spam filter that only recognizes spam emails it was trained on is useless. Generalization is what makes an NLP model practical on the messy, varied, real sentences people actually write.

NLP, NLU vs NLG

NLP covers approaches that help machines understand, interpret, and generate human language. It splits into two broad families. Natural Language Understanding (NLU) is about making sense of text that already exists: examples include text classification, POS tagging, named-entity recognition, and text-to-speech (which requires reading and understanding text). Natural Language Generation (NLG) is about producing text: examples include visual caption generation (describe an image in words) and speech recognition / speech-to-text (write down what was spoken).

Some tasks need both understanding and generation. Question answering must understand a document and a question, then generate an answer. Summarization must understand a long document, then generate a short version. Machine translation must understand a source sentence, then generate it in another language.

Why it matters: Knowing whether a task is NLU, NLG, or both tells you what the system has to do (read, write, or both) and roughly how hard it is.

Text classification and sentiment analysis

Text classification assigns a sentence or document to one or more categories. The most common version today is sentiment analysis: deciding whether a piece of feedback is positive, negative, or neutral, and to what degree. The course’s example shows why this is subtle: “The food was good, not bad at all” is positive, while “The food was bad, not good at all” is negative, even though both sentences use the same words. Only the word order changed. A close cousin is text categorization, where a whole article is sorted into a topic like politics, business, sports, or entertainment.

Real feedback varies in length and often ignores grammar, so the system must still classify sentences that are not syntactically correct.

Why it matters: Companies use sentiment analysis to read product and movie reviews at massive scale, turning unstructured opinions into measurable signals.

Parts-of-speech (POS) tagging

POS tagging predicts the grammatical category of each word in a sentence based on its role and context. Categories include noun (N), verb (V), article (A), adjective (ADJ), preposition (P), conjunction (CON), pronoun (PRO), and interjection (INT). For the sentence “She sells sea shells by the sea shore”, the tags are PRO V N N P A N N. For “We like eels except as meals”, the tags are PRO V N CON P N.

The tricky part is context: the same word can play different roles in different sentences, so the model must look at the word’s surroundings, not just the word itself.

Why it matters: POS tags are a building block for deeper understanding (parsing, translation, information extraction), because knowing whether a word is a verb or a noun changes how the rest of the sentence is interpreted.

Named-entity recognition (NER)

NER identifies and labels the words or phrases in a sentence that refer to a real-world entity: a person, location, organization, date, time, or quantity. For example, “George Washington” is a Person, “Washington State” is a Location, “General Motors” is an Organization, “4th July” is a Date, “half past noon” is a Time, and “5 litres” is a Quantity.

Unlike POS tagging, where every word gets a tag, in NER only a few words carry an entity label. The system has to find which words (or phrases) are entities and which type each one is.

Why it matters: NER pulls structured facts out of free text (who, where, when, how much), which powers search, knowledge bases, and information extraction.

Textual question answering

Question answering takes a question in natural language and produces an answer, often by reading a document. It is like an open-book exam: find the relevant material, understand it, understand the question, then produce the answer. There are two flavors. Closed-domain QA answers questions about a predefined set of topics (as in many chatbots). Open-domain QA answers questions on any topic (as in web search engines).

QA is a multi-step process: retrieve relevant documents, extract the useful information from them, propose possible answers, and generate the final short text answer. Because it both reads and writes, it needs NLU and NLG together.

Why it matters: QA underlies chatbots, virtual assistants, and search, letting people ask in plain language instead of crafting keyword queries.

Text summarization: extractive vs abstractive

Summarization identifies the most relevant information in a document (or group of documents) and produces a shorter version. There are two approaches. The extraction-based (extractive) approach selects the most important phrases or sentences from the original and stitches them together; it is a content-selection method, so it needs a way to measure how important each sentence is. The abstraction-based (abstractive) approach is semantic: it understands the content and writes a fresh summary in its own words (paraphrasing), the way a human author writes an abstract for a paper.

Abstractive is harder because the system must genuinely understand the content and then express it concisely, possibly tailored to the document’s domain (science, politics, business, and so on).

Why it matters: Summarization compresses long reports, articles, or document sets into something readable in seconds, and the extractive-vs-abstractive distinction explains why some summaries read like copy-paste and others read like genuine rewrites.

Machine translation and why language is hard

Machine translation takes text in a source language and produces text with the same meaning in a target language. It is hard even for trained humans, because you must capture both the meaning and the style of the source, then reproduce them in the target. Languages differ in three ways the course highlights: morphology (rules for forming words, such as plurality, gender, and conjugation; “human” becomes “humans” but “man” becomes “men”), syntax (grammar), and semantics (meaning). Syntactic order varies too: German is Subject-Verb-Object, Japanese is Subject-Object-Verb, and Arabic is Verb-Subject-Object, so translating between them means rearranging the sentence, not just swapping words.

On top of that, a single word can mean different things in different contexts, so the system must read the context to pick the right translation.

Why it matters: Translation removes language barriers at scale, and its difficulty (word order, morphology, context) is a compact illustration of why language is genuinely hard for machines.

The text-to-numbers pipeline

A recurring point this week: all these statistical, neural, kernel, and deep-learning models work on numbers, not raw words. So any text task must first represent the data as numerical features. How text gets processed into numbers is itself an important part of building any pattern-recognition system, and it is the bridge between the language examples above and the math that models actually run on.

Why it matters: No model can multiply or add “words”, so converting text to numbers is the unavoidable first step of every NLP system, which is exactly what the worked example below walks through.

Deep-dive practical example

Let us take one short sentence and walk it all the way from raw text to numeric vectors. This makes the text-to-numbers pipeline concrete.

Our tiny corpus (two documents): - Doc 1: “The food was good, not bad at all.” - Doc 2: “The food was bad, not good at all.”

(These are the two sentiment examples from the lecture, which conveniently use the same words in different orders.)

Step 1 - Tokenization. Split each sentence into tokens (words), dropping punctuation. - Doc 1 tokens: [The, food, was, good, not, bad, at, all] - Doc 2 tokens: [The, food, was, bad, not, good, at, all]

Step 2 - Lowercasing. Normalize case so “The” and “the” are treated as the same word. - Doc 1: [the, food, was, good, not, bad, at, all] - Doc 2: [the, food, was, bad, not, good, at, all]

Step 3 - Stop-word removal. Remove very common, low-information words (the, was, at, all). Note: “not” is sometimes kept for sentiment work because it flips meaning; here we keep it to show why word order still matters. - Doc 1: [food, good, not, bad] - Doc 2: [food, bad, not, good]

Step 4 - Stemming / lemmatization. Reduce words to a base form. Stemming chops endings (running -> run); lemmatization maps to a dictionary form (better -> good). In this small example the words are already base forms, so nothing changes. (For illustration, if Doc 1 had said “the food tasted amazing”, a stemmer might turn “tasted” -> “tast” and a lemmatizer would turn it into “taste”.) - Doc 1: [food, good, not, bad] - Doc 2: [food, bad, not, good]

Step 5 - Build the vocabulary. Collect every unique token across both documents and fix an order. This is the “feature space.” - Vocabulary: [bad, food, good, not] (4 words, indexed 0..3)

Step 6 - Bag-of-words (BoW) vectors. For each document, count how many times each vocabulary word appears. Order in the sentence is thrown away; only counts remain.

word bad food good not
Doc 1 1 1 1 1
Doc 2 1 1 1 1

So both vectors are \([1, 1, 1, 1]\). This exposes a real weakness: bag-of-words gives the positive sentence and the negative sentence the identical vector, even though one says the food was good and the other says it was bad. That is exactly the word-order problem the lecture pointed out, and it is why richer representations (and word position / context) are needed.

Step 7 - TF-IDF vectors. TF-IDF weights each word by Term Frequency (how often it appears in this document) times Inverse Document Frequency (how rare it is across all documents). Words that appear in every document get downweighted because they do not help tell documents apart.

  • Term Frequency (TF): every word appears once in a 4-word document, so \(\text{TF} = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25\) for each word in each document.
  • Document Frequency: every word here appears in both documents (\(\text{df} = 2\) out of \(N = 2\) documents). Using \(\text{IDF} = \ln\!\frac{N}{\text{df}}\) with natural log, \(\text{IDF} = \ln\!\frac{2}{2} = \ln 1 = 0\) for every word.
  • \(\text{TF-IDF} = \text{TF} \times \text{IDF} = 0.25 \times 0 = 0\) for every word.
word bad food good not
Doc 1 \(0\) \(0\) \(0\) \(0\)
Doc 2 \(0\) \(0\) \(0\) \(0\)

The TF-IDF vectors are all zeros because every word appears in every document, so no word helps distinguish Doc 1 from Doc 2. This is the point of IDF: it suppresses words that are common everywhere. With a larger, more varied corpus, distinctive words (say “delicious” appearing in only a few reviews) would get a high IDF and dominate the vector, while ubiquitous words like “food” would be downweighted.

What the example teaches: the pipeline reliably turns words into numbers, but plain bag-of-words and TF-IDF ignore word order, so they cannot by themselves separate “good, not bad” from “bad, not good.” That motivates the models introduced later in the course that account for word position and context.

Key takeaways

  • NLP is one of the core abilities a machine needs to seem intelligent, and this week surveys the main text tasks it enables.
  • A pattern is any regularity or structure in data; prediction is only possible when such structure exists, and pattern recognition is its automatic discovery.
  • Models learn from example data (training), but the real goal is generalization: doing well on new, unseen data.
  • NLP tasks split into Natural Language Understanding (NLU), Natural Language Generation (NLG), and tasks needing both.
  • Core tasks: text classification / sentiment, POS tagging, named-entity recognition, question answering, summarization, and machine translation.
  • Word order matters: “good, not bad” and “bad, not good” use the same words but mean opposite things.
  • Summarization comes in two forms: extractive (select important original sentences) and abstractive (understand and paraphrase in new words).
  • Language is hard because of morphology (plurality, gender, conjugation), differing syntax (SVO vs SOV vs VSO), and context-dependent word meanings.
  • Every model works on numbers, so text must first be converted to numerical features via tokenization, normalization, stop-word removal, stemming/lemmatization, vocabulary building, and vectorization (bag-of-words, TF-IDF).
  • Plain bag-of-words and TF-IDF discard word order, which is why richer, context-aware representations are needed for many tasks.

Week 10: Image, Video and Audio Processing Applications

In one line: This week surveys the wide range of real-world tasks that AI systems perform on visual data (images and video) and audio data (speech and music), showing how each kind of data is first turned into numbers a model can process and what makes each task challenging.

What this week covers

  • How non-text data becomes numbers a machine can work with: an image is a grid of pixels stored as a tensor (a multi-dimensional array of numbers); a video is a sequence of frames (each frame is an image) plus an audio track plus optional subtitle text, so it is multi-modal and is represented as sequences of feature vectors; audio (speech or music) is a signal captured from a microphone, stored as a waveform (amplitude versus time) and then converted into sequences of feature vectors that capture spectral information.
  • Image tasks: classification (assign one label to an image), clustering (group similar images without labels), segmentation (find regions of interest), object detection (find and label objects with bounding boxes), retrieval (search a repository by example image or by keyword), annotation (assign one or more object labels per image), captioning (describe an image in sentences), and generation (sketch-to-image, text-controlled image manipulation, text-to-image).
  • Video tasks: video classification (label a whole clip), video captioning (find events and describe each), visual question answering (VQA: answer a text question about the visual content), and visual common-sense reasoning (VCR: answer plus justify the answer with a reason).
  • Audio/speech tasks: automatic speech recognition (ASR, speech-to-text), speaker identification and verification, spoken language recognition, speech emotion recognition, speaker and language diarisation, speech transcription, text-to-speech (TTS), speech-to-speech translation, spoken dialogue, and speech retrieval. Music tasks: raga identification, instrument identification, music transcription, music retrieval, and music generation (including text-to-music).
  • Applications in other domains: autonomous/unmanned vehicles, autonomous planning and scheduling (space remote agents), recommendation systems, game playing (chess, Go), education (personalised learning, immediate feedback, content generation), banking (customer service, sentiment analysis, fraud/anomaly detection, credit scoring, portfolio management), and healthcare (disease diagnosis, progression prediction, personalised treatment, drug safety, patient monitoring).

The topics, explained simply

How images, video and audio become numbers

A computer cannot “see” or “hear” directly. An image is stored as a set of pixels arranged in a grid; each pixel is one or more numbers (for example, three numbers for the red, green and blue intensity). Stacked together, these numbers form a tensor, which is just a multi-dimensional array. A picture has no time information in it, so it is called a static pattern.

A video adds time. It is a sequence of frames where each frame is an image, and different parts of the video often correspond to different events. Because the number of frames varies from clip to clip, a video is a sequential pattern of varying length. A recorded video is usually multi-modal: it has the visual frames, an audio track, and sometimes text in the form of subtitles. The frames become a sequence of feature vectors capturing visual content, the audio becomes another sequence of feature vectors capturing spectral information, and the subtitles are a sequence of words.

Audio starts as a signal collected from a microphone and converted to digital form. If you plot it, you see a waveform: amplitude (loudness) on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis. Speech in the waveform can be marked into syllables (for example, the syllable “mukhya”). The raw waveform is then turned into sequences of feature vectors that the model actually consumes.

Why it matters: Every task in this week, no matter how clever, starts by converting pixels, frames, or waveforms into numbers (tensors and feature vectors). Understanding this conversion is the foundation for everything that follows.

Image classification, clustering, and the core challenges

Image classification means assigning a single class label to an image, like deciding a picture shows a tiger, giraffe, horse, or bear. Humans learn this very young (a child shown fruit pictures learns “apple”, “banana”). Two challenges make it hard for machines. Intra-class variability: a thousand tiger photos differ in pose, count, viewpoint, background, and color, yet all are “tiger”, and the model must be robust to all of it. Inter-class confusability (inter-class similarity): some classes look alike (cheetah versus tiger), so the model needs strong discrimination ability. As the number of classes grows from a few to many tens, both problems get worse. Natural scene classification (tall building, coast, mountain, forest) is even harder because there is no single object of interest; the whole content and its structure (the pattern) must be captured.

Clustering is the related but different task of taking a set of images and forming subsets (clusters) so that images within a subset are similar, then optionally tagging each subset (residential interiors, mountains, military vehicles, sacred places, sunsets). The key contrast: classification is supervised learning, because every training image comes with a human-assigned label (the desired output). Clustering is unsupervised learning, because there are no labels; the system must discover groupings purely from similarity.

Why it matters: Classification versus clustering is the central supervised-versus-unsupervised distinction of the whole course, and the twin challenges of intra-class variability and inter-class similarity motivate why we need the machine-learning techniques taught later.

Segmentation, detection, retrieval and annotation

Segmentation identifies regions of interest (ROIs) in an image, for example drawing the boundary around just the cat. Object detection goes further: in an image with many objects (people walking, people on bicycles), it finds each ROI, draws a bounding box, and labels the box’s category (person boxes in blue, bicycle boxes in red). So detection is “where is it” plus “what is it” for possibly many objects.

Retrieval is searching a repository of images. There are two styles. Query-by-example (QBE): the query is itself an image, and the system returns the database images most similar to it, by matching the query against every stored image and keeping those whose similarity exceeds a threshold. Query-by-semantics (QBS): the query is a keyword like “tiger”, and the system returns every image whose labels include that keyword. QBS only works if the repository images have first been annotated.

Annotation is the task of assigning one or more object labels to each image (for example \(\{\text{tiger}, \text{rocks}\}\) or \(\{\text{bear}, \text{ice}\}\)). It is harder than classification because the number of labels varies from image to image, depending on how many objects of interest are present. Once a repository is annotated, keyword search becomes trivial: just check whether the keyword is among an image’s labels.

Why it matters: These tasks build on each other (annotation enables semantic retrieval, segmentation enables detection) and show how similarity measures and labels turn a pile of images into something searchable and structured.

Captioning and the visual reasoning ladder (VQA, VCR)

Image captioning takes an image and produces a natural-language description, like “a group of people at an outdoor market”. Generating one or two sentences is image captioning; generating a full descriptive paragraph is image paragraph captioning, which can be even more detailed than a single human’s description. Captioning requires understanding the visual content and then expressing it as text that is syntactically correct, meaningful, and coherent. Both rely on selective attention, focusing on the relevant content and ignoring the rest. Video captioning is harder still: a video has several events, so the system must first localise the events, caption each one, then stitch the captions together.

Visual question answering (VQA) asks a text question about an image or video and expects a text answer, for example “Is there something to cut the vegetables with?” (answer depends on whether a knife is present) or “How many children in the bed?” (requires counting). Traditional VQA can be posed as picking the right option from multiple choices; open-ended VQA requires generating a free-form sentence like “The zebra is grazing in grasslands”, which needs natural-language generation on top of visual understanding.

Visual common-sense reasoning (VCR) is an order of magnitude harder. Given an image, a question (Q), an answer (A), and a rationale/reason (R), the system must not only choose the answer (the Q-to-A subtask) but also justify it (the question-and-answer-to-rationale subtask). If multiple choices are given for both answer and rationale, each is a classification task; if both must be generated as free text, it additionally requires natural-language generation and genuine reasoning with common-sense knowledge.

Why it matters: This is a ladder of increasing intelligence, from describing what is seen, to answering questions about it, to reasoning about why an answer is correct, and it shows why complex tasks are solved by combining several deep-learning models.

Image generation: sketch-to-image, manipulation, and text-to-image

Image-to-image translation maps an image from a source domain to a target domain. A clear example is sketch-to-image: given a hand-drawn sketch of a person’s face, generate a realistic photo of that face. A well-built system can make a sketch-to-photo output nearly as good as regenerating a photo from a real photo.

Text-controlled image manipulation keeps the structure of an input image but changes its content according to a text caption. Given flower images and the caption “the flower has petals that are white and has patches of yellow”, the system recolors each flower’s petals to white with a yellow center; given bird images and “blue crown and white belly”, it adapts each bird accordingly. This needs the model to understand both the image content and the text instruction and then apply the change.

Text-to-image translation (a form of prompt engineering) goes from words alone to a brand-new image. The word prompt “a painting of squirrel eating a burger” produces an image with a squirrel and a burger in a painted style, even though such a scene is pure imagination. This is the reverse of captioning: caption goes image-to-text, while text-to-image goes text-to-image.

Why it matters: These are the generative-AI tasks of the visual domain. They are not just academic, they let you create large amounts of new data, and they motivate the generative deep-learning models introduced later in the course.

Speech tasks: recognition, identity, and the factors that make ASR hard

Speech is the most natural mode of human communication, learned without formal education. Automatic speech recognition (ASR), or speech-to-text, converts a spoken sentence into text. Note it only recognises the words; understanding the meaning is a separate task. Several classification-style tasks take speech as input and produce an identity: speaker identification (who is speaking) and speaker verification (is the claimed identity true, used in biometric access), spoken language recognition (which language), and speech emotion recognition (happy, sad, angry, surprised, disgust, fear, or neutral).

ASR systems are categorised by many factors. By vocabulary: limited-vocabulary (a few hundred words) versus vocabulary-independent. By speech type: isolated-word (one word, good for commands like Alexa), connected-word (short pauses between words, like digits “two five seven”), and continuous speech (read text, or the most natural conversational/fluent speech). By speaker: speaker-dependent (trained for one person), multi-speaker (a fixed set), or speaker-independent (anyone). By language: monolingual, multilingual (a few languages), or language-independent (any language or a mix). The recording channel also matters: a microphone is usually clean, a landline adds noise, a mobile phone adds the most distortion.

Why it matters: The hardest ASR system is vocabulary-independent, speaker-independent, and language-independent, working on conversational speech over a mobile phone with background noise, varied emotions, and varied speaking rates. Naming these factors explains exactly what robustness an ASR system must achieve.

Diarisation, transcription, and speech generation/translation

When several people talk (as in a meeting), speaker diarisation segments the recording to identify who spoke when and for how long, since people take turns and turn lengths vary. Spoken language diarisation does the same for language, identifying which segment is in which language when speakers mix two or more languages. Speech transcription is the most complex combined task: for a multi-speaker conversation it produces the text, the speaker for each segment (diarisation plus speaker identification), the language (spoken language identification and diarisation), and even the emotion per speaker. It is literally built from several of the earlier sub-tasks.

On the generation side, text-to-speech (TTS) or speech synthesis takes text and produces natural-sounding, fluent speech. Good TTS must capture the right voice and emotion and handle prosody (pitch variation and stress), which differs across languages and sentence types. Most TTS systems are built for a single speaker’s voice; voice conversion systems can change one person’s voice into another’s to support multiple voices.

Speech-to-speech translation takes a spoken sentence in a source language and produces a spoken sentence in a target language, conveying the same meaning (not word-for-word). It is a pipeline of three systems: ASR (source speech to source text), machine/text translation (source text to target text), and TTS (target text to target speech). A spoken dialogue system lets a human converse with a computer entirely in speech, combining speech recognition, speech understanding, information retrieval, response formation, and speech synthesis. Speech retrieval finds all speech segments containing a given keyword. Music processing mirrors these ideas: raga identification, instrument identification, music transcription, music retrieval, and music generation including text-to-music.

Why it matters: The most powerful audio systems are assembled by chaining simpler modules, which is the recurring lesson of this week: complex AI behaviour comes from combining many machine-learning and deep-learning components.

Applications in other domains

The same machine-learning and deep-learning ideas extend well beyond images, video, and audio. Autonomous (unmanned) vehicles must process visual data to see the road and detect obstacles, and they rely on reinforcement learning for control. Autonomous planning and scheduling powers remote agents in space systems. Recommendation systems suggest products or movies by tracking a user’s history and the preferences of similar users. Game-playing systems handle chess and Go, sometimes beating humans.

In education, AI enables personalised learning tailored to a student’s grasping ability, immediate feedback through quizzes and evaluation, and automatic generation of supplementary learning content. In banking, AI provides customer service (chat or speech), sentiment analysis of feedback, fraud detection framed as anomaly detection (spotting abnormal behaviour), credit scoring and credit-worthiness assessment using regression-style prediction, credit-card approval decisions, portfolio management via optimisation, and financial planning. In healthcare, AI supports disease diagnosis, disease-progression prediction, personalised treatment plans from genetic/clinical/lifestyle data, prediction of medication and therapy response, drug-safety testing that reduces physical trials, and real-time patient monitoring, often integrating text, image (X-rays, scans), and signal data.

Why it matters: These examples show that the techniques taught in the rest of the course are general-purpose. The same supervised, unsupervised, prediction, anomaly-detection, and reinforcement-learning tools reappear across self-driving cars, finance, and medicine.

Deep-dive practical example

Let us trace one automatic speech recognition (ASR) pipeline end to end, the same speech-to-text conversion task introduced this week, using a single spoken sentence.

  1. Capture. A person speaks a sentence into a microphone, for example the news-bulletin sentence “Is bulletin key mukhya samachar” in an Indian language. The microphone converts the air-pressure changes of the speech into an analog electrical signal.

  2. Digitise into a waveform. The analog signal is sampled and stored digitally on the computer. If you plot it, you get the speech-signal waveform: amplitude on the vertical axis, time on the horizontal axis. Within this waveform the spoken words and syllables occupy distinct stretches of time; the sentence can be marked into syllable parts such as “Is”, “bulletin”, “key”, “mukhya”, “samachar”. Notice an important real-world wrinkle from the lecture: the syllable “mukhya” in isolation may be barely audible, yet inside the full sentence it is clearly perceived, because surrounding context aids perception, exactly the kind of thing humans do effortlessly and machines struggle with.

  3. Convert the waveform into feature vectors. The raw waveform is too low-level to classify directly, so it is transformed into a sequence of feature vectors that capture the spectral (frequency) content over short time windows. The result is a sequential pattern, a series of feature vectors over time, and crucially its length varies with how long and how fast the sentence was spoken.

  4. Feed the sequence into the model. The ASR model takes this varying-length sequence of feature vectors as input. The design choices from this week apply here: is it vocabulary-independent so it can handle any words from the language? Is it speaker-independent so it works regardless of whether a man, woman, or child spoke? Is it language-independent or at least multilingual? Was it trained for the recording channel in use (microphone versus noisy mobile phone)? The model must be robust to emotion, speaking rate, and background noise.

  5. Produce the output text. The model decodes the feature-vector sequence into the recognised words and emits the sentence as text in the appropriate language, here the Indian-language transcription corresponding to “Is bulletin key mukhya samachar”. This text is the final output of the speech-to-text system.

  6. Plug into a bigger system (optional). This ASR pipeline rarely stands alone. In speech-to-speech translation it becomes stage one (source speech to source text), feeding a text-translation system and then a text-to-speech system. In a spoken dialogue system it feeds an understanding module, an information-retrieval module, a response-formation module, and finally a speech-synthesis module so the computer can reply out loud. In speech transcription of a meeting, it runs alongside speaker diarisation, language identification, and emotion recognition to say who said what, when, in which language, and with what emotion.

The same shape of pipeline, capture, convert to a numeric representation, feed a model, produce output, recurs for images (image to pixels/tensor to features to predicted class) and video (clip to sequences of feature vectors to model to label or caption).

Key takeaways

  • All visual and audio AI starts by turning raw data into numbers: images into pixel tensors, videos into sequences of feature vectors (frames + audio + subtitle words), and audio into waveforms then feature vectors.
  • Images are static patterns; videos are varying-length sequential patterns; this difference is why video tasks need models that handle variable-length sequences.
  • Classification is supervised (labels provided), clustering is unsupervised (no labels); the recurring difficulties are intra-class variability and inter-class similarity, plus growing numbers of classes.
  • Image tasks form a progression: classification, clustering, segmentation, detection, annotation, retrieval (by example or by semantics), captioning, and generation; annotation is what makes keyword (semantic) retrieval possible.
  • Visual intelligence climbs a ladder: captioning (describe), VQA (answer), and VCR (answer plus justify with a reason), with open-ended versions requiring natural-language generation.
  • Image generation spans sketch-to-image translation, text-controlled image manipulation, and text-to-image (prompt-driven) synthesis, the generative-AI side of vision.
  • Speech tasks split into input tasks (ASR, speaker/language/emotion ID, diarisation, transcription) and generation tasks (TTS, speech-to-speech translation, dialogue); ASR difficulty is set by vocabulary, speech type, speaker, language, and channel.
  • The most powerful systems (speech transcription, speech-to-speech translation, spoken dialogue) are pipelines that chain several simpler modules together.
  • The same machine-learning and deep-learning toolkit generalises to autonomous vehicles, recommendation, games, education, banking, and healthcare, often combining multiple data types and techniques.

Week 11: Paradigms of Machine Learning — I (Supervised & Unsupervised)

In one line: Machine learning is the automatic discovery of patterns in data, and this week splits that idea into its two foundational paradigms — supervised learning (learn from labelled data to map inputs to outputs) and unsupervised learning (find structure in unlabelled data by grouping similar samples).

What this week covers

  • What machine learning really is: learning from data by finding regularity, structure, or relations — i.e. pattern recognition. A pattern is “any regularity or structure in data,” and pattern analysis is the “automatic discovery of patterns in data.”
  • Why we need many examples: real data has intra-class variability (samples in the same class look different) and inter-class similarity (samples from different classes can look alike); a learner must cope with both.
  • Training vs test data & generalization: the model learns on training data, but is judged on unseen test data. Performing well on new data is the generalization ability — the goal of every ML technique.
  • Learning as parameter estimation: a model is a function with parameters; “learning” means estimating those parameters from data (statistical models / maximum likelihood, kernel methods, neural networks / error-correction, deep learning / representation learning).
  • The ML paradigms overview: supervised, unsupervised, semi-supervised, active, self-supervised, reinforcement, online, and domain adaptation (zero/one/few-shot). This week focuses on the first two.
  • Supervised learning: labelled data, function approximation \(y = f(\mathbf{x})\), and its two tasks — classification (categorical target) and regression / numeric prediction (continuous target).
  • Unsupervised learning: unlabelled data, learning by observation, and its two tasks — clustering (group similar samples) and association rule mining (find items that occur together).
  • Similarity measures: the criterion used to decide which samples are “alike”; changing it changes the clusters you get.

The topics, explained simply

What is machine learning? (Learning from data)

Humans predict by spotting structure. Shown a number series like \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, ?\), you fill in \(6\) because you recognise the natural-number pattern; for \(1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, ?\) you fill in \(13\) because you spot the Fibonacci rule (each term is the sum of the previous two). Even for an unfamiliar series like \(3, 5, 12, 24, 41, ?\), you can crack it by looking at the differences (\(2, 7, 12, 17, 22, 27 \ldots\)) and discovering the hidden regularity, which gives \(63\). But a truly random series has no relation, so no prediction is possible.

The driving factor in every case is the relation or structure present in the data. A machine does exactly the same thing: it looks for that regularity. The formal name for this is pattern analysis.

\[\text{A } pattern = \text{any regularity or structure in data (or its source).}\] \[\textit{Pattern analysis} = \text{automatic discovery of patterns in data.}\]

Why it matters: This is the whole premise of ML. If structure exists, a machine can learn it and predict; if it does not, no algorithm can help. Everything else this week is a way of discovering that structure.

Learning, knowledge, and dealing with variability

“Learning” is defined narrowly here as acquiring new knowledge or modifying existing knowledge, where “knowledge” means familiarity with the information in the data. When you first met a strange series and then figured out its rule, you modified your knowledge — that is learning.

Real-world data is messy. In image classification (tiger, giraffe, horse, bear), every tiger image differs in pose, background, and orientation — this is intra-class variability. In scene classification (street vs inside-city, mountain vs open-country), images from different classes can look confusingly alike — this is inter-class similarity.

Why it matters: A learner cannot memorise one example; it must see many and extract the common structure of a class while ignoring irrelevant variation, and it must find features sharp enough to tell similar classes apart. This is why ML is data-driven (it learns from examples / samples).

Training, testing, and generalization

ML is data-driven: the phase of learning patterns from examples is the training process, and the result is a trained model. But just as a student must be tested, a model must be verified — and verified on data it did not see during training, called the test data.

A model that predicts well on unseen data is said to generalize. Generalization ability is achieved only when the model performs well on data not seen during training.

Why it matters: Good training-set accuracy is meaningless if the model fails on new data. Achieving good generalization is stated as the goal of every machine learning technique. (Analogy from the lecture: exam questions are not the same ones discussed in class — if you truly learned, you can answer the new ones.)

Learning as parameter estimation

In ML, a model is some mathematical function that captures the data’s pattern. That function has parameters, and “learning” is nothing but estimating those parameters from data. The exact technique depends on the model family:

  • Statistical models — maximum likelihood (maximise the likelihood of generating the observed data).
  • Kernel methods (e.g. SVM) — learning optimal linear relationships.
  • Artificial neural networks — error-correction learning (reduce the gap between network output and desired output).
  • Deep learning — representation learning (learn a better representation of the input for the task).

\[\text{model output } y = f(\mathbf{x}; \boldsymbol{\theta}), \text{ where } \boldsymbol{\theta} \text{ are the parameters estimated during training.}\]

Why it matters: It reframes “learning” as a concrete, mathematical optimisation — find the parameter values that best fit the data — which is the common thread under every algorithm later in the course.

The paradigms of machine learning

The course lists eight paradigms: supervised, unsupervised, semi-supervised, active, self-supervised (SSL), reinforcement (RL), online, and domain adaptation (zero-shot, one-shot, few-shot). Most everyday ML uses the first two, which is why this week treats supervised and unsupervised in depth and only names the rest for now.

Why it matters: Knowing where supervised and unsupervised sit on this map helps you recognise, later, what is different about the others (e.g. RL learns from rewards, SSL invents its own labels).

Supervised learning

“Supervised” means learning happens under supervision. A child is repeatedly told “this is a tiger, this is a dog” by a parent (the supervisor) and learns to recognise them. In ML there is no human in the loop, so the supervision must come from the data itself: the training data is labelled, meaning each sample’s target value is already known.

The goal is to learn the underlying function that maps inputs to the output — this is called function approximation.

\[y = f(\mathbf{x}), \text{ where } \mathbf{x} \text{ is the input vector and } y \text{ is the known target.}\]

The nature of the target decides the task: - Classification — target is categorical/discrete. Identify which class a new observation belongs to. Examples: adult vs child (binary), iris species (multi-class: setosa/versicolor/virginica), handwritten digit recognition (10 classes, 0–9 from a \(28\times28 = 784\)-dimensional image), spam vs not-spam, disease present vs absent. - Regression (numeric prediction) — target is continuous. Predict a number. Examples: salary from years of experience (fit a line/curve); rainfall from temperature, humidity, pressure (fit a surface over a 3-D input).

Both tasks are two-step: (1) build the model (training / parameter estimation) and (2) use the model to predict on unseen test data. Quality is measured by accuracy (classification: % of test samples correctly classified) or how close predictions are to true values (regression) — always on data not seen in training.

Why it matters: Supervised learning is the most-used paradigm; the classification/regression split, judged purely by the target type, is the mental model you will reuse for almost every applied ML problem.

Unsupervised learning

Unsupervised learning is the opposite of supervised: the data has no labels and no target. Nobody tells the machine what to learn, so it must discover structure on its own through learning by observation — comparing each sample to the others and grouping ones that are alike.

Its two common tasks: - Clustering — partition the data into cohesive groups so that samples within a group are more similar to each other than to samples in other groups. Example: group people by similar height/weight; group customers by similar purchased products. - Association rule mining — a rule-based approach that discovers interesting relationships among variables, e.g. which products are frequently purchased together (also related to frequent-pattern mining).

Why it matters: Huge amounts of real data are unlabelled (labelling is expensive). Unsupervised methods extract hidden structure — segments, groupings, co-occurrences — without anyone having to annotate the data first.

Similarity measures

Clustering hinges entirely on the similarity measure — the criterion for deciding two samples are “alike.” Take the same 500–600 students: cluster by gender and you get a male group and a female group; cluster by region (north vs south India) and you get two completely different groups. Same data, different similarity measure, different clusters — and therefore different hidden information surfaces.

Why it matters: The result of unsupervised learning is not fixed by the data alone; it is shaped by the similarity measure you choose. Choosing it well is what makes the discovered groups meaningful. (Cluster quality is later judged by compactness — how tightly similar samples sit together.)

Deep-dive practical example

Use one tiny dataset of people described by height (\(x_1\), cm) and weight (\(x_2\), kg) — the same data, viewed two ways.

Person Height (x1) Weight (x2) Label y (supervised only)
A 90 21.5 0 = child
B 100 32.45 0 = child
C 116 38.21 0 = child
D 152 46.8 1 = adult
E 173 82.9 1 = adult
F 183 90.0 1 = adult

Part 1 — Supervised (labels ARE used). The target is known: 0 = child, 1 = adult, so \(y \in \{0, 1\}\). The task is to learn \(y = f(x_1, x_2)\) that maps height and weight to the class. - Step 1 (build the classifier / training): the algorithm estimates the parameters of a boundary that separates the labelled children from the labelled adults. Looking at the data, children sit low-left (short and light) and adults sit high-right. A simple learned rule of thumb might be: “if height is roughly below ~135 cm, predict child (0); otherwise predict adult (1).” - Step 2 (predict on unseen data): a NEW person arrives with height = 160 cm, weight = 60 kg — no label. Feed \((160, 60)\) into \(f(\mathbf{x})\). Since \(160 > {\sim}135\), the model outputs \(y = 1 \rightarrow\) adult. - How we’d judge it: hold out some labelled people the model never trained on, predict their class, and compute accuracy = % correctly classified. Good accuracy on these unseen people means good generalization.

Part 2 — Unsupervised (labels are DROPPED). Now hide the \(y\) column entirely. We only have the six (height, weight) points and no idea what “child” or “adult” even means. - Step 1 (learning by observation): for each person, the algorithm measures similarity to the others using height and weight. Persons A, B, C are all near each other (heights 90–116, weights ~21–38). Persons D, E, F are near each other (heights 152–183, weights ~47–90). - Step 2 (clustering): using distance as the similarity measure, the algorithm partitions the six points into two clusters — Cluster 1 = {A, B, C} (the low-height, low-weight group) and Cluster 2 = {D, E, F} (the high-height, high-weight group). It outputs cluster identities (Cluster 1 / Cluster 2), NOT the names “child” / “adult” — it has no labels, so it cannot name the groups. - The new person \((160, 60)\): compute distance to each cluster; they are closer to Cluster 2 (D/E/F), so they are assigned there.

The contrast. Same points, same new person, same final grouping — but supervised learning knew the answer was “adult vs child” and learned a labelled boundary, while unsupervised learning only discovered that two natural groups exist and left them unnamed. And note the similarity-measure lesson: if we instead clustered these people by, say, “north vs south India,” the two groups would be totally different even though the data is identical.

Key takeaways

  • ML = learning from data by discovering patterns (any regularity/structure); if there’s no structure, there’s nothing to learn.
  • A learner must handle intra-class variability (same class, different-looking samples) and inter-class similarity (different classes, similar-looking samples) — which is why it needs many examples.
  • Train on training data, but judge on unseen test data; performing well there is generalization, the goal of every ML technique.
  • Learning = estimating a model’s parameters from data; the technique differs by model family (max likelihood, kernel/SVM, error-correction NNs, representation learning).
  • Supervised = labelled data → \(y = f(\mathbf{x})\) (function approximation). Categorical target → classification; continuous target → regression. Both are build-then-predict, two-step processes.
  • Unsupervised = unlabelled data → learning by observation. Clustering groups similar samples; association rule mining finds items occurring together.
  • The similarity measure is the key knob in clustering — same data, different measure, different (and differently meaningful) clusters.
  • Crucial difference: supervised learning uses labels to learn a known mapping; unsupervised learning discovers structure and outputs unnamed cluster identities.

Week 12: Paradigms of Machine Learning — II (Advanced Paradigms)

In one line: When you don’t have enough labels, enough samples, or a stable data stream, these advanced paradigms (semi-supervised, active, self-supervised, online, meta/few-shot, and reinforcement learning) let you still build good, generalisable models.

What this week covers

  • Semi-supervised learning — train on a small labelled set plus a large unlabelled set by generating pseudo-labels and iterating.
  • Active learning — a special case of semi-supervised learning that actively queries the most uncertain/informative samples for an expert to label.
  • Self-supervised learning (SSL) — turn unlabelled data into its own supervision via a pretext task, then use the learned representations for downstream tasks.
  • Online learning — learn incrementally from a continuous stream of data and adapt as the distribution drifts, instead of training once on the whole dataset.
  • Meta learning / few-shot learning — “learning to learn”: rapidly adapt to a new task or new class using only a few examples, by transferring knowledge from prior tasks.
  • Reinforcement learning (RL) — an agent learns by trial and error, taking actions in an environment and updating its behaviour based on rewards and penalties.

The topics, explained simply

Semi-supervised learning

Supervised learning is powerful, but it needs labels for everything, and labelling is expensive: it takes people, time, and money. In practice you often have a mountain of data but only a tiny slice of it is labelled. If you train only on that tiny labelled slice, the model tends to overfit and never sees the full diversity of the data.

Semi-supervised learning (“semi” = partially supervised) uses both the small labelled set and the large unlabelled set together. The recipe is iterative: (1) train a model on the small labelled set; (2) run the unlabelled data through it and let the model predict “pseudo-labels” for them; (3) move those now pseudo-labelled samples into the training set; (4) retrain on the combined labelled + pseudo-labelled data. Repeat steps 2-4 until generalisation is good enough.

A concrete case: image classification with a few labelled images and a huge corpus of unlabelled images. The unlabelled images, once pseudo-labelled, inject extra information about the data distribution that the small labelled set alone could never provide.

Why it matters: It dramatically reduces the need for expensive manual labelling while still pulling useful signal out of the unlabelled data to improve accuracy and generalisation.

Active learning

Active learning is a special case of semi-supervised learning, but with a smarter twist. Instead of blindly trusting pseudo-labels, it identifies the samples the model is most unsure about and sends only those to a human expert for a correct label.

How the loop runs: start with the small labelled pool and train a model. Predict on the unlabelled pool and flag the low-confidence predictions — the blurry, ambiguous, or confusing cases. Query strategies such as uncertainty sampling, diversity sampling, entropy-based sampling, or query-by-committee pick these samples. A human annotator labels them, they move from the unlabelled pool into the training pool, and the model is retrained. The cycle repeats until the model hits the desired accuracy.

Why pay for expert labelling? Because the number of genuinely ambiguous samples is small. Out of thousands of unlabelled points, only a handful are confusing enough to need an expert — and that small, targeted spend buys a big jump in quality. In image classification, for example, the algorithm might pick out blurry or borderline images for an expert to label.

Why it matters: It gets the most model improvement for the least labelling cost by spending human effort only where the model is genuinely confused — ideal when annotation is expensive.

Relationship: semi-supervised learning is the broader umbrella; active learning is one technique inside it. Both leverage unlabelled data, but active learning focuses on efficient labelling (querying the valuable samples), while semi-supervised learning in general focuses on using the unlabelled data to better understand the overall data distribution.

Self-supervised learning (SSL)

SSL goes one step further than semi-supervised learning: it claims to need no labelled data at all to get started. It is especially popular in deep learning for unstructured data — images, speech, and text — and sits at the intersection of supervised and unsupervised learning.

The trick: instead of relying on human labels, the model generates its own supervisory signals (“pseudo/fake” labels) from inherent patterns in the data. This happens in two stages. The pretext task uses a pre-trained model (often trained on a large dataset for some other task) to learn meaningful representations and produce pseudo-labels. The downstream task (classification, object detection, segmentation, etc.) then reuses those learned representations and pseudo-labels in a supervised manner.

Because the pre-trained model already captured useful structure, passing your new (unlabelled) images through it yields partially-learned features for free — this is transfer learning. SSL essentially transforms an unsupervised problem into a supervised one by manufacturing its own labels. Popular SSL models include SimCLR and BYOL, and pretext tasks like rotation prediction.

Why it matters: It removes the dependence on manual labels, learns from vast readily-available unlabelled data, and produces representations that transfer well — improving generalisation on downstream tasks.

Online learning

Everything above assumes offline learning: you have the whole dataset and train on it once. But sometimes you can’t — either the data never stops arriving, or the world keeps changing, or you simply don’t have the memory to hold it all.

Online machine learning learns from a continuous stream of data and updates itself incrementally as each new sample (or batch) arrives. It shines in dynamic environments where the underlying data distribution drifts over time — for example stock prices or weather patterns, where a model built on January-to-May data would be badly wrong by the rainy season. It also fits streaming data (sensor feeds, social media feeds) and limited-resource settings where you can’t load 100,000 samples at once and instead process small chunks, adapting as you go.

Good practice (from the slides): start with an offline model to debug fundamentals first, use a validation set to track performance over time, detect and manage concept/data drift (e.g. weight recent data more), keep a rollback plan to revert bad updates, and update incrementally rather than overfitting to the latest few examples. Example techniques include online gradient descent and incremental learning.

Why it matters: It enables real-time adaptation to changing data without retraining on the entire dataset, and it keeps working when memory or compute is constrained. The catch: if it is supervised, every incoming sample needs a label, so annotation cost can be high.

Meta learning / few-shot learning

Sometimes the labels exist but the samples are too few. Building a complex model on a handful of examples per class is hard, and traditional ML/DL struggles: it needs large labelled datasets, breaks under distribution shifts, and must be retrained from scratch when a new class appears. (Imagine a 10-class classifier; add 5 new classes and you would normally rebuild the whole model — slow, and biased toward the original classes.)

Meta learning — “learning to learn” — fixes this by training across a distribution of tasks so the model can rapidly adapt to a new task or new class with only a few examples, transferring knowledge from prior tasks. Because it learns on so few samples, it is also called few-shot learning (the two terms are used interchangeably).

The standard setup is N-way K-shot classification: discriminate between \(N\) classes (\(N\)-way) using \(K\) examples per class (\(K\)-shot). \(K=1\) is one-shot learning; \(K=5\) is a typical few-shot setting; zero-shot learning uses no training samples at all and relies on auxiliary information like class attributes or text descriptions. A classic use case is medical image / rare-disease detection, where large labelled datasets simply don’t exist.

Why it matters: It cuts training time, improves generalisation to similar-but-unseen tasks via transfer learning, and supports adaptive/domain adaptation — all critical when training examples are genuinely scarce.

Reinforcement learning (RL)

RL is a completely different paradigm — neither supervised nor unsupervised. It mirrors how humans learn many things: not from labelled answers, but from experience and trial and error. An agent learns to achieve a goal in an uncertain, possibly complex environment by taking actions and receiving feedback in the form of rewards (positive) or penalties (negative).

The key concepts: the agent (the decision-maker), the environment (the context and all external factors), actions (the moves the agent can make), the state (the situation the agent is in), the reward (feedback signalling how good or bad an action was), and the policy (the strategy mapping states to actions, learned through interaction).

The loop: the agent starts in a state, takes an action based on its current policy, the environment transitions to a new state and returns a reward, and the agent updates its policy toward actions that earn higher cumulative rewards. Think of a robot navigating a maze: step forward without hitting an obstacle = positive reward; hit an obstacle = negative reward, so it adjusts. Over many cycles it learns to maximise cumulative reward and reach the goal.

Why it matters: RL is the engine behind robotics (movement and control) and game-playing AI, and any setting where an agent must learn good sequential decisions from feedback rather than from labelled examples.

Deep-dive practical example

A self-supervised, cluster-based pseudo-label pipeline for object detection (based on the Noroozi et al. “Boosting Self-Supervised Learning via Knowledge Transfer” approach shown in the slides). Goal: build a useful classifier from a dataset that has no labels at all.

  1. Start with unlabelled data. You have a large pile of images and zero labels. Pure supervised learning is impossible here.

  2. Pick a pre-trained model for the pretext task. Take an arbitrary deep model already trained on a large image collection for some other purpose. In deep nets this has an encoder (input images -> compressed feature representations in intermediate layers) and a decoder (turns features into a task output).

  3. Extract feature representations. Pass every image from your unlabelled dataset through the encoder and grab the output of an intermediate layer. Even though your images were not in the original training set, the encoder still produces partially-meaningful features — this is the transfer-learning benefit.

  4. Cluster the features. Run a clustering algorithm over all the extracted feature vectors and compute the cluster centers. Similar images naturally group together — say four clusters, drawn as green, red, blue, and orange.

  5. Assign pseudo-labels. For each image, find its nearest cluster center and assign that cluster’s identity as the image’s pseudo-label. Green -> class A, red -> class B, orange -> class C, blue -> class D. You now have a fully “labelled” dataset — labelled by the model itself, not by humans.

  6. Train the target model supervised. Attach a fresh classifier and train it from scratch (or fine-tune) to predict the pseudo-labels A/B/C/D from the images. You have converted an unsupervised problem into a supervised one.

  7. (Optional) refine and transfer. Use the resulting representations for the real downstream task (object detection / classification). Optionally add a small amount of genuine labelled data to fine-tune, giving a better-generalising model.

Net result: starting from completely unlabelled images, the pipeline manufactured its own targets (via clustering), trained a supervised classifier on them, and produced transferable representations — all with essentially zero manual annotation cost.

Key takeaways

  • These paradigms exist to handle three real-world pains: too few labels, too few samples, or a non-stationary / streaming data source.
  • Semi-supervised learning uses small labelled + large unlabelled data and iteratively generates pseudo-labels; active learning is the subset that asks experts to label only the most uncertain samples (uncertainty/diversity/entropy sampling, query-by-committee).
  • Self-supervised learning needs no labels: a pretext task on a pre-trained model creates pseudo-labels and representations (via transfer learning), which feed a supervised downstream task. Examples: SimCLR, BYOL, rotation prediction.
  • Online learning trains incrementally on streaming data and adapts to drift; great for real-time systems and limited memory, but annotation cost is high since every sample needs a label.
  • Meta / few-shot learning is “learning to learn” — rapid adaptation to new tasks/classes from few examples via transfer across many tasks. Terminology: N-way K-shot, one-shot (\(K=1\)), few-shot (e.g. \(K=5\)), zero-shot (uses class descriptions, no samples).
  • Reinforcement learning is its own paradigm (neither supervised nor unsupervised): an agent learns a policy by trial and error to maximise cumulative reward. Core terms: agent, environment, action, state, reward, policy. Big in robotics and gaming.
  • Quick comparison axes from the slides — main idea, label requirement, model update, efficiency, best use case, handling new patterns, annotation cost: active learning = medium cost / expert-on-demand; SSL = low cost / lots of unlabelled data; online learning = high cost / real-time streaming.

Week 13: Regression Methods — I (Linear & Multiple Linear Regression)

In one line: Regression predicts continuous numeric outputs by fitting a linear function (a straight line for one input, a hyperplane for many) to training data using the least-squares method, then evaluates it with RMSE.

What this week covers

  • Numeric prediction (regression): a supervised task that predicts continuous values from input.
  • Simple linear regression (straight-line fitting): one input variable, one output, fit \(y = w x + w_0\).
  • The least-squares cost function: minimize the sum of squared errors between actual and predicted values.
  • Closed-form solution for slope and intercept derived by setting the cost’s gradient to zero.
  • Multiple linear regression: two or more input variables, fitting a plane or hyperplane.
  • The normal equation / pseudo-inverse: a closed-form solution \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}\) for the weight vector.
  • RMSE evaluation: measuring how well the model generalizes to unseen test data.

The topics, explained simply

What is regression (numeric prediction)?

Regression is the task of predicting a continuous number for a given input. Unlike classification, which assigns a category, regression produces a real-valued output. The inputs are called independent variables (or predictors), and the single output is the dependent variable (or response). The output is “dependent” because its value depends entirely on the input values through some underlying function.

Common examples from the lecture: predicting salary from years of experience (one input), predicting rainfall from temperature and humidity (two inputs), and predicting wine quality from 10 chemical measurements like alcohol content, pH, and sulphates. With one input, fitting a regression model means fitting a line or curve; with two inputs, it means fitting a surface; with many inputs, it means fitting a higher-dimensional surface.

\[y = f(x)\]

Regression is a two-step process. Step 1 is the training (learning) phase, where the model learns the mapping function from input-output pairs in the training set. Step 2 is the testing phase, where the learned model predicts outputs for new, unseen inputs.

Why it matters: Most real-world prediction problems (prices, demand, sensor readings, scores) are about estimating a number, not a label. Regression is the foundational tool for all of them.

What “linear” means and the two types of linear regression

Linear regression assumes the relationship between input and output is linear, meaning the output is a weighted sum of the inputs plus a constant. Importantly, the function is linear in its parameters (the weights), which is exactly why it is called a “linear model for regression.” This linearity in the parameters is what makes the solution easy to compute.

There are two types. Simple linear regression has a single input variable and a single output, so it fits a straight line, which is why it is also called straight-line regression. Multiple linear regression has two or more input variables and a single output, so it fits a plane (for two inputs) or a hyperplane (for more than two inputs), also called a linear surface.

\[\hat{y} = w_1 x + w_0\]

(simple linear regression; \(w_1\) is the slope, \(w_0\) is the \(y\)-intercept)

Why it matters: Linear models are simple, fast, interpretable, and have an exact closed-form solution. They are the natural starting point before any more complex method.

Simple linear regression and the least-squares cost function

With one input, we want a line \(\hat{y} = w x + w_0\) that best represents the data. The parameters \(w\) (slope) and \(w_0\) (intercept) are unknown and must be estimated from the training data. Real data never lies perfectly on a line because of intrinsic noise (recording errors, system effects), so two people with the same years of experience can have slightly different salaries. We accept that and look for the line with the smallest total error.

To measure how good a line is, for every training example we compute the predicted value \(\hat{y}\), subtract the actual value \(y\), and square the difference. Squaring makes the error positive (we care about magnitude, not sign) and penalizes big misses more. Summing these squared errors over all \(N\) examples gives the total error, which we treat as a cost function of \(w\) and \(w_0\) that we want to minimize.

\[\hat{y} = f(x_n, w, w_0) = w x_n + w_0\]

\[E(w, w_0) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_n (\hat{y}_n - y_n)^2\]

This is an unconstrained optimization problem (no constraints on \(w\) and \(w_0\)). We solve it with the gradient method: take the partial derivatives of \(E\) with respect to \(w\) and \(w_0\), set both to zero, and solve.

Why it matters: The squared-error cost is the heart of “least squares.” Because the derivatives are linear in the parameters, the minimization has a single unique solution that can be written in closed form, no iterative search required.

Closed-form solution for slope and intercept

Setting the two partial derivatives of the cost to zero and solving gives explicit formulas. The optimal slope \(\hat{w}\) is a ratio: the numerator is the sum of the products of input deviations and output deviations from their means (a covariance-like quantity), and the denominator is the sum of squared input deviations (a variance-like quantity). The optimal intercept \(\hat{w}_0\) is then computed so the line passes through the point of the two means.

\[\hat{w} = \frac{\sum_n (x_n - \mu_x)(y_n - \mu_y)}{\sum_n (x_n - \mu_x)^2}\]

\[\hat{w}_0 = \mu_y - \hat{w}\,\mu_x\]

Here \(\mu_x\) is the sample mean of the input and \(\mu_y\) is the sample mean of the output. So the entire training phase reduces to: compute the two means, then plug into these two formulas. In the testing phase, for any new input \(x\), predict \(\hat{y} = \hat{w} x + \hat{w}_0\).

\[\hat{y} = f(x, \hat{w}, \hat{w}_0) = \hat{w} x + \hat{w}_0\]

Why it matters: Training a simple linear regression is just arithmetic on means and deviations. There is nothing to tune and no risk of getting stuck; the answer is exact.

Multiple linear regression and the normal equation (pseudo-inverse)

When there are \(d\) input variables, the input becomes a vector \(\mathbf{x} = [x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_d]\), and the model is a weighted sum of all inputs plus a bias. By augmenting each input vector with a leading 1 (so \(x_{n0} = 1\) multiplies \(w_0\)), the prediction becomes a clean dot product between the weight vector \(\mathbf{w} = [w_0, w_1, \ldots, w_d]\) and the augmented input vector.

\[\hat{y} = w_0 + w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \cdots + w_d x_d = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_n\]

We again minimize the same least-squares cost, but now \(E\) is a function of the whole weight vector \(\mathbf{w}\). The optimality condition is that the gradient (the vector of all partial derivatives with respect to each weight) equals the zero vector. Solving leads to the normal equation, a single matrix formula for the optimal weights.

\[E(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_n (f(\mathbf{x}_n, \mathbf{w}) - y_n)^2\]

\[\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}\]

Here \(\mathbf{X}\) is the data matrix of size \(N \times (d+1)\): each row is one training example augmented with a leading 1, and \(\mathbf{y}\) is the column vector of actual outputs. The combination \((\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top\) is called the pseudo-inverse of \(\mathbf{X}\), written \(\mathbf{X}^+\), so the solution can also be written \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbf{X}^+ \mathbf{y}\). A square matrix has a normal inverse, but the rectangular data matrix \(\mathbf{X}\) does not, which is exactly why the pseudo-inverse is used.

\[\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbf{X}^+ \mathbf{y}, \quad \text{where } \mathbf{X}^+ = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top\]

One key condition: this works only when the number of input variables \(d\) is much smaller than the number of training samples \(N\) (\(d < N\)). If \(d\) is larger than \(N\), there is data insufficiency, the inverse cannot be computed reliably, and \(\mathbf{w}\) cannot be optimally estimated.

Why it matters: The normal equation gives one matrix expression that solves regression for any number of inputs at once. It is the standard closed-form recipe for multiple linear regression.

Evaluating the model: squared error and RMSE

After training, we judge the model on test data the model never saw, which measures generalization. For a single test sample, the error is the squared difference between predicted and actual. Across all test samples, we average the squared errors and take the square root to get the root mean squared error (RMSE). Smaller RMSE means a better model.

\[E = (\hat{y} - y)^2 \quad \text{(squared error for one sample)}\]

\[\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N_t} \sum_n (\hat{y}_n - y_n)^2}\]

Because RMSE depends on the scale of the outputs, it is often expressed as a percentage by dividing by the average actual output and multiplying by 100.

\[\text{RMSE}(\%) = \frac{\text{RMSE}}{\frac{1}{N_t} \sum_n y_n} \times 100\]

Why it matters: RMSE puts error in the same units as the target and gives a single, interpretable number for comparing models. Evaluating on unseen data is the only honest test of generalization.

Deep-dive practical example

Let us fit a straight line to a small salary dataset (salary in thousands of rupees) using the least-squares formulas, exactly as in the lecture’s salary example.

Data (5 points): years of experience \(x\) and salary \(y\) - \(x = 1\), \(y = 20\) - \(x = 3\), \(y = 36\) - \(x = 6\), \(y = 43\) - \(x = 11\), \(y = 59\) - \(x = 9\), \(y = 64\)

Step 1 — Compute the means. - \(\mu_x = (1 + 3 + 6 + 11 + 9) / 5 = 30 / 5 = 6\) - \(\mu_y = (20 + 36 + 43 + 59 + 64) / 5 = 222 / 5 = 44.4\)

Step 2 — Compute deviations from the means for each point. - \(x=1\): \((x - \mu_x) = -5\), \((y - \mu_y) = 20 - 44.4 = -24.4\) - \(x=3\): \((x - \mu_x) = -3\), \((y - \mu_y) = 36 - 44.4 = -8.4\) - \(x=6\): \((x - \mu_x) = 0\), \((y - \mu_y) = 43 - 44.4 = -1.4\) - \(x=11\): \((x - \mu_x) = 5\), \((y - \mu_y) = 59 - 44.4 = 14.6\) - \(x=9\): \((x - \mu_x) = 3\), \((y - \mu_y) = 64 - 44.4 = 19.6\)

Step 3 — Numerator: sum of products \((x - \mu_x)(y - \mu_y)\). - \((-5)(-24.4) = 122.0\) - \((-3)(-8.4) = 25.2\) - \((0)(-1.4) = 0.0\) - \((5)(14.6) = 73.0\) - \((3)(19.6) = 58.8\) - Sum \(= 122.0 + 25.2 + 0.0 + 73.0 + 58.8 = 279.0\)

Step 4 — Denominator: sum of squared input deviations \((x - \mu_x)^2\). - \((-5)^2 = 25\) - \((-3)^2 = 9\) - \((0)^2 = 0\) - \((5)^2 = 25\) - \((3)^2 = 9\) - Sum \(= 25 + 9 + 0 + 25 + 9 = 68\)

Step 5 — Slope. - \(\hat{w} = 279.0 / 68 = 4.103\) (approximately \(4.10\))

Step 6 — Intercept. - \(\hat{w}_0 = \mu_y - \hat{w}\,\mu_x = 44.4 - 4.103 \times 6 = 44.4 - 24.62 = 19.78\)

So the best-fit line is:

\[\hat{y} = 4.10\,x + 19.78\]

Step 7 — Predict a new value. For a candidate with 8 years of experience (\(x = 8\)): - \(\hat{y} = 4.10 \times 8 + 19.78 = 32.8 + 19.78 = 52.58\)

The model predicts a salary of about 52.6 thousand rupees for 8 years of experience. (As a sanity check on the method, the lecture’s full 10-point version gives \(\hat{w} = 3.54\) and \(\hat{w}_0 = 23.21\), and predicting \(x = 10\) yields \(\hat{y} = 3.54 \times 10 + 23.21 = 58.584\) against an actual \(58.000\), a squared error of just \(0.34\).)

Key takeaways

  • Regression predicts continuous values; inputs are independent variables, the single output is the dependent variable.
  • Simple linear regression fits a straight line \(\hat{y} = w x + w_0\); multiple linear regression fits a hyperplane \(\hat{y} = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}\).
  • Both are trained by the least-squares method, minimizing the cost \(E = \frac{1}{2} \sum (\hat{y} - y)^2\).
  • Because the cost is quadratic and its derivatives are linear in the parameters, there is always a unique closed-form solution (no iteration needed).
  • Simple LR solution: \(\hat{w} = \frac{\sum (x - \mu_x)(y - \mu_y)}{\sum (x - \mu_x)^2}\) and \(\hat{w}_0 = \mu_y - \hat{w}\,\mu_x\).
  • Multiple LR solution (normal equation): \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}^+ \mathbf{y}\), where \(\mathbf{X}^+\) is the pseudo-inverse; valid only when \(d < N\).
  • Model quality is measured by RMSE on unseen test data (and RMSE% to be scale-independent); smaller is better and signals good generalization.

Week 14: Regression Methods — II (Nonlinear Regression, Overfitting & Regularization)

In one line: When a straight line cannot capture the data, we fit curves with polynomials, watch out for overfitting when the model is too complex for the data we have, and tame it with ridge regularization (an L2 penalty on the weights).

What this week covers

  • Nonlinear regression — modeling an output as a nonlinear function of one or more inputs (any function that is not a straight line/plane).
  • Polynomial curve fitting — the main technique for nonlinear regression with a single input: fit a degree-\(M\) polynomial to the data.
  • Feature / design-matrix view — turning each scalar input into a vector of its powers (a “phi” transform) so the curve-fitting problem becomes ordinary linear regression in a higher-dimensional space; stacking those vectors gives the design matrix (Vandermonde matrix).
  • Model complexity vs overfitting — raising the degree \(M\) increases model complexity, drives training error toward zero, but on small data sets makes test error explode and weights blow up.
  • Regularization / ridge regression (L2 penalty) — adding a \(\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2\) term to the cost to penalize large weights, restoring a good fit even with few samples.
  • Polynomial hyper-surface fitting (multiple inputs) — extending the same idea to two or more independent variables, where we fit a surface (hyper-surface) instead of a curve.
  • Summary of regression — how linear regression, polynomial curve fitting, and ridge regression all fall under the same “linear model for regression” umbrella.

The topics, explained simply

Nonlinear regression

In linear regression we fit a best-fit line (or plane) that relates inputs to an output. But for many data sets the true relationship is a curve, not a line. If you force a straight line through curved data, the fit is poor. Anything that is not linear — a curve, a circle, a parabola, a sine-like wiggle — is a nonlinear function, and nonlinear regression is about estimating such functions from data.

The catch: a line has one fixed equation, but “nonlinear” is not one thing. A circle, a parabola, and an arbitrary squiggle each have different forms, and some squiggles have no neat closed-form expression at all. So we need a flexible family of functions whose parameters we can estimate from training data.

As with linear regression there are two cases. Simple nonlinear regression has a single input and a single output (we fit a curve); the common techniques are polynomial curve fitting and neural networks. Multiple nonlinear regression has two or more inputs and a single output (we fit a surface); the common techniques are polynomial regression and neural networks.

Why it matters: Real-world relationships (temperature vs humidity, dose vs response) are rarely straight lines. Nonlinear regression is what lets a model bend to follow the data.

Polynomial curve fitting

For a single input \(x\), we model the output as a polynomial of degree \(M\): a weighted sum of the powers of \(x\). The terms \(1, x, x^2, \dots, x^M\) are called the monomials of the polynomial up to degree \(M\), and each gets its own weight.

\[\hat{y} = w_0 + w_1 x + w_2 x^2 + \dots + w_M x^M = \sum_{j=0}^{M} w_j x^j\]

Notice how degree controls shape. When \(M = 1\) the polynomial collapses to \(\hat{y} = w_0 + w_1 x\), which is just the equation of a line — so simple linear regression is the special case \(M = 1\) of polynomial curve fitting. \(M = 2\) gives a quadratic (parabola-like) curve, \(M = 3\) a cubic (can wiggle like part of a sine curve), and larger \(M\) gives ever more complex shapes. The exact shape is decided by the actual values the weights take.

The weights \(w_0 \dots w_M\) (there are \(M+1\) of them) are the regression coefficients to be learned. A key observation: the polynomial is a nonlinear function of \(x\), but it is a linear function of the coefficients \(\mathbf{w}\). Because of that, polynomial curve fitting is still called a linear model for regression, and the same machinery as linear regression applies.

Why it matters: One simple knob — the degree \(M\) — turns a straight-line fitter into a flexible curve fitter, while keeping the easy, closed-form math of linear regression.

Training: least squares and the design-matrix (feature) view

We are given \(N\) training pairs \((x_n, y_n)\). We pick the weights that minimize the sum of squared errors between the actual \(y_n\) and the predicted \(\hat{y}_n\).

\[E(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} (y_n - \hat{y}_n)^2\]

Because the prediction is linear in \(\mathbf{w}\), this error function is quadratic in \(\mathbf{w}\), its derivative is linear in \(\mathbf{w}\), and setting the derivative to zero gives a single closed-form (unique) solution.

The clean trick is to transform each scalar input into a feature vector of its powers. Define \(\phi(x_n) = [1, x_n, x_n^2, \dots, x_n^M]\) (an \((M+1)\)-dimensional vector). For example, if \(x_n = 5\) and \(M = 3\), then \(\phi(x_n) = [1, 5, 25, 125]\). Now the prediction is just a dot product, exactly like multiple linear regression — only with \(\phi(x)\) in place of the raw input vector.

\[\hat{y}_n = \mathbf{w}^\top \phi(x_n)\]

Stack the phi-vectors as rows to get the design matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Phi}\) (size \(N \times (M+1)\); also called the Vandermonde matrix). The least-squares solution is then the same normal-equation formula as multiple linear regression.

\[\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \boldsymbol{\Phi})^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \mathbf{y} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}^{+} \mathbf{y} \quad (\text{where } \boldsymbol{\Phi}^{+} \text{ is the pseudo-inverse of } \boldsymbol{\Phi})\]

An important condition: this solution exists only when the number of samples \(N\) is larger than the degree \(M\) (\(M < N\)). If you have fewer samples than the polynomial degree, the solution does not exist.

Why it matters: The phi-transform is the bridge — it shows that “fit a curve” is really “do linear regression in a richer feature space,” so we reuse the exact same solver.

Testing and choosing the degree M

After training, for any new test input \(x\) we transform it to \(\phi(x)\) and take the dot product with \(\hat{\mathbf{w}}\) to predict \(y\). Goodness is measured by squared error per sample, and overall model quality by root mean squared error (RMSE) over all test samples.

\[E = (\hat{y} - y)^2 \quad (\text{per sample})\]

\[E_{\text{RMSE}} = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{N_t} \sum_{n=1}^{N_t} (\hat{y}_n - y_n)^2 }\]

There is no fixed rule for the best degree \(M\). You choose it experimentally: start at \(M = 1\), build the model, measure test error, then increment \(M\) and repeat, and pick the \(M\) that gives the lowest test error. Remember \(M = 1\) is just straight-line regression, so polynomial curve fitting contains simple linear regression as a subset.

Why it matters: The degree \(M\) is a hyperparameter. RMSE on held-out test data is the yardstick you use to tune it.

Model complexity vs overfitting

As \(M\) grows, the model can represent more complex curves and the number of parameters (\(M+1\)) grows — that is what “model complexity” means here. With plenty of data this is good. The danger appears when data is scarce.

The course uses Bishop’s classic example: 10 points sampled from the underlying function \(\sin(2\pi x)\) plus small Gaussian noise. At \(M = 0\) the fit is a useless flat line; at \(M = 3\) the curve nicely tracks the true sine; at \(M = 9\) (with only 10 points) the curve passes through every training point exactly — training error becomes 0 — but it oscillates wildly and is nowhere near the true function. On unseen test data its error is huge. This is overfitting: very low training error but very high test error, meaning the model fails to generalize.

Two tells of overfitting show up together. First, \(M = 9\) with \(N = 10\) violates the \(M < N\) condition (degree equals sample count), so the model essentially memorizes the data. Second, the magnitudes of the learned weights blow up to very large values; multiplying those huge weights by the feature vector produces wildly large predictions and therefore large errors on new points.

The complexity is not inherently bad — it is bad relative to the data. If you keep \(M = 9\) but increase samples from 10 to 15 the oscillations shrink, and at 100 samples the \(M = 9\) fit is very close to the true sine. So a complex model needs enough data. But in practice we rarely have the luxury of huge data sets, so we must either pick a modest \(M\) or find another way to control complexity.

Why it matters: Overfitting is the central failure mode of flexible models. Recognizing its signatures — zero/low training error, exploding test error, ballooning weights, too few samples — is what keeps you from shipping a model that memorizes instead of learns.

Regularization and ridge regression (L2 penalty)

We want the expressiveness of a complex model without the overfitting. Since one root cause is weights growing huge, we add a penalty on the size of the weights directly to the cost function. This is quadratic (L2) regularization, and a linear model for regression with this penalty is called ridge regression.

\[E(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} (y_n - \hat{y}_n)^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2} \lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2\]

Now minimizing the cost does two things at once: it makes predictions fit the data (the squared-error term) and it keeps the weights small (the \(\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2\) term). The trade-off between them is controlled by \(\lambda\), the regularization coefficient, chosen in the range 0 to 1 (excluding 0; \(\lambda = 0\) means no regularization, back to plain least squares).

Solving for the optimum gives the same normal equation as before, but with \(\lambda\) times the identity matrix added inside the inverse — a diagonal “ridge” added to \(\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \boldsymbol{\Phi}\) (which also stabilizes the matrix inversion).

\[\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \boldsymbol{\Phi} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \mathbf{y}\]

Choosing \(\lambda\) well is critical and is done experimentally. Too small and you barely regularize (overfitting returns). Too large and the penalty dominates: the weights are forced so small that the model learns nothing, training and test errors are both high — that is underfitting. A moderate \(\lambda\) is the sweet spot.

Why it matters: Ridge regression lets you keep a high-capacity model and still generalize on small data — you do not have to throw away complexity, you just constrain it.

Polynomial hyper-surface fitting (multiple inputs)

Everything above used a single input, so we fit a curve. With two or more independent variables and one output, the same polynomial idea applies, but now we fit a surface (a hyper-surface in higher dimensions) instead of a curve. The feature transform now includes the powers of each input (and, in general, cross-terms), the weights are estimated by the same least-squares / ridge machinery, and overfitting and regularization behave the same way.

Why it matters: The single-input curve-fitting story scales directly to many inputs — it is the same recipe (features → design matrix → normal equations → optional L2 penalty), just in more dimensions.

Summary of regression

Linear regression, polynomial curve fitting, and ridge regression are all linear models for regression: the prediction is linear in the weights, even when it is nonlinear in the inputs. Polynomial curve fitting handles a single input (curve), polynomial regression handles multiple inputs (surface), the design-matrix / pseudo-inverse solution unifies their training, and L2 regularization (ridge) is the standard guard against overfitting when data is limited.

Why it matters: Seeing the whole family under one umbrella means you learn one solver and one regularizer and apply them everywhere.

Deep-dive practical example

Scenario: predicting atmospheric humidity (\(y\)) from temperature (\(x\)), using a small training set of 15 samples, similar to the humidity illustration in the lecture. Suppose the true relationship between temperature and humidity is gently curved, and we have a test reading at temperature 22.98 with actual humidity 98.76.

Step 1 — Degree \(M = 1\) (straight line). The design matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Phi}\) is \(15 \times 2\). Solving \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \boldsymbol{\Phi})^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \mathbf{y}\) gives \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = [175.27, -3.49]\). Prediction: \(\hat{y} = 175.27 + (-3.49 \times 22.98) = 95.05\). Actual is 98.76, so squared error $= (95.05 - 98.76)^2 = $ about 13.77. A straight line is too rigid; the error is high.

Step 2 — Degree \(M = 2\) (quadratic). Now \(\boldsymbol{\Phi}\) is \(15 \times 3\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = [-197.89, 28.17, -0.67]\). Prediction: \(\hat{y} = -197.89 + 28.17 \times 22.98 - 0.67 \times (22.98)^2 = 96.21\). Squared error drops to about 6.49. The curve bends to follow the data better, and the test error falls. (Note the weights are already getting bigger in magnitude.)

Step 3 — Degree \(M = 3\) (cubic). \(\boldsymbol{\Phi}\) is \(15 \times 4\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = [-7399.2, 945.0, -39.5, 0.5]\). Prediction now lands at about 97.71, very close to the true 98.76, with squared error around 1.1. Training error keeps dropping — but notice the weights have exploded from order \(\sim 100\) (\(M=1\)) to thousands (\(M=3\)). That ballooning is the early warning sign.

Step 4 — Push the degree too far (overfitting). Imagine continuing to \(M = 9\) with only \(\sim 10\) training points (the Bishop sine example). The fitted curve threads through every training point, training error hits 0, but it oscillates violently between points. The weights become enormous, and on a fresh test point the prediction is wildly off — test error shoots up. The model has memorized the 15 points instead of learning the trend. We have crossed from “good fit” into overfitting because \(M\) is now close to \(N\) and there is not enough data to pin down so many parameters.

Step 5 — Apply ridge regularization. Keep the complex model (high \(M\)) but switch the solver to \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \boldsymbol{\Phi} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \mathbf{y}\) and pick a moderate \(\lambda\) (in the lecture’s sine example a value like \(1.5 \times 10^{-8}\) was chosen experimentally). The penalty \(\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2\) pulls the giant weights back down to reasonable magnitudes. The fitted curve stops oscillating and once again tracks the true trend, so test error drops back to a good level — even though we still have only a handful of samples.

Step 6 — Don’t over-regularize. If \(\lambda\) is made very large, the weights are crushed toward zero and the model becomes nearly flat: it fits neither the training data nor the test data, and both errors are high. That is underfitting. The lesson: \(\lambda\) must be tuned experimentally to a moderate value — small enough to fit the data, large enough to keep the weights tame.

Key takeaways

  • Nonlinear regression models the output as a nonlinear function of the inputs; with one input we fit a curve, with several inputs we fit a (hyper-)surface.
  • Polynomial curve fitting uses \(\hat{y} = \sum_j w_j x^j\); it is nonlinear in \(x\) but linear in the weights \(\mathbf{w}\), so it is a “linear model for regression” solved by least squares.
  • The phi-transform turns each input into a vector of its powers; stacking them gives the design (Vandermonde) matrix, and \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \boldsymbol{\Phi})^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \mathbf{y} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}^{+} \mathbf{y}\). The solution needs \(M < N\).
  • \(M = 1\) is exactly simple linear (straight-line) regression — it is a special case of polynomial fitting.
  • Raising degree \(M\) increases model complexity: training error falls, but on small data the test error eventually rises and the weights explode — that is overfitting (memorizing, not generalizing).
  • More data fixes overfitting for a fixed \(M\); but data is usually scarce, so we control complexity instead.
  • Ridge regression adds an L2 penalty: \(E = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum (y - \hat{y})^2 + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2\), giving \(\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \boldsymbol{\Phi} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Phi}^\top \mathbf{y}\). It keeps weights small and restores generalization.
  • \(\lambda\) (0 to 1, excluding 0) is tuned experimentally: too small → overfitting, too large → underfitting; pick a moderate value.
  • The whole regression family — linear, polynomial curve, polynomial surface, ridge — shares one solver (design matrix / pseudo-inverse) and one main guardrail (L2 regularization).

Week 15: Probabilistic Models for Classification — I (Nearest Neighbour, K-NN & Evaluation)

In one line: This week introduces classification as a supervised prediction task and walks through the simplest classifiers — Nearest Neighbour and K-NN — how to prepare data and tune k, how to measure a classifier with confusion matrices and accuracy/precision/recall/F1, and how the reference template (mean, Mahalanobis distance) method points the way toward probabilistic classifiers.

What this week covers

  • Classification overview: identifying the category a new sample belongs to; binary (2-class) vs multi-class; the two-step process (train a classifier, then predict on unseen data); the goal of good generalization.
  • Data preparation: splitting into training set and test set; further splitting training into learning set (build the model) and validation set (tune hyperparameters); balanced vs imbalanced data and how to keep the classifier unbiased.
  • Nearest Neighbour (1-NN): classify a test point by the single closest training example using Euclidean distance; its weakness near class boundaries.
  • K-NN: look at the k closest neighbours and take a majority vote; how to choose k; lazy / instance-based learning and its computational and storage costs.
  • Performance evaluation: confusion matrix; accuracy, precision, recall, F1; the precision-vs-recall trade-off with real examples; extension to multi-class via per-class metrics and their means.
  • Reference template method: represent each class by a template (sample mean) instead of all points; refine with the covariance matrix and Mahalanobis distance; the bridge to probabilistic (Bayes) classifiers.

The topics, explained simply

What classification is

Classification is the problem of identifying which category a new sample belongs to — it predicts a categorical label rather than a number. Predicting whether a person is an adult or a child, whether an email is spam or not, or whether a patient has diabetes are all two-class (binary) problems. Sorting iris flowers into three varieties, recognizing handwritten digits (one of ten), or naming an animal species are multi-class problems (more than two classes).

It is a two-step process. Step 1 builds the classifier — this is the training (learning) phase, and it is supervised, meaning every training sample comes as a pair: an input example \(\mathbf{x}\) and its desired output \(y\) (the class label). The model learns a mapping \(y = f(\mathbf{x})\) that discriminates one class from another. Step 2 uses that model to predict the label of unseen data, in the testing (validation) phase.

The course uses one running example throughout: a person is described by two attributes, height (\(x_1\)) and weight (\(x_2\)), and the task is to predict child (class 0) or adult (class 1).

Why it matters: Accuracy is the percentage of test examples classified correctly, and the real target of any learning technique is good generalization — performing well on data not seen during training, not just memorizing the training set.

Preparing the data: splits and balance

Given a dataset, we set aside part for training and part for testing. The training portion is itself split further: a learning set used to estimate the model’s parameters, and a validation set (set-aside data) used to tune hyperparameters. The test set behaves like real-time data for final prediction. Crucially, the validation and test sets must contain data points never seen during learning.

For balanced data (each class has roughly the same number of samples), a common practice is a 70-30 split: 70% of the samples from each class for training, 30% for testing. Within the 70%, about 60% is learning and 10% is validation. Other splits (50-50, 60-40, 80-20, 90-10) are also used.

For imbalanced data (one class has far more samples than another), blindly applying 70-30 makes the training set lopsided, and statistical models then bias toward the majority class. The fix: find the class with the fewest samples, split it (say 70-30), note how many training samples that yields, then take that same number from every other class. Now each class contributes equally to training, so the model stays unbiased.

Why it matters: A biased split silently makes a classifier “good” only at the majority class. Equalizing per-class training counts is the standard defence, especially for statistical / probabilistic models.

Nearest Neighbour (1-NN) and Euclidean distance

The nearest neighbour classifier is the simplest idea possible: to label a test point, find the single training example closest to it and copy that example’s class. “Closest” is measured by Euclidean distance — for two vectors, the square root of the summed squared differences of their elements.

\[d(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_n)=\sqrt{\sum_j (x_j-x_{n,j})^2}=\lVert \mathbf{x}_n - \mathbf{x} \rVert\]

The algorithm is: (Step 1) compute the Euclidean distance from the test point to every one of the \(N\) training examples; (Step 2) sort the training examples in ascending order of that distance; (Step 3) assign the class of the example with the smallest distance. In the height/weight example, a test person (135 cm, 46.29 kg) has its smallest distance (\(17.01\)) to a training adult, so it is labelled Adult (1).

Why it matters: 1-NN is simple and elegant, but it breaks down at class boundaries. If a test point sits between two classes, its single nearest neighbour might be \(17.01\) away in one class and \(17.49\) in another — almost a tie — and trusting just one neighbour is shaky. That motivates K-NN.

K-Nearest Neighbour (K-NN)

Instead of one neighbour, K-NN looks at the k closest training samples and assigns the class that is most common among them (a majority vote). The first three steps are the same as 1-NN (compute distances, sort ascending, then take the first k examples); the final step is the majority vote over those k neighbours. When \(k = 1\), K-NN is exactly the nearest neighbour method.

In the running example with \(k = 5\), the five nearest neighbours of the test point are 3 adults and 2 children, so the majority — Adult (1) — wins, resolving the near-tie that confused 1-NN.

Choosing k: There is no formula; k is found experimentally. Start at \(k = 1\), measure accuracy on the test/validation data, increment k, repeat, and keep the k that gives the highest accuracy. k should not be too small (\(k = 1\) is too sensitive to noise) nor too large (a huge k blurs the classes). Practitioners prefer k to be an odd number (so a two-class vote cannot tie) and ideally a prime number. k is a hyperparameter, tuned using the validation set.

Lazy / instance-based learning: K-NN does no real work during training — it simply stores all the training data. All computation (distances + sorting) happens at test time. Because it just stores instances, it is called a lazy learner or instance-based learner.

Why it matters: K-NN is simple and powerful, but it has two real costs. (1) It is computationally intensive: every test point requires \(N\) distance computations plus a sort. (2) It needs to store every training example of every class, so large datasets demand large storage. These drawbacks motivate the reference template method below.

Performance evaluation: the confusion matrix

When evaluating a classifier, two outcomes occur: a test example is classified into the correct class, or it is misclassified. The confusion matrix organizes all of these. For two classes, it is a 2x2 table. In this course’s convention, rows are the predicted class and columns are the actual (ground-truth) class. Treating class 1 as the positive class and class 2 as the negative class, the four cells are:

  • True Positive (TP, C11): predicted positive and actually positive — correct.
  • False Positive (FP, C12): predicted positive but actually negative — wrong.
  • False Negative (FN, C21): predicted negative but actually positive — wrong.
  • True Negative (TN, C22): predicted negative and actually negative — correct.

The diagonal (TP, TN) holds correct classifications; the off-diagonal holds errors. Summing a column gives the total actual samples in that class; summing a row gives the total predicted into that class; summing all four cells gives the total number of test examples.

Why it matters: Different libraries (e.g. scikit-learn) may give the confusion matrix transposed — actual on rows, predicted on columns. The reliable trick: whichever side (row or column) sums to the true per-class totals is the actual side; the other is predicted. Always orient yourself before reading off TP/FP/FN/TN.

Accuracy, precision, recall, and F1

Accuracy is the share of test examples classified correctly — the diagonal divided by the total.

\[\text{Accuracy}=\frac{TP+TN}{\text{total number of test samples}}\]

(multiply by 100 for a percentage).

Precision answers: of everything I predicted positive, how much was actually positive? It is also called positive predictive value.

\[\text{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP}\]

Recall answers: of everything that is actually positive, how much did I catch? It is also called sensitivity or true positive rate (TPR).

\[\text{Recall}=\frac{TP}{TP+FN}\]

F1 / F-measure combines precision and recall as their harmonic mean, weighting them evenly.

\[F_1=\frac{2\,\text{Precision}\cdot\text{Recall}}{\text{Precision}+\text{Recall}}\]

The precision-vs-recall trade-off matters because they move in opposite directions: pushing one up tends to pull the other down. Two examples make the choice concrete: - Cancer screening: positive = cancer. A false negative (missing real cancer) is dangerous; a false positive (a false alarm caught at the next screening) is tolerable. So we want false negatives as low as possible — recall should be high. - Biometric access (fingerprint locker): positive = genuine person. A false positive (letting an impostor in) is dangerous; a false negative (rejecting a genuine user) is a tolerable inconvenience. So we want false positives as low as possible — precision should be high.

Why it matters: A high accuracy can hide a useless classifier when classes or error types are imbalanced. Precision, recall, and F1 expose which kind of mistake the model is making, and F1 is handy for picking the best among several classifiers (e.g. K-NN vs neural net vs SVM) that all show similar accuracy.

Multi-class evaluation

With m classes the confusion matrix is m x m. Cell \(C_{ij}\) counts examples predicted as class \(i\) but actually belonging to class \(j\). To define precision/recall per class, treat it as “class i versus the rest”: for class \(i\), \(C_{ii}\) is its true positive, everything in its column (but off its row) summed is its false negative, everything in its row (but off its column) is its false positive, and the rest is true negative.

Accuracy stays simple: the sum of the diagonal (\(C_{11} + C_{22} + C_{33} + \dots\)) divided by the total test samples. Precision, recall, and F1 are computed per class and then averaged: the classifier’s precision is the mean precision across classes, its recall is the mean recall, and its F-measure is the mean F-measure.

Why it matters: In multi-class settings, accuracy is the most common single number, but the mean precision / recall / F1 give a fairer per-class picture when some classes matter more or are harder than others.

Reference template method (mean and Mahalanobis distance)

K-NN’s cost comes from comparing each test point against every stored training example. The reference template method removes that cost by representing each class with one template instead of all its points. The natural template is the sample mean (the average feature vector, the first-order statistic) of each class.

\[\boldsymbol{\mu}_i = \frac{1}{n_i}\sum_{n=1}^{n_i} \mathbf{x}_n\]

Classification then mirrors nearest neighbour, but distances are computed only to the m class means: assign the class whose mean is closest. Now there is genuine learning at training time (estimating each mean), tiny storage (just the means), and very fast testing.

Using the mean alone has a flaw: it ignores the spread of each class. A test point can be numerically closer to a tight class’s mean while really belonging to a wide, spread-out class nearby. The fix is to also estimate each class’s covariance matrix (the second-order statistic capturing variances and covariances — the spread in every direction) and to normalize distance by it. That normalized distance is the Mahalanobis distance.

\[d_M(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\mu}_i)=\sqrt{(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_i)^{\mathsf{T}}\,\Sigma_i^{-1}\,(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_i)}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_i\) and \(\Sigma_i\) are the mean vector and covariance matrix of class \(i\).

The Mahalanobis distance measures the distance between a point and a distribution parameterized by a mean and covariance — which is precisely a Gaussian (normal) distribution. Named after the Indian statistician P. C. Mahalanobis (founder of the Indian Statistical Institute), it is the conceptual bridge to next week’s topic: instead of just “nearest template,” estimate the probability that a sample belongs to each class — the Bayes classifier.

Why it matters: This method shows the progression from a pure distance rule (K-NN) to a statistical model (mean + covariance) and finally to a probabilistic one (Gaussian-based Bayes), each step buying speed and richer use of the data’s structure.

Deep-dive practical example

(A) Classifying a new 2D point with K-NN

We classify a test person x = (height 135, weight 46.29) as Adult (1) or Child (0). Each training row has a class, a height, a weight, and its already-computed Euclidean distance (ED) to x.

Sorting all training examples by ED (ascending), the closest ones are:

Rank Class Height Weight ED
1 1 (Adult) 152 46.80 17.01
2 0 (Child) 121 35.80 17.49
3 1 (Adult) 154 52.60 20.02
4 0 (Child) 116 38.21 20.65
5 1 (Adult) 156 45.30 21.02

How each distance is found — take rank 1 as a worked check: \(\text{ED} = \sqrt{(135 - 152)^2 + (46.29 - 46.80)^2} = \sqrt{(-17)^2 + (-0.51)^2} = \sqrt{289 + 0.26} = \sqrt{289.26} \approx 17.01\). Matches.

k = 1: The single nearest neighbour is rank 1 (ED \(17.01\)), an Adult. But rank 2 (ED \(17.49\)) is a Child and almost as close — this near-tie is exactly the weakness of 1-NN. Prediction at \(k = 1\): Adult (1), but with low confidence.

k = 3: Take the three nearest — Adult, Child, Adult. Votes: Adult = 2, Child = 1. Majority is Adult. Prediction at \(k = 3\): Adult (1).

k = 5 (sanity check): Adult, Child, Adult, Child, Adult. Votes: Adult = 3, Child = 2. Majority Adult. Prediction at \(k = 5\): Adult (1).

Note \(k = 3\) and \(k = 5\) are both odd, so the vote cannot tie — and the larger neighbourhood confirms the decision more reliably than \(k = 1\).

Key takeaways

  • Classification predicts a categorical label; it is supervised and two-step (build the model, then predict on unseen data), and the real goal is generalization, not memorization.
  • Split data into training (further: learning + validation) and test sets; keep per-class counts balanced for training so the classifier is not biased toward a majority class.
  • 1-NN labels a point by its single closest neighbour using Euclidean distance; K-NN uses a majority vote of the k nearest neighbours, fixing 1-NN’s instability at boundaries; \(k = 1\) reduces K-NN to 1-NN.
  • Choose k experimentally to maximize accuracy; keep it odd (avoid vote ties) and ideally prime; k is a hyperparameter tuned on validation data.
  • K-NN is a lazy / instance-based learner: no training computation, but heavy test-time computation and large storage.
  • The confusion matrix (TP, FP, FN, TN) is the foundation: \(\text{Accuracy}=\frac{TP+TN}{\text{total}}\), \(\text{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP}\), \(\text{Recall}=\frac{TP}{TP+FN}\), \(F_1=\) harmonic mean of precision and recall.
  • Precision and recall trade off; pick recall when missing positives is dangerous (cancer screening) and precision when false positives are dangerous (biometric access). F1 balances both and helps pick among similarly-accurate classifiers.
  • For multi-class, accuracy is the diagonal over the total; precision/recall/F1 are computed per class (“class versus rest”) and averaged into mean precision / recall / F-measure.
  • The reference template method replaces all stored points with one template per class (the sample mean), cutting computation and storage; adding the covariance matrix and using Mahalanobis distance accounts for class spread and leads directly to probabilistic (Gaussian / Bayes) classifiers next week.

Week 16: Probabilistic Models for Classification — II (Bayes, Naive Bayes & GMM)

In one line: Build a classifier that assigns a test point to the class with the highest posterior probability, estimating each class’s data distribution from training data — first as a single Gaussian, then as a simplified diagonal-covariance (Naive Bayes) model, and finally as a flexible mixture of Gaussians fitted by the EM algorithm.

What this week covers

  • Bayes classifier: posterior = (likelihood x prior) / evidence; the decision rule that picks the class with maximum posterior probability.
  • Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE): how to estimate the parameters of a class’s distribution from its training data by maximising the (log) total data likelihood.
  • Bayes with unimodal Gaussian density: assume each class is one Gaussian “blob”; the MLE parameters reduce to the sample mean vector and sample covariance matrix (univariate and multivariate forms).
  • Naive Bayes classifier: assume features are conditionally independent, so the class-conditional density becomes a product of per-feature densities — equivalent to a diagonal covariance matrix.
  • Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM): model an arbitrary-shaped, multi-modal class density as a weighted sum of several Gaussian “clusters”.
  • Bayes classifier using GMM: estimate GMM parameters with the EM algorithm (responsibilities = soft cluster assignments), handle the “incomplete data” problem, and choose the number of components k with the elbow method.

The topics, explained simply

The Bayes classifier and its decision rule

Suppose there are \(M\) classes \(c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_M\), and each class has its own pile of training examples. When a new test point \(\mathbf{x}\) arrives, the Bayes classifier asks a simple question for every class: “Given that I saw \(\mathbf{x}\), how probable is it that \(\mathbf{x}\) came from class \(i\)?” That quantity is the posterior probability of the class, \(P(c_i\mid\mathbf{x})\). Whichever class has the highest posterior wins.

Bayes’ rule lets us compute that posterior from three things we can actually estimate from training data.

\[P(c_i\mid \mathbf{x})=\frac{P(\mathbf{x}\mid c_i)\,P(c_i)}{P(\mathbf{x})}\]

The three pieces each have a name and a meaning. The prior \(P(c_i)\) is how common the class is before we even look at \(\mathbf{x}\) — estimated as \(n_i/n\), the fraction of training examples belonging to class \(i\). The likelihood \(P(\mathbf{x}\mid c_i)\), also called the class-conditional density, is how well \(\mathbf{x}\) fits the distribution of class \(i\)’s data. The evidence \(P(\mathbf{x})\) in the denominator is a normaliser, the total probability that \(\mathbf{x}\) exists across all classes; it makes the posteriors sum to 1.

\[P(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{M} P(\mathbf{x}\mid c_i)\,P(c_i) \quad \text{(total probability / evidence)}\]

The final rule just compares posteriors across classes.

\[\text{class label of } \mathbf{x} = \arg\max_{i} P(c_i\mid\mathbf{x}), \quad i = 1, 2, \ldots, M\]

Because \(P(\mathbf{x})\) is the same for all classes, you can ignore it when only comparing — the class with the largest (likelihood x prior) is also the class with the largest posterior.

Why it matters: This single rule is the backbone of every probabilistic classifier this week. If you know the true distribution each class is drawn from, the Bayes classifier is provably the minimum-error classifier — no other classifier can do better. The whole challenge therefore reduces to one thing: estimating the likelihood (the class-conditional density) well.

Maximum likelihood estimation (estimating the likelihood)

The prior is easy (just count samples), so the real work is computing the likelihood \(P(\mathbf{x}\mid c_i)\). To do that we assume class \(i\)’s data comes from some probability distribution with unknown parameter vector \(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i\) (for example, the mean and covariance of a Gaussian). The training phase is about estimating \(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i\) from the training samples, one class at a time, independently of the other classes.

We pick the parameters that make the observed training data most probable. Because samples are assumed independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.), the likelihood of the whole training set is the product of the per-sample likelihoods.

\[L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i) = \prod_{n} P(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_i)\]

Multiplying thousands of tiny probabilities together underflows toward zero, so in practice we maximise the log-likelihood, which turns the product into a sum and is numerically stable. Log is a monotonically increasing function, so it does not change where the maximum is.

\[\log L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i) = \sum_{n} \log P(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}_i)\]

To find the optimum we treat this as an unconstrained optimisation: take the derivative of the log-likelihood with respect to each parameter, set it to the zero vector, and solve.

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_i} \log L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i) = \mathbf{0}\]

Why it matters: MLE is the principled recipe that connects “I have some training data” to “here are the parameters of the distribution I will plug into Bayes’ rule.” It is reused for the single Gaussian, for Naive Bayes, and (via EM) for the GMM.

Bayes with a unimodal Gaussian density

The most common assumption is that each class is a single Gaussian (normal) “blob” — unimodal means one peak, sitting at the centre of the data. For one-dimensional (univariate) data the density is the familiar bell curve defined by a mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\).

\[\mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu, \sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\,\exp\!\left( -\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right)\]

For \(d\)-dimensional (multivariate) data \(\mathbf{x}\), the parameters become a \(d\)-dimensional mean vector \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and a \(d \times d\) covariance matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\). Here \(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i = (\boldsymbol{\mu}_i, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_i)\).

\[\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}\,\sqrt{\det\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}}\,\exp\!\left( -\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \right)\]

When you run MLE on a Gaussian, all that heavy calculus collapses into something delightfully simple: the optimal mean is just the sample mean vector, and the optimal covariance is just the sample covariance matrix. So in practice, “fitting a Gaussian to a class” means computing the average and the spread of that class’s training data. The diagonal entries of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) are the per-feature variances; the off-diagonal entries are covariances that tilt the elliptical contours.

The number of parameters per class grows fast with dimension \(d\): \(d\) means plus \(d(d+1)/2\) unique covariance terms (the matrix is symmetric). For \(d = 2\) that is 5 parameters; for \(d = 100\) it is already 5150.

Why it matters: This is the workhorse “Gaussian Bayes classifier.” It works well when classes really are roughly elliptical blobs (a lot of real-world noise is Gaussian), but it forces an ellipse onto every class whether it fits or not — which motivates the GMM later.

Naive Bayes (conditional independence / diagonal covariance)

Naive Bayes is the Bayes classifier with one extra “naive” assumption: within a class, the features \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_d\) are conditionally independent of each other. That single assumption lets the multivariate class-conditional density factor into a product of one-dimensional densities.

\[P(\mathbf{x} \mid c_i) = \prod_{j=1}^{d} P(x_j \mid c_i)\]

For discrete/categorical features, each factor \(P(x_j \mid c_i)\) is just a count: (number of class-\(i\) examples with that feature value) / (number of class-\(i\) examples). For continuous features, each factor is modelled as a univariate Gaussian, so you only need the mean and variance of each feature, separately.

Geometrically, dropping all the cross-feature dependencies is exactly the same as forcing the covariance matrix to be diagonal (all off-diagonal covariances \(= 0\)). So Naive Bayes is just the Gaussian Bayes classifier with a diagonal \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\). Parameter count drops from \(d + d(d+1)/2\) down to only \(d\) means + \(d\) variances.

Why it matters: It is “naive” but cheap and robust: far fewer parameters to estimate, so it works even with limited data and high dimensionality. The rest of the pipeline (compute likelihood, multiply by prior, take argmax) is identical to the full Bayes classifier — only the covariance structure changes.

Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)

Real data is often not a single neat ellipse — a class can have multiple modes (several bumps) or an arbitrary shape. Forcing one Gaussian onto such data puts the mean in the wrong place and hurts accuracy. The fix is to model the class density as a weighted sum of several Gaussians, each Gaussian covering one cluster of the class’s data.

\[P(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)\]

Here \(K\) is the number of components (clusters), each with its own mean \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k\), covariance \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k\), and mixing weight (mixture coefficient) \(w_k\). The weights say how much each Gaussian contributes, and they must form a valid distribution.

\[\sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k = 1, \quad \text{with each } w_k \geq 0\]

By combining a few simple Gaussians, a GMM can approximate almost any complex, arbitrary-shaped density — which is exactly what the Bayes classifier needs to approach minimum error.

Why it matters: GMM is a powerful, general density model. A Bayes classifier built on GMMs typically beats one built on a single Gaussian, because it approximates the true class-conditional density far more closely. It is the standard tool in tasks like speaker/language recognition (often with 1024 or 2048 components).

Bayes classifier using GMM (EM, responsibilities, choosing k)

To use a GMM we must estimate its parameters (all the \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k\), \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k\), and \(w_k\)) from a class’s training data. The catch: if we knew which sample belonged to which cluster, we could just run MLE per cluster. But we do not — the cluster identity of each point is hidden (latent). This is the incomplete data problem, a chicken-and-egg situation: we need cluster assignments to estimate parameters, and we need parameters to make assignments.

The Expectation-Maximisation (EM) algorithm breaks this loop by iterating two steps after initialising the means (e.g. random samples or k-means centres), covariances (identity matrices), and weights (\(1/K\) each):

  • E-step (Expectation): with the current parameters, softly assign each point to clusters by computing its responsibility \(\gamma(z_{nk})\) — the posterior probability that component \(k\) generated sample \(\mathbf{x}_n\).

\[\gamma(z_{nk}) = \frac{w_k\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} w_j\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_j, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)}\]

Responsibilities for a point sum to 1 across clusters (e.g. a point might be 0.99 in cluster 1, 0.01 in cluster 2). Here \(w_k\) acts like a prior over clusters — the formula is literally Bayes’ rule applied to clusters instead of classes.

  • M-step (Maximisation): use the responsibilities as soft counts to re-estimate the parameters — weighted means, weighted covariances, and \(w_k = (\text{sum of responsibilities for cluster } k) / (\text{total samples})\).

Repeat E and M until the total log-likelihood stops improving. Each iteration is guaranteed not to decrease the likelihood.

Choosing K: build GMMs for several values of \(K\) and plot the resulting total log-likelihood. It keeps rising as \(K\) grows, but the gains flatten after a point. Pick \(K\) at the “elbow” — where the curve bends from steep to flat (the elbow method, a heuristic).

Why it matters: EM is how you actually train a GMM in the absence of labels for clusters. But beware the parameter explosion: total parameters scale with both \(d\) and \(K\), and a rule of thumb is to have at least 10x as many training samples as parameters for reliable estimates. With too few samples, complex GMMs over-fit or fail to estimate meaningfully.

Deep-dive practical example

Task: Classify an email as Spam or Not-Spam (Ham) using Naive Bayes with three binary features: - F1 = contains the word “free” - F2 = contains the word “winner” - F3 = contains a suspicious link

Step 1 — Priors from the training set. Out of 100 training emails, 40 are spam and 60 are ham. - \(P(\text{Spam}) = 40/100 = 0.40\) - \(P(\text{Ham}) = 60/100 = 0.60\)

Step 2 — Per-feature likelihoods (counts within each class). From the training emails:

Feature present \(P(\text{feature}\mid\text{Spam})\) \(P(\text{feature}\mid\text{Ham})\)
“free” (F1) 0.80 0.10
“winner” (F2) 0.60 0.05
“link” (F3) 0.70 0.20

Step 3 — The incoming test email. It contains “free” and a suspicious link, but NOT “winner”. So F1 = present, F2 = absent, F3 = present. For the absent feature we use \(1 - P(\text{present})\): - \(P(\text{no "winner"} \mid \text{Spam}) = 1 - 0.60 = 0.40\) - \(P(\text{no "winner"} \mid \text{Ham}) = 1 - 0.05 = 0.95\)

Step 4 — Class-conditional likelihood = product of per-feature factors (Naive Bayes independence).

For Spam: \[P(\mathbf{x} \mid \text{Spam}) = 0.80 \times 0.40 \times 0.70 = 0.224\]

For Ham: \[P(\mathbf{x} \mid \text{Ham}) = 0.10 \times 0.95 \times 0.20 = 0.019\]

Step 5 — Multiply by the prior (numerator of Bayes’ rule).

\[\text{Spam score} = P(\mathbf{x} \mid \text{Spam}) \times P(\text{Spam}) = 0.224 \times 0.40 = 0.0896\] \[\text{Ham score} = P(\mathbf{x} \mid \text{Ham}) \times P(\text{Ham}) = 0.019 \times 0.60 = 0.0114\]

Step 6 — Normalise by the evidence to get posteriors.

\[P(\mathbf{x}) = 0.0896 + 0.0114 = 0.1010\]

  • \(P(\text{Spam} \mid \mathbf{x}) = 0.0896 / 0.1010 = 0.887\)
  • \(P(\text{Ham} \mid \mathbf{x}) = 0.0114 / 0.1010 = 0.113\)

Step 7 — Decision (argmax). \(P(\text{Spam} \mid \mathbf{x}) = 0.887 > P(\text{Ham} \mid \mathbf{x}) = 0.113\), so the email is classified as Spam (with about 89% confidence).

Notice that even the unnormalised scores (0.0896 vs 0.0114) already tell us the answer — the evidence \(P(\mathbf{x})\) only rescales them so the two posteriors add to 1. This mirrors the Gaussian example from the lecture, where an adult-vs-child test point had likelihood \(\sim 3.5 \times 10^{-8}\) for the child class but a far larger likelihood for the adult class, giving \(P(\text{adult}\mid\mathbf{x}) = 0.99\).

Key takeaways

  • The Bayes classifier picks the class with the highest posterior: \(P(c_i\mid\mathbf{x}) = \frac{P(\mathbf{x}\mid c_i)\,P(c_i)}{P(\mathbf{x})}\), and decides by argmax over classes; \(P(\mathbf{x})\) is just a normaliser you can drop when comparing.
  • The three ingredients are prior (count fraction \(n_i/n\)), likelihood / class-conditional density (depends on the assumed distribution), and evidence (sum of likelihood x prior over all classes).
  • If the assumed distribution matches the true one, the Bayes classifier is the minimum-error classifier — so estimating the likelihood well is the whole game.
  • Parameters of a class’s distribution are learned by maximum likelihood; using the log-likelihood turns products into sums and avoids numerical underflow.
  • For a unimodal Gaussian, MLE reduces to simply computing the sample mean vector and sample covariance matrix per class.
  • Naive Bayes assumes conditionally independent features, factoring the likelihood into a product of 1-D densities — equivalent to a diagonal covariance matrix, with far fewer parameters (\(d\) means + \(d\) variances).
  • A GMM models each class density as a weighted sum of \(K\) Gaussians (weights sum to 1), letting it fit arbitrary, multi-modal shapes.
  • GMM parameters are fitted by the EM algorithm, which solves the incomplete-data problem by alternating soft assignments (E-step responsibilities, a Bayes rule over clusters) and re-estimation (M-step).
  • Choose the number of components \(K\) with the elbow method on the log-likelihood curve, and ensure enough data (roughly 10x as many samples as parameters) since parameter count grows with both \(d\) and \(K\).

Week 17: Support Vector Machines — I (Discriminant Functions & Perceptron)

In one line: Classification is really about learning a discriminant function whose sign tells you the class, and this week builds the foundation — what discriminant functions are, how they hide inside Bayes/kNN/GMM classifiers, and how the Perceptron learns a linear one directly from data.

What this week covers

  • Classification reframed as learning a discriminant function — the mapping \(y = f(\mathbf{x})\) learned during training that discriminates the region between classes and whose shape defines the decision boundary.
  • The two big families: non-discriminative learning (model a discriminant function per class, independent of other classes) vs discriminative learning (learn one boundary directly from the data of two or more classes).
  • The discriminant function inside the Gaussian Bayes classifier, and how the covariance matrix decides whether the boundary is linear or quadratic (three cases: same covariance + same variance, same covariance, arbitrary covariance).
  • Boundary shapes that kNN, Bayes (unimodal Gaussian), and Bayes (GMM) implicitly learn — piecewise/smooth non-linear, at-most-quadratic, and any-complex-non-linear respectively.
  • The linear discriminant function \(g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0\), its positive and negative sides, and the augmented-vector form.
  • The Perceptron learning algorithm: update rule, learning rate (\(\eta\)), epochs, the convergence theorem, and its two key limitations.

The topics, explained simply

What a discriminant function is

During training a classifier learns a mapping from the input vector to its class label. That learned mapping is called the discriminant function — literally, the function that discriminates (separates) the regions of the classes. Everything on one side of it is one class; everything on the other side is the other class.

The shape this function takes is the decision boundary. It can be linear (a line in 2D, a plane in 3D, a hyperplane in higher dimensions) or non-linear (a curve or surface).

\[g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0\]

Why it matters: Every classifier you have seen — kNN, Bayes — is secretly learning a discriminant function, even if it never wrote one down. Naming it lets you compare classifiers by the shape of boundary they can produce.

Two families: non-discriminative vs discriminative learning

There are two ways to get a discriminant function.

Non-discriminative (modelling) learning: define a separate discriminant function \(g_i(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i)\) for each class \(C_i\), built only from that class’s own data, independent of the others. At test time you assign \(\mathbf{x}\) to whichever class gives the highest discriminant value.

\[\text{Class label for } \mathbf{x} = \arg\max_i \; g_i(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i), \quad i = 1, 2, \ldots, M\]

Examples: kNN (\(g_i\) = Euclidean distance from \(\mathbf{x}\) to class \(i\)’s training data) and Bayes classifier (\(g_i\) = posterior probability of class \(i\)). These never learn a boundary directly; the boundary only appears when you test.

Discriminative learning: learn the separating surface directly from the data of at least two classes — you place the hyperplane (or curved surface) in the region of separation. Examples: Perceptron and SVM (linear), neural networks (non-linear).

Why it matters: This is the conceptual hinge of the whole week. SVM and the Perceptron belong to the second family — they go straight after the boundary instead of modelling each class separately.

The discriminant function inside the Gaussian Bayes classifier

For a Bayes classifier where each class is a multivariate unimodal Gaussian, the discriminant function for class \(i\) is its posterior probability. Since the denominator \(P(\mathbf{x})\) of Bayes’ rule is common to all classes, you keep only the numerator (likelihood × prior) and take the log:

\[g_i(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i) = -\tfrac12 (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i)^\top \Sigma_i^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i) - \tfrac12\ln(2\pi) - \tfrac12\ln(|\Sigma_i|) + \ln(P(C_i))\]

The boundary between two classes \(i\) and \(j\) is where their discriminant functions are equal:

\[g(\mathbf{x}) = g_i(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i) - g_j(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}_j) = 0\]

When you expand that subtraction, you get a general expression of the form \(-\tfrac12 \mathbf{x}^\top W \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0 = 0\). Whether the quadratic term \(\mathbf{x}^\top W \mathbf{x}\) survives depends entirely on the covariance matrices.

Why it matters: The covariance matrix decides the boundary shape. Same covariance for both classes kills the quadratic term, leaving a line; different covariances keep it, giving a curve.

The three covariance cases (what decides linear vs quadratic)

Case 1 — \(\Sigma_i = \sigma^2 I\) (isotropic, same for all classes): features are independent and every variance is equal, so the density contours are perfect circles. The boundary is linear and is the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the two class centres. (If the priors are equal, it sits exactly in the middle.)

Case 2 — \(\Sigma_i = \Sigma\) (same covariance for both, but variances not all equal / full matrix): contours are tilted ellipses of the same shape. The boundary is still linear (a hyperplane) but is no longer the perpendicular bisector — it cuts the centre-joining line at an angle.

Case 3 — \(\Sigma_i\) arbitrary (different for each class): boundary becomes quadratic (hyper-quadratic in higher dimensions) — parabola, hyperbola, circle, ellipse, or pair of lines in 2D. If the covariances are diagonal, the boundary’s axes are parallel to the coordinate axes; if full, they are tilted.

Why it matters: Same covariance ⇒ linear boundary. Different covariance ⇒ at-most-quadratic boundary. This is the single most testable fact of the Bayes half of the week. The unimodal Gaussian Bayes classifier’s limitation is that it can never produce a boundary higher than second order.

Boundary shapes of kNN, Bayes, and GMM

  • kNN implicitly learns a non-linear boundary. For each test point, the boundary between its two nearest opposite-class neighbours is the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining them; stitching these together gives a piecewise-linear (\(k=1\)) boundary that smooths into a clean non-linear curve as \(k\) grows.
  • Bayes + unimodal Gaussian learns at most a quadratic boundary (see the three cases above).
  • Bayes + GMM (multimodal Gaussian) represents each class as a sum of several Gaussians. Each Gaussian pair contributes a quadratic piece, and stitching them together approximates any complex non-linear boundary — so GMM can separate shapes (e.g. interleaving moons) that a single Gaussian cannot.

Why it matters: This gives a clean ranking of expressive power: kNN (non-linear) and GMM (arbitrary non-linear) are flexible; unimodal Gaussian Bayes is capped at quadratic. It also motivates why we move to discriminative methods like SVM.

The linear discriminant function and its two sides

In 2D, the linear discriminant function is a line: \(w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + w_0 = 0\). Rearranging into \(x_2 = m x_1 + c\) shows the weights are the line’s geometry: slope \(m = -w_1/w_2\) and y-intercept \(c = -w_0/w_2\).

In \(d\) dimensions it becomes a hyperplane. Augmenting the input with a constant \(x_0 = 1\) lets you fold \(w_0\) into the weight vector and write it as a single dot product:

\[g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \sum_{i=0}^{d} w_i x_i, \quad \text{with } x_0 = 1 \quad \left(\text{equivalently } g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0\right)\]

A hyperplane splits space into a positive side (where \(g(\mathbf{x}) > 0\)) and a negative side (where \(g(\mathbf{x}) < 0\)); points on it give \(g(\mathbf{x}) = 0\).

Why it matters: This sign rule is the classifier. Compute \(g(\mathbf{x})\); positive ⇒ class \(+1\), negative ⇒ class \(-1\). Training labels are set as \(y_n = +1\) for the positive side and \(y_n = -1\) for the negative side.

The Perceptron learning algorithm

The Perceptron is the simplest discriminative linear classifier — a binary classifier that learns the hyperplane directly from data. Its one unrealistic assumption: the two classes are linearly separable.

It learns by adaptation (error correction). Start with some initial weight vector \(\mathbf{w}\). Pick a training example, check it, and if it is misclassified, nudge \(\mathbf{w}\) toward fixing it:

  • A positive example (\(y_n = +1\)) wrongly scored \(\mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}} \le 0\): add to the weights.
  • A negative example (\(y_n = -1\)) wrongly scored \(\mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}} > 0\): subtract from the weights.

\[\text{(update for a missed } +1\text{): } \mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} + \eta\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}\] \[\text{(update for a missed } -1\text{): } \mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}\]

Here \(\eta\) is the learning rate, \(0 < \eta \le 1\) (it must be strictly positive — \(\eta = 0\) means no update ever happens). Large \(\eta\) moves fast but can oscillate between the classes before settling; small \(\eta\) converges slowly. A common heuristic: shrink \(\eta\) each epoch, e.g. \(\eta = 1/m\) at epoch \(m\) (1, then 0.5, then 0.33, …), but floor it (e.g. don’t let it drop below \(0.0001\)) so updates never stop.

An epoch = presenting all training examples once. Each epoch you reset the misclassification count, sweep every example, update on each mistake, and count the mistakes. You repeat epochs until the convergence criterion: an epoch with zero misclassifications. At that point \(\mathbf{w}\) defines the separating hyperplane.

\[\text{(test-time rule): assign } +1 \text{ if } \mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}} > 0, \text{ else assign } -1\]

Perceptron Convergence Theorem: if the classes are linearly separable, the algorithm is guaranteed to reach a solution in a finite number of steps.

Limitations (the motivation for SVM): 1. It fails on non-separable / overlapping data — it never converges. 2. Even when data is separable, the learned hyperplane is not optimal — it stops the instant training error hits zero, so it can sit right against one class and misclassify nearby unseen test points. The ideal hyperplane sits in the middle of the gap for best generalization. Getting that optimal middle hyperplane is exactly what the SVM delivers — the bridge to next week.

Deep-dive practical example

(A) Classifying 2D points by the sign of g(x)

Take the line \(g(\mathbf{x}) = x_1 + x_2 - 1\). Here \(w_1 = 1\), \(w_2 = 1\), \(w_0 = -1\). - Slope \(m = -w_1/w_2 = -1\); y-intercept \(c = -w_0/w_2 = -(-1)/1 = +1\). So the line passes through \((1, 0)\) and \((0, 1)\).

Now read the sign of \(g(\mathbf{x})\) for several points:

Point (x1, x2) g(x) = x1 + x2 - 1 Sign Verdict
(1, 0) 1 + 0 - 1 = 0 0 on the line (boundary)
(0, 1) 0 + 1 - 1 = 0 0 on the line (boundary)
(1, 1) 1 + 1 - 1 = +1 + positive side ⇒ class +1
(0, 0) 0 + 0 - 1 = -1 - negative side ⇒ class -1

So \((1,1)\) lies on the positive side and \((0,0)\) on the negative side — and the two points \((1,0)\) and \((0,1)\) confirm where the line actually is. The single computation \(g(\mathbf{x})\) plus its sign is the entire classification step.

(B) Perceptron weight updates, step by step (eta = 1)

Suppose we have two 1D points (made 2D by augmenting with \(x_0 = 1\), so \(\hat{\mathbf{x}} = [1, x]^\top\)): - Point A: \(x = 2\), true label +1, so \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_A = [1, 2]^\top\). - Point B: \(x = -1\), true label -1, so \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_B = [1, -1]^\top\).

Start with weights \(\mathbf{w} = [0, 0]^\top\) and \(\eta = 1\).

Epoch 1, example A: score \(\mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}}_A = 0\cdot 1 + 0\cdot 2 = 0\). This is not \(> 0\), so the \(+1\) example is misclassified. Update (missed \(+1\), add): \(\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} + \eta\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}_A = [0, 0]^\top + 1\cdot[1, 2]^\top = [1, 2]^\top\). (misclassification count = 1)

Epoch 1, example B: score \(\mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}}_B = 1\cdot 1 + 2\cdot(-1) = 1 - 2 = -1\). This is \(\le 0\), which is correct for a \(-1\) example. No update. (count stays 1)

End of epoch 1: 1 misclassification ⇒ not converged, reset count, run epoch 2.

Epoch 2, example A: score \(\mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}}_A = 1\cdot 1 + 2\cdot 2 = 1 + 4 = 5 > 0\). Correct (it’s \(+1\)). No update.

Epoch 2, example B: score \(\mathbf{w}^\top\hat{\mathbf{x}}_B = 1\cdot 1 + 2\cdot(-1) = -1 \le 0\). Correct (it’s \(-1\)). No update.

End of epoch 2: zero misclassifications ⇒ converged. Final weights \(\mathbf{w} = [1, 2]^\top\), i.e. boundary \(g(\mathbf{x}) = 1 + 2x = 0\), which sits at \(x = -0.5\) — neatly between A (\(x=2\), \(+1\) side) and B (\(x=-1\), \(-1\) side). Notice it converged in a finite number of steps exactly because the two points are linearly separable — the convergence theorem in action.

Key takeaways

  • A classifier’s job is to learn a discriminant function; its shape is the decision boundary, and you classify by reading the sign of \(g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0\).
  • Non-discriminative methods (kNN, Bayes) model one function per class independently and only reveal their boundary at test time; discriminative methods (Perceptron, SVM, neural nets) learn the boundary directly.
  • In Gaussian Bayes, the covariance matrix decides boundary shape: same covariance ⇒ linear (perpendicular bisector only when variances are also equal); different covariance ⇒ quadratic / hyper-quadratic.
  • Expressive-power ranking of implicit boundaries: kNN (non-linear) ≈ GMM Bayes (any complex non-linear) > unimodal Gaussian Bayes (capped at quadratic).
  • The Perceptron updates \(\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} + \eta\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) for a missed \(+1\) and \(\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) for a missed \(-1\), sweeping the data in epochs until an epoch has zero mistakes; \(0 < \eta \le 1\).
  • The Convergence Theorem guarantees a solution in finite steps if the data is linearly separable.
  • Two Perceptron limitations — fails on non-separable data and yields a non-optimal (non-maximal-margin) hyperplane — are the direct motivation for the Support Vector Machine.

Week 18: Support Vector Machines — II (Margins, Kernels & Multiclass)

In one line: SVM finds the unique separating boundary that sits exactly in the middle of the gap between two classes (the maximum-margin hyperplane), then extends to messy and curved data using slack variables and the kernel trick, and to many classes via one-vs-rest or one-vs-one.

What this week covers

  • Maximum-margin hyperplane — Case 1 (hard margin, linearly separable): the primal problem, its dual, support vectors, and the KKT condition.
  • Case 2 (linearly non-separable, soft margin): slack variables (zeta), hinge loss, the trade-off parameter C, and how the dual changes (alpha bounded by C).
  • Case 3 (non-linear): the kernel trick, Cover’s theorem, and learning a linear boundary in a transformed space that is non-linear in the input space.
  • Kernel functions (linear, polynomial, Gaussian/RBF), Mercer’s theorem, the kernel Gram matrix (symmetric + positive semi-definite), and kernel parameters as hyperparameters.

  • Why SVM is free from the curse of dimensionality (parameters scale with number of training samples, not data dimension).

The topics, explained simply

Optimal separating hyperplane and the margin

When two classes are linearly separable, there are infinitely many lines (hyperplanes) that sit in the gap between them and classify the training data correctly. They are not equally good. The perceptron stops at any one of them, even one that hugs one class closely. SVM instead looks for the single best one: the hyperplane that sits exactly in the middle of the gap, equidistant from the nearest points of both classes. This is the “optimal” separating hyperplane, because a line in the middle leaves the most room on both sides, so more unseen test points fall on the correct side and get classified correctly (better generalization).

To make “middle” precise we need a notion of distance. The discriminant (decision) function of a hyperplane is \(g(\mathbf{x})\), and the distance of any point \(\mathbf{x}\) to the hyperplane is the magnitude of \(g(\mathbf{x})\) divided by the length of the weight vector \(\mathbf{w}\).

\[g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0\] \[\text{distance of } \mathbf{x} \text{ to hyperplane} = \frac{|g(\mathbf{x})|}{\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert}\]

The margin of a hyperplane is then defined as the distance of the nearest training example to it. Maximizing this margin is the whole game.

Why it matters: A bigger margin is a buffer zone. A boundary jammed against one class misclassifies the first slightly-shifted test point; a centered, wide-margin boundary tolerates noise and generalizes better.

Maximum-margin hyperplane — Case 1: hard margin (linearly separable)

We can freely rescale \(\mathbf{w}\) and \(w_0\) without moving the actual boundary (only the magnitude changes, not the direction). We pick the scaling so that the nearest points satisfy \(|g(\mathbf{x})| = 1\). This is called the canonical separating hyperplane. With that convention, the two margin lines become \(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0 = +1\) (positive side) and \(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0 = -1\) (negative side), and the full width between them is \(\frac{2}{\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert}\).

\[\text{margin width} = \frac{2}{\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert}\]

Every training point must lie on its own side of the gap. Using class labels \(y_n \in \{+1, -1\}\), this single constraint captures both sides at once:

\[y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + w_0) \ge 1, \quad \text{for all } n = 1, 2, \ldots, N\]

Maximizing \(\frac{2}{\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert}\) is the same as minimizing \(\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert\) (reciprocal), which is the same as minimizing \(\tfrac12\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2\) (squaring removes the square root and the \(\tfrac12\) makes the derivative clean). So the learning problem is:

\[\min_{\mathbf{w}, w_0} J(\mathbf{w}, w_0) = \tfrac12\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + w_0) \ge 1 \text{ for all } n\]

This is a constrained optimization, so we use a Lagrangian. Each constraint gets a Lagrange multiplier \(\alpha_n\) (always \(\ge 0\)). Setting the derivatives with respect to \(\mathbf{w}\) and \(w_0\) to zero gives two key results: \(\mathbf{w}\) is a weighted sum of the data, and the alphas are balanced across the two classes.

\[\mathbf{w} = \sum_n \alpha_n y_n \mathbf{x}_n\] \[\sum_n \alpha_n y_n = 0\]

Substituting these back turns the problem into the dual (a maximization in the alphas only):

\[\max_{\alpha} J(\alpha) = \sum_n \alpha_n - \tfrac12 \sum_m \sum_n \alpha_m \alpha_n y_m y_n (\mathbf{x}_m^\top\mathbf{x}_n), \quad \text{with } \alpha_n \ge 0 \text{ and } \sum_n \alpha_n y_n = 0\]

This is a quadratic optimization problem, solved in practice with Sequential Minimal Optimization (SMO).

Why it matters: The dual depends on the data only through dot products \(\mathbf{x}_m^\top\mathbf{x}_n\). This single fact is what later lets us swap in a kernel and handle curved boundaries without ever leaving this framework.

Support vectors and the KKT condition

After solving the dual, most alpha values come out as zero; only a few are non-zero. The training points with non-zero alphas are the support vectors — they are exactly the points sitting on the margin lines. They are the only points that matter; the rest can be thrown away.

This is enforced by the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) condition:

\[\alpha_n \left[ y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + w_0) - 1 \right] = 0, \quad \text{for all } n\]

Read it as a product that must equal zero. If \(\alpha_n\) is non-zero, the bracket must be zero, meaning \(y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + w_0) = 1\) — the point is exactly on the margin (a support vector). If the point is strictly past the margin (the bracket is positive), then \(\alpha_n\) must be zero. The optimal weight vector is therefore a sum over support vectors only:

\[\mathbf{w} = \sum_{\text{support vectors}} \alpha_n y_n \mathbf{x}_n\]

At test time, compute \(g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + w_0\); assign \(\mathbf{x}\) to class \(C_1\) if \(g(\mathbf{x}) \ge 0\), else \(C_2\).

Why it matters: The model is defined by a handful of border points, which makes it compact and is the reason SVM is so memory- and computation-light at prediction time.

Case 2: soft margin (linearly non-separable)

Real data overlap. A few points sit inside the gap or on the wrong side of the boundary. A hard margin is impossible, so we allow controlled violations using a slack variable \(\zeta_n\) per point that measures how far a point deviates from where it “should” be. Points on or beyond their correct margin have \(\zeta = 0\); points that merely cross into the gap have \(0 < \zeta < 1\); misclassified points have \(\zeta > 1\). Slack is computed by the hinge loss:

\[\zeta_n = \max(0,\, 1 - y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + w_0))\]

The constraint relaxes from “\(\ge 1\)” to “\(\ge 1 - \zeta_n\)”, and we add the total slack to the objective, weighted by a trade-off parameter C:

\[\min_{\mathbf{w}, w_0, \zeta} J(\mathbf{w}, w_0, \zeta) = \tfrac12\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2 + C \sum_n \zeta_n, \quad \text{subject to} \quad y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + w_0) \ge 1 - \zeta_n \text{ and } \zeta_n \ge 0\]

C is a user-chosen hyperparameter balancing a wide margin against few errors. Small C: errors weighted lightly, so the margin can be wide but more points are allowed to violate it. Large C: errors weighted heavily, so the model fits training points tightly with a narrower margin. The margin you get here is called a soft margin.

The dual is identical to Case 1 except the multipliers are now bounded above by C:

\[0 \le \alpha_n \le C\]

The KKT condition gains a second part for the slack multipliers. Support vectors are now the points on the margin plus all points causing error (margin violations and misclassifications).

Why it matters: Soft margin is what makes SVM usable on real, noisy data; C is one of the two knobs (with the kernel parameter) you actually tune in practice.

Case 3: non-linear data and the kernel trick

When classes are separated by a curved (e.g. circular or elliptical) boundary, no straight line works in the input space. The idea: map the data through a non-linear function \(\phi\) into a much higher-dimensional feature space. Cover’s theorem says that data which is non-linearly separable in the low-dimensional input space tends to become linearly separable (or close to it) in a high-enough feature space. So we map up, run the same linear SVM there, and the linear boundary we learn there corresponds to a curved boundary back in the input space.

The catch: we don’t know which \(\phi\) to use, and the feature space can be enormous (even infinite). The escape is the kernel trick. Recall the SVM only ever needs dot products between examples. A kernel function \(K\) computes, directly from the original input vectors, a value equal to the dot product of their images in feature space — without ever building \(\phi\) explicitly.

\[K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n) = \phi(\mathbf{x}_m)^\top \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)\]

We just replace every dot product \(\mathbf{x}_m^\top\mathbf{x}_n\) in the dual (and in the test-time discriminant) with \(K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n)\). The test discriminant becomes a sum of kernels between the test point and each support vector:

\[g(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\text{support vectors}} \alpha_n y_n K(\mathbf{x}_n, \mathbf{x}) + w_0\]

Why it matters: The kernel trick lets SVM learn arbitrarily complex non-linear boundaries at the cost of linear math, never paying for the high-dimensional space directly. This is the single most important idea in the week.

Kernel functions and Mercer’s theorem

A kernel acts as a similarity measure between a pair of examples. To be a valid kernel (to genuinely correspond to some dot product in a feature space) it must satisfy Mercer’s theorem. A practical, checkable sufficient condition: form the kernel Gram matrix \(K\) (the \(N\)-by-\(N\) matrix of \(K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n)\) over all training pairs). It must be (a) symmetric — \(K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n) = K(\mathbf{x}_n, \mathbf{x}_m)\) — and (b) positive semi-definite — its eigenvalues are all non-negative. PSD-ness is what guarantees the quadratic optimization converges.

Common kernels: - Linear kernel: \(K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n) = \mathbf{x}_m^\top\mathbf{x}_n\). Just the input-space dot product; gives a linear boundary (no real transformation). - Polynomial kernel: \[K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n) = (a\,\mathbf{x}_m^\top\mathbf{x}_n + b)^p\] where \(a\) is a multiplicative coefficient, \(b\) an additive coefficient, \(p\) the polynomial degree. Implicitly maps to a feature space of all monomials up to degree \(p\). - Gaussian / RBF kernel: \[K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{\lVert\mathbf{x}_m - \mathbf{x}_n\rVert^2}{\sigma}\right)\] where \(\sigma\) is the width parameter. It resembles a Gaussian centered at \(\mathbf{x}_n\); because it contains polynomial terms of all degrees, its feature space is effectively infinite-dimensional.

The kernel choice and its parameters (\(a\), \(b\), \(p\), or \(\sigma\)) are hyperparameters tuned experimentally.

Why it matters: RBF is the default workhorse: an infinite-dimensional feature space means almost any data becomes linearly separable there, so a well-tuned RBF SVM gives near-optimal non-linear boundaries.

Multiclass classification with SVM

SVM is fundamentally a binary classifier, so a C-class problem is decomposed into several binary problems whose outputs are combined.

  • One-vs-Rest (OvR): Build one SVM per class — that class as positive, all other classes lumped as negative. Number of SVMs \(= C\). A test point is run through all of them and the class whose SVM gives the highest discriminant value wins (winner-takes-all).
  • One-vs-One (OvO): Build one SVM for every pair of classes. Number of SVMs \(= \frac{C(C-1)}{2}\). Each SVM votes for one of its two classes; the class with the most votes (majority/max-wins voting) is the prediction.

Both usually give comparable accuracy. The interesting trade-off appears with many classes. Suppose 1000 classes, 100 samples each. OvR builds only 1000 SVMs, but each is trained on all 100,000 samples and is badly class-imbalanced (100 positive vs ~99,900 negative). OvO builds ~500,000 SVMs, but each trains on just 200 balanced samples, so each optimization is small and fast. With many classes, OvO is often faster overall and easier on the optimizer despite building far more SVMs. With few classes it doesn’t matter. (Library defaults differ: scikit-learn and LIBSVM default to OvO; LIBLINEAR, SVM-light, SVM-torch default to OvR.)

Why it matters: Knowing the per-SVM training size and balance — not just the SVM count — is the real basis for choosing OvO vs OvR.

Why SVM is free from the curse of dimensionality

For most classifiers, more input dimensions means more parameters to estimate, and without enough samples the model becomes suboptimal. SVM’s parameters are the Lagrange multipliers \(\alpha\), which are tied to training samples, not to data dimensions. Even when an RBF kernel works in an infinite-dimensional space, the number of parameters equals the number of training examples. The minimum to build an SVM is two points (one per class).

Why it matters: This is why SVM trains reliably even in very high-dimensional spaces (like the kernel feature space) where other classifiers would need impossibly many samples.

Deep-dive practical example

(A) A 1-D / 2-D maximum-margin computation with support vectors and margin width

Take a simple 1-D problem along a single axis. Class \(-1\) has a point at \(x = 1\); class \(+1\) has a point at \(x = 3\). By symmetry the optimal boundary sits exactly in the middle, at \(x = 2\), which is equidistant (distance 1) from both points. Both points are the nearest examples, so both are support vectors, and the margin (nearest-point distance) is 1, giving a full margin width of 2.

Now derive the canonical SVM weights. In 1-D, \(g(x) = w x + w_0\). We need the support vectors to sit on the margin lines, i.e. \(g = +1\) at \(x = 3\) and \(g = -1\) at \(x = 1\): - \(w \cdot 3 + w_0 = +1\) - \(w \cdot 1 + w_0 = -1\)

Subtracting: \(2w = 2\), so \(w = 1\). Then \(w_0 = -1 - w \cdot 1 = -2\). Check the margin width: \(\frac{2}{\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert} = \frac{2}{1} = 2\) — exactly the gap we expected. The boundary is at \(g(x) = x - 2 = 0\), i.e. \(x = 2\).

Verify the labels: at \(x = 3\), \(g = 1 > 0 \to\) class \(+1\) (correct); at \(x = 1\), \(g = -1 < 0 \to\) class \(-1\) (correct). A new test point at \(x = 2.4\) gives \(g = 0.4 > 0 \to\) class \(+1\), comfortably inside the margin’s correct side. This is the entire SVM idea in miniature: only the two border points define everything, and the boundary is centered for maximum buffer.

(B) Kernel trick: mapping circular (non-separable) data into a higher dimension

Consider 2-D data where one class sits inside a circle and the other outside — say inner-class points near the origin like \((0, 0)\) and \((0.5, 0.5)\), and outer-class points like \((3, 0)\), \((0, 3)\), \((-3, 0)\). No straight line separates inside-the-circle from outside-the-circle in the \((x_1, x_2)\) plane.

Apply a degree-2 polynomial map to a 3-D feature space using the monomials \(z_1 = x_1^2\), \(z_2 = x_2^2\), \(z_3 = \sqrt{2}\, x_1 x_2\):

\[\phi(x_1, x_2) = \left( x_1^2,\; x_2^2,\; \sqrt{2}\, x_1 x_2 \right)\]

Now look at \(z_1 + z_2 = x_1^2 + x_2^2\), which is the squared radius. Inner points have small \(z_1 + z_2\) (e.g. \((0,0) \to 0\); \((0.5,0.5) \to 0.5\)), outer points have large \(z_1 + z_2\) (e.g. \((3,0) \to 9\); \((0,3) \to 9\)). In the 3-D feature space the two classes now separate by a flat plane like \(z_1 + z_2 = 4\) (a constant-radius threshold). That plane is a perfectly linear boundary in feature space — and mapping it back to the input space, \(z_1 + z_2 = 4\) is the circle \(x_1^2 + x_2^2 = 4\). A linear cut up top became the desired circular boundary down below.

The kernel trick means we never compute \(\phi\). Using the polynomial kernel \(K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n) = (\mathbf{x}_m^\top\mathbf{x}_n + 1)^2\) with \(a = 1\), \(b = 1\), \(p = 2\), one can expand it and confirm it equals \(\phi(\mathbf{x}_m)^\top \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)\) for the degree-2 monomial map (including the \(\sqrt{2}\) cross-term that makes the algebra match). So we get the 3-D (here 6-monomial) feature-space dot product for free, straight from the 2-D inputs. The XOR problem works the same way: XOR is not linearly separable in 2-D, but adding the product feature \(x_1 x_2\) (exactly the degree-2 cross term) makes the four points linearly separable in the lifted space. For circular data with no known polynomial degree, the RBF kernel \(\exp\!\left(-\frac{\lVert\mathbf{x}_m - \mathbf{x}_n\rVert^2}{\sigma}\right)\) does the same job implicitly in an infinite-dimensional space.

Key takeaways

  • The optimal separating hyperplane is the maximum-margin hyperplane: centered in the gap, equidistant from the nearest points of both classes, for best generalization.
  • Margin width \(= \frac{2}{\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert}\), so maximizing the margin \(=\) minimizing \(\tfrac12\lVert\mathbf{w}\rVert^2\) subject to \(y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + w_0) \ge 1\).
  • Solving the dual yields support vectors (non-zero alphas); only they define the boundary, enforced by the KKT condition.
  • Hard margin (Case 1) requires perfect separation; soft margin (Case 2) allows errors via slack \(\zeta\) and hinge loss, traded off by C (which also bounds alpha: \(0 \le \alpha \le C\)).
  • The kernel trick replaces dot products with \(K(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_n) = \phi(\mathbf{x}_m)^\top \phi(\mathbf{x}_n)\), learning a linear boundary in a high-dimensional space that is non-linear in the input space (Cover’s theorem).
  • Valid kernels satisfy Mercer’s theorem; check via a symmetric, positive semi-definite Gram matrix. Common kernels: linear, polynomial \((a\,\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x} + b)^p\), and Gaussian/RBF (infinite-dimensional, the default workhorse).
  • Kernel choice and its parameters (\(p\), \(\sigma\), …) plus C are the hyperparameters you tune.
  • Multiclass: one-vs-rest (\(C\) SVMs, winner-takes-all) or one-vs-one (\(\frac{C(C-1)}{2}\) SVMs, majority vote); OvO is often faster with many classes due to small, balanced per-SVM training sets.
  • SVM is free from the curse of dimensionality — parameters scale with the number of training samples, not the data dimension.
